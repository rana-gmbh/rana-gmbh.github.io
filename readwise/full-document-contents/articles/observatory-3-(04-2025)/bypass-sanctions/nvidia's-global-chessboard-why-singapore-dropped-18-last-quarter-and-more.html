Nvidia's fourth-quarter earnings for fiscal year 2025 revealed a complex global strategy, where regional performances reflect both market demand and geopolitical pressures. The US surge, China's defiance, Taiwan's stability, and Singapore's decline paint a picture of a company navigating a fractured global landscape.

The US stands as Nvidia's undisputed top market, with revenue rocketing to US$19.939 billion, a 63% year-over-year increase. This surge is propelled by the explosive growth of AI applications across US industries, from cloud computing to autonomous vehicles. The US's robust investment in AI infrastructure and its thriving tech ecosystem is directly translated into Nvidia's bottom line, solidifying the company's position as a key enabler of the AI revolution.

Despite stringent US export controls, Nvidia's China revenue nearly tripled, reaching US$5.534 billion. This remarkable growth underscores the immense demand for AI solutions within China. Nvidia's provision of compliant chips, such as the H20, allows it to maintain a foothold in this crucial market.

However, the surge also raises questions about the potential circumvention of export restrictions and the resilience of China's AI ecosystem in the face of geopolitical pressures. The tension between export controls and market demand creates an ongoing dynamic between US and Chinese technological ambitions.

Taiwan's revenue, while relatively stable at US$5.307 billion, underscores its indispensable role in Nvidia's global supply chain. As the home of TSMC, Nvidia's primary chip manufacturer, Taiwan ensures the production of the advanced GPUs that power the company's growth. The stability of Taiwan's revenue reflects its focus on manufacturing, rather than end-user consumption, and underscores its crucial role in the global semiconductor ecosystem. The reliance on Taiwan for manufacturing shows the delicate balance of the global tech industry.

In contrast, Singapore experienced a notable 18% revenue decline, falling to US$6.328 billion from a peak of US$7.697 billion in the third quarter of fiscal year 2025. This drop, after a strong quarter, suggests a shift in Nvidia's billing practices or a potential impact from increased scrutiny on international shipment activities due to US export controls.

While Singapore remains a significant billing hub—accounting for 18% of Nvidia's total fiscal year 2025 revenue based on customer billing location, according to its 10-K filing—the decline indicates that the company is adapting to evolving regulatory pressures and market dynamics.

DIGITIMES analyst Luke Lin noted that the decline is unlikely related to TSMC's actions, as TSMC has no oversight over Nvidia's billing or shipment locations. Lin emphasizes that chips or wafers leave TSMC's facilities and are not directly shipped to Nvidia's customers, ruling out TSMC's stricter supply chain controls as a direct cause.

Instead, Lin suggests the quarter-over-quarter drop of 18% may not be significant enough to conclusively attribute to normal fluctuations or deliberate efforts by Nvidia's customers to avoid using Singapore as a billing location. He recommends observing subsequent quarters to determine if this is a temporary shift or a longer-term trend.

Additionally, Lin highlights that shipments to Singapore were less than 2% of Nvidia's total fiscal year 2025 revenue, as customers often use Singapore to centralize invoicing while products are almost always shipped elsewhere. This underscores that the decline is not necessarily a decrease in chip usage within the region but more likely a change in how sales are being processed or attributed, possibly in response to global scrutiny over chip export regulations or shifts in customer billing strategies.
