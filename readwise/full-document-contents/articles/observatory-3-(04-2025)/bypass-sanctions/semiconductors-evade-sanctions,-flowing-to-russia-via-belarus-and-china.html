The UK has unveiled its most expansive round of sanctions yet targeting Russia, encompassing 107 new designations, among which are 10 companies hailing from China and Hong Kong. In a departure from previous measures, this latest blacklist incorporates a broader array of individuals and entities from regions beyond Russia, including so-called "third country" firms acting as intermediaries. According to Voice of America, legal experts studying Hong Kong's role in facilitating Russia's activities note that, while these steps are proactive, the implicated companies can dissolve within days, only to swiftly reemerge and resume their operations unabated.

A Nikkei investigation further reveals that Belarusian military-industrial firm Planar, already under sanctions from the US and other nations, continues to procure semiconductor-related components from Japan, Europe, and the US through Chinese intermediaries, thereby sustaining Russia's military-industrial demands. Data from BelPol, a Belarusian opposition group operating abroad, indicates that Planar—a semiconductor equipment manufacturer based in Belarus and integral to Russia's military-industrial complex—successfully imported precision semiconductor equipment from Western sources even after its designation as a sanctioned entity in December 2023. It appears much of this trade was facilitated by Chinese vendors.

Nikkei uncovers persistent violation

Nikkei's report identifies several companies allegedly involved in supplying components to Planar. Shenzhen Yuxing Machinery Equipment is accused of rerouting high-purity glass from the US-based Corning, while Shanghai Songqi International Trade is said to have funneled Nikon stereomicroscopes from Japan and Piezomechanik ultrasonic motors from Germany.

Additionally, Excell Photoelectric (Nantong) is implicated in transferring laser equipment for semiconductor production from South Korea's Microimage. Although this equipment is typically used in civilian applications, such as medical devices and industrial robotics, and may not be directly subject to economic or technological sanctions, it has become a critical substitute for Planar, which is prohibited from importing advanced semiconductor manufacturing tools. Experts suggest that this loophole results from insufficient oversight by the exporting entities.

Corporate responses

Responses from the accused entities vary. Planar acknowledges transactions with companies in the US, Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and beyond, serving clients not only in Russia but also in other Western nations—though it declines to elaborate further. Corning, Nikon, and Piezomechanik asserted they had not supplied the cited items to Planar or the implicated Chinese firms and are investigating potential rerouting pathways. South Korea's Ministry of Trade, Industry, and Energy stated it could not confirm the allegations. Meanwhile, Microimage, Shenzhen Yuxing Machinery Equipment, Shanghai Songqi International Trade, and Excell Photoelectric (Nantong) had yet to respond to the reports.

Russia's Achilles' heel

Semiconductors are a critical vulnerability for Russia in producing advanced weaponry. The US, Japan, and Europe have restricted the export of advanced chips and equipment to Russia to limit its military capabilities. These sanctions face challenges, particularly with enforcing restrictions through China, whose vast economic connections make control difficult.
