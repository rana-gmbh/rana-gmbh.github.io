Founded in 2012, Russian CPU developer Baikal Electronics has shipped 85,000 processors as of the end of 2024, defying international sanctions and growing technological isolation sparked by the Russia–Ukraine war. The majority of shipments were low-power Baikal-T chips, designed for embedded systems and telecom infrastructure, underscoring the company's emphasis on domestic, utilitarian computing needs.

With export restrictions tightening across Taiwan, the US, and Europe, Baikal is ramping up efforts to localize chip production. The loss of access to TSMC's fabrication services has pushed the firm to pivot toward in-house development and broaden its product scope to support PCs, smartphones, and data center applications.

From obscurity to national priority

As reported by Tom's Hardware and Sina, Baikal had shipped only 17,000 processors in Russia by 2019, operating with one office and a staff of 81. By 2024, the company had expanded to four offices and 200 employees, despite its parent firm, T-Platforms, declaring bankruptcy in mid-2023 and offloading its intellectual property.

Baikal's 85,000-unit shipment includes three main processor families:

● Baikal-T1 – a 28nm, dual-core, 32-bit MIPS P5600 chip used primarily in embedded and telecom systems.

● Baikal-M – an 8-core Arm Cortex-A57 processor built on a 28nm node, paired with an eight-core Arm Mali-T628 GPU, positioned for commercial PC use.

● Baikal-S – a server-grade processor featuring 48 Arm Cortex-A75 cores and six 72-bit DDR4 memory interfaces.

Among the three, the Baikal-T1 has seen the highest shipment volume, thanks to its lower design complexity and alignment with the constrained capabilities of local fabrication partners.

Until 2022, Baikal relied on TSMC to manufacture all of its chips for export to Russia. That supply line was severed when Taiwan, the US, and Europe imposed sweeping semiconductor sanctions following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, effectively banning exports of advanced chips to both Russia and Belarus.

Blockades, confiscations, and pivot to Chinese manufacturing

Sanctions have pushed Baikal into crisis mode. According to CEO Andrey Evdokimov, as cited by Softonic, 150,000 Baikal-M processors are currently stranded in Taiwan, while a separate 15,000-unit order for Baikal-S chips has been canceled entirely.

The company reportedly attempted to circumvent restrictions by moving completed processors into Russia, though these efforts ultimately failed.

At present, Baikal and other Russian firms can only access outdated 32-bit chips with clock speeds capped at 25MHz and peak performance of just 5 GFLOPS—nowhere near the requirements of modern consumer or enterprise-grade computing.

In response, Baikal is stepping up local R&D. According to Softonic, it has begun production of a new microcontroller and is developing two next-generation chips:

● Baikal-L – designed for mobile devices

● Baikal-S2 – a processor aimed at data center workloads

Both chips are reportedly being co-developed with SMIC, China's largest contract chipmaker. While specs remain under wraps, the partnership signals a strategic pivot in Baikal's supply chain and highlights Russia's increasing dependence on Chinese semiconductor infrastructure amid Western decoupling.

Baikal's renewed drive to innovate, expand its chip portfolio, and rebuild domestic capabilities reflects a broader push for technological sovereignty in Russia. Despite these efforts, with advanced manufacturing nodes remaining out of reach and geopolitical pressure intensifying, the company faces an uncertain future, balanced between immediate survival and long-term strategic necessity.
