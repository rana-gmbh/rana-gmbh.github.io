Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang returned to China this week in a markedly more high-profile visit than before. According to the Financial Times, he is expected to meet with DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfeng to explore the joint development of next-generation AI chips tailored for China—designed to align with export controls imposed by both Washington and Beijing.

Huang landed in Beijing on April 17 at the invitation of the China Council for the Promotion of International Trade (CCPIT) and immediately drew widespread media attention. That evening, he met with Vice Premier He Lifeng at the Great Hall of the People—underscoring the diplomatic significance of his visit.

"China is a key market," says Huang

Huang received similar treatment to Apple CEO Tim Cook, who met with He Lifeng in March during the 2025 China Development Forum. As China's top economic envoy and liaison for foreign businesses, He plays a central role in managing Beijing's outreach to global tech firms.

Having privately met with US President Donald Trump in February and later joined him for a dinner at Mar-a-Lago on April 4, Huang now appears equally comfortable navigating the upper echelons of China's political establishment.

His meeting at the Great Hall signals a strategic recalibration, akin to Cook's approach, aimed at maintaining active engagement with both Washington and Beijing.

According to Xinhua News Agency, Huang expressed confidence in China's economic prospects and reaffirmed Nvidia's long-term commitment to the market, saying the company hopes to contribute positively to US-China trade relations.

He Lifeng welcomed Nvidia's deeper involvement in China's digital and AI transformation, describing the country as fertile ground for foreign investment. He urged US tech companies, including Nvidia, to leverage their capabilities in shaping the next wave of industrial innovation.

Behind closed doors with DeepSeek as US tightens screws

Huang reportedly held a private meeting with DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfeng, raising eyebrows as the Chinese AI firm faces heightened scrutiny from US lawmakers. While Nvidia has declined to comment, concerns are growing that DeepSeek may have used its chips to train AI models, potentially violating tightened export restrictions.

US lawmakers have grown increasingly alarmed over DeepSeek's possible ties to China's military-industrial complex. They've demanded Nvidia disclose whether its technologies have been used by such firms, particularly amid concerns about the dual-use nature of AI.

DeepSeek is believed to be a driving factor behind the US government's decision to impose an indefinite export ban on Nvidia's H20 chip—a downgraded GPU specifically developed for China.


Credit: AFP

Navigating regulatory crossfire: Nvidia pledges to stay in China

Earlier on April 17, Huang also met with the CCPIT and China Chamber of International Commerce (CCOIC) Chairman Ren Hongbin to discuss the H20 export ban. He acknowledged the restrictions had dealt a significant blow to Nvidia's business but reaffirmed the company's commitment to producing "compliant" products for the Chinese market, rejecting speculation about a potential withdrawal.

Huang did not elaborate on what qualifies as a "compliant" product or when new offerings might become available in China.

He noted that Nvidia has operated in China for over three decades, calling it not just a major consumer market but a critical innovation hub. "AI is transforming industries globally, and China now represents nearly half of global AI research output," he said.

Huang encouraged young people to pursue math and science, highlighting AI's potential to close the digital divide and broaden access to coding. He added that nearly all Nvidia engineers now rely on AI tools for software development.

Washington turns up the heat, but Trump backs Huang

As Huang's visit dominated headlines in China, political pressure mounted in Washington. On April 16, the US House Select Committee on China sent a letter to Nvidia requesting details on whether DeepSeek had used restricted chips to train AI models, flagging national security concerns.

The committee's report claims DeepSeek transmits data through infrastructure tied to China Mobile—a telecom provider the Pentagon has linked to China's military.

Committee Chair John Moolenaar called DeepSeek a "weapon in the Chinese Communist Party's arsenal, designed to spy on Americans, steal our technology, and subvert US law," accusing it of leveraging Nvidia chips and US AI models to subvert American interests. Democrat Raja Krishnamoorthi co-signed the letter, demanding greater transparency over Nvidia's sales in China and Southeast Asia.

Despite growing scrutiny, Trump publicly praised Huang during an April 17 press conference with Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni. "Jensen's an amazing guy—he's become a friend of mine. He's proud of our country. I'm not worried about him at all," Trump said.

Trump's remarks suggest tacit support for Nvidia's engagement with China and underscore the delicate balancing act US tech leaders must perform as they navigate escalating geopolitical tensions and economic interests.
