Against a backdrop of escalating geopolitical strain and unresolved trade friction, the US and China have concluded a new round of high-level negotiations, releasing a joint statement to adjust and suspend recently imposed tariffs. Though narrow in scope, the deal signals a partial de-escalation and offers markets overdue relief after months of volatility since April 2025.

Under the updated terms, the US will amend Executive Order No. 14257—signed on April 2 and known as the Liberation Day tariffs—by suspending the 24% ad valorem tariff on imports from China, including Hong Kong and Macau, for 90 days, retaining only a 10% base rate during the period.

Tariffs introduced under Executive Orders No. 14259 and No. 14266 will also be rescinded in full.

China, in response, will suspend the 24% duty outlined in the Customs Tariff Commission's Announcement No. 4 of 2025, keeping only a 10% base rate on US imports. Additional tariffs from Announcements Nos. 5 and 6 will be scrapped, and Beijing has pledged to pause non-tariff retaliatory measures imposed since April 2.

While smaller in scope than the 2019 Phase One agreement, the deal marks the most tangible thaw in US-China trade ties in years. But the underlying motives remain tactical, not conciliatory.

Policy pressure at home, strategic calculus abroad

In Washington, the rollback reflects a balancing act between inflation control and strategic positioning. Though pitched as a curb on China's industrial ascent, tariffs have disproportionately burdened US firms and consumers, particularly in electronics, appliances, and component-heavy manufacturing reliant on Chinese inputs.

Easing duties may help deflate inflation and voter dissatisfaction, while also answering calls from US tech and manufacturing sectors to stabilize supply chains. These industries, especially in AI, HPC, EVs, and clean energy, remain tightly integrated with Chinese production networks.

Beijing's concessions are equally pragmatic. Facing weak domestic demand and sluggish exports, China is pushing to revive trade and stabilize its economy. At the same time, it is accelerating the global reach of its semiconductor, EV, and green energy sectors—areas now squarely in Washington's crosshairs.

The temporary détente signals Beijing's readiness to re-engage while securing time to advance its tech self-reliance and harden supply chain resilience.

Calculated pause, not ceasefire

This agreement was carefully engineered to preserve leverage. By maintaining a 10% base tariff and limiting the pause to 90 days, both sides retain flexibility and bargaining chips. It's a tourniquet—not a cure.

Crucially, the joint statement sidesteps thornier issues—tech export curbs, investment screening, and digital oversight, where tensions persist. Any real de-escalation hinges on how these topics are tackled in the next round.

Short-term gains are tangible. For Chinese firms like BYD and Luxshare Precision, tariff relief could ease margin pressure and bring order visibility back into focus.

US companies, including Tesla, Apple, Intel, Qualcomm, and Nvidia—with vast supply chains rooted in China, also benefit, as Beijing's softened tone may defuse regulatory friction.

Still, risks abound. Neither side wants to be seen as conceding first, and both fear domestic blowback if this détente is spun as weakness.

Markets get breather, but deeper realignment looms

To avoid turning this truce into another diplomatic false start, both sides are said to be exploring more durable communication channels. Even so, the 90-day pause has already given markets and supply chains a modest confidence boost.

Whether this thaw evolves into a lasting framework depends on political resolve and policy alignment in both capitals. One thing is certain: the next phase of US-China trade diplomacy will reshape—not just tariffs—but the future geography of global supply chains.
