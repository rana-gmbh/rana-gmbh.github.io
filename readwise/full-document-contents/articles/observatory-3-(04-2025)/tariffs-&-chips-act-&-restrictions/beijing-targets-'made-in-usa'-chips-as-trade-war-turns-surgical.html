The US-China trade war is stuck in a deadlock, with both governments refusing to back down. Beijing's early defiance—vowing to "fight to the end"—gave way to silence after Washington announced tariff hikes reaching 145%, signaling a tactical recalibration by Chinese authorities.

Beijing's muted response appears designed to sidestep escalation traps and reposition itself strategically. Behind the scenes, it is channeling efforts into cementing alliances and tightening customs enforcement. Since February, authorities have held nationwide customs meetings to map out 2025 plans, indicating long-standing preparations for renewed tariff warfare.

China sharpens its aim at 'Made in USA' chips

In a notable policy shift, the China Semiconductor Industry Association (CSIA) announced that the origin of imported semiconductors will now be determined by the wafer fabrication site, not the packaging location, effectively redrawing how tariffs may apply.

CSIA's directive requires importers to declare the wafer production site—that is, the location of the foundry—as the product's origin. Though the US wasn't explicitly named, analysts view the rule as a direct shot at American manufacturers, raising the stakes for Washington's "Made in USA" campaign.

The US has championed reshoring since Trump's first term, but Beijing's move could blunt that effort. The new rule hits US-based integrated device manufacturers (IDMs) like Intel, Texas Instruments, and Analog Devices—firms with heavy domestic wafer production—who may now face tariffs as high as 125% when exporting to China. It's a pinpointed counter to Trump's manufacturing revival narrative.

The ripple effects may extend to semiconductor equipment suppliers as well. As import costs climb, companies might be compelled to shift production capacity to Southeast Asia—or even to China itself—to stay aligned with local demand and navigate the shifting tariff landscape.

Introduced on April 11, CSIA's rule throws a wrench into US tariff policy planning. Washington may be forced to respond with more detailed guidelines on semiconductor and electronics origin tracing, possibly including stricter thresholds for verifying US-origin content.

The US trade strategy is also evolving, shifting from sweeping tariffs toward more selective, precision-based actions aimed at minimizing blowback on domestic industries.

Tariff policy enters a new phase of precision

Beneath the rhetorical fireworks, both sides are recalibrating. The era of blanket tariffs is fading, replaced by more tactical actions aimed at particular sectors, supply chains, and product categories.

China's shift from public bravado to strategic maneuvering is telling. It's doubling down on outreach to Southeast Asia—Vietnam, Cambodia, Malaysia—and the EU to dilute its dependency on US trade and build a broader economic safety net.

Amid continued pressure from Washington, Beijing seems to grasp that diversification is its best hedge—deepening economic ties beyond the US to lessen exposure to any one market.

While some Chinese electronics have been temporarily exempted—many of them high-value—the overall tariff regime remains unchanged. Most analysts see the move as a tactical concession, not a meaningful loosening of trade policy.

Beijing's response has been measured, calling the exemption "a small step" toward correction. But Chinese officials remain watchful, waiting to see whether Washington can disentangle confrontation from diplomacy and restart substantive negotiations.

Trump, meanwhile, keeps fanning the flames—accusing China of treating the US "worse than any other country" and pledging zero tolerance. Will Beijing respond in kind? And what countermeasures might it still be holding back? One thing's clear: the tariff chess match between Trump and Xi is far from reaching checkmate.
