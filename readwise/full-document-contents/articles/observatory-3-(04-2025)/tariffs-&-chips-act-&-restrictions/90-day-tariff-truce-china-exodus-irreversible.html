The US-China negotiations have reached an agreement to temporarily reduce tariffs on each other for 90 days, buying more time for further talks. According to statements from both countries, the US will lower tariffs on China to 30%, while China will reduce tariffs on the US to 10%. However, even with this concession, electronics assembly supply chain players state that production capacity already relocated will not return—it is a one-way move.

The notebook (NB) supply chain expressed optimism about the US-China deal but noted that brand manufacturers' demands for diversified production across supply chains remain unchanged. Even before the announcement of reciprocal tariffs, brands had been requesting their supply chains to relocate affected production lines out of China. Given the current agreement, brands are unlikely to adjust their strategies in the short term.

The server supply chain has taken a more direct approach by establishing production sites within the US itself, and this plan will continue despite the US-China agreement. Aside from Quanta's facilities in California and Tennessee, most original design manufacturers (ODMs) are concentrated in Texas near Mexico, including Foxconn, Wistron, Wiwynn, and Inventec. Compal chairman Ray Chen also announced plans to soon reveal investment and factory establishment projects in Mexico and the US.

Supply chain analysts state that the US and China have only reached a preliminary agreement to temporarily reduce tariffs for 90 days, which does not guarantee the final outcome of negotiations. Moreover, even at a 30% tariff rate, it remains higher than rates in other regions, posing unbearable pressure on consumer electronics with limited profit margins. Furthermore, the outcome after the 90 days remains unpredictable.

Since the tariff issues initiated by the Trump administration involve dynamic negotiations affecting tax rates across different countries and regions, brand manufacturers and supply chains must proceed cautiously. The consensus among most supply chains is that multi-location production is now inevitable. Future adjustments will flexibly manage capacities across various locations, and once established outside China, these bases will not disappear.

Additionally, to expand profits, both brands and supply chains will seek ways to increase individual site capacities to reduce costs. For example, Hewlett-Packard (HP) has chosen Thailand; Dell and Apple have selected Vietnam as primary production bases for products exported to the US. Taiwanese brands with smaller shares in the Americas market tend to produce in either Vietnam or Thailand.

Acer's strategy amid uncertainty

Acer(2353.TW) chairman and CEO Jason Chen pointed out that the uncertainty brought by US President Donald Trump is too great to predict. Acer plans to quickly boost Southeast Asian production capacity through its supply chain to meet demand in the US market. He also stressed efforts to use Acer's subsidiary as a buffer to mitigate uncertainties.

Chen shared that during a recent interview in the United Kingdom, he discussed the situation caused by the Trump administration and possible responses. However, none of his predictions materialized because the situation changed so rapidly and unpredictably, one of the negotiation tactics employed by Trump.

Chen explained that Trump's negotiation strategy involves four steps: first, bluffing on price; second, intimidating opponents to create bargaining space; third, breaking the rules of the game; and finally, closing a good deal. The initial two steps have already been observed, and the process is entering the latter stages. This tactic is expected to be common over the next four years.

He stressed that although tariffs seem to affect only the US directly, they actually cause global impacts. For Acer, approximately 20% of revenue comes from the Americas, while over 50% derives from the Asia-Pacific region. One key factor is that many subsidiaries generate most of their revenue from Asia-Pacific markets, forming a multi-engine business model that acts as a protective buffer.

Acer reported April 2025 revenues of NT$18.595 billion (US$612.8 million), up 4.2% year-over-year; cumulative revenue for the first four months reached NT$80.016 billion, increasing 4.4% year-over-year. Its strategy to build multiple business engines continues to bear fruit, with non-computer and display-related businesses accounting for 32.8% of April revenue and 31.9% of cumulative revenue for the first four months.
