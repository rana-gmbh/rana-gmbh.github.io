Since taking office, President Trump has positioned tariffs as both a strategic tool for national security and a means to bolster the US economy. The ongoing tariffs on Asian nations are just the beginning, with expectations of further actions against these countries in the future. Meanwhile, the Asia-Pacific stock markets have hit a significant low, and national crises are unfolding across the region. How these countries respond will be pivotal not only for their economic stability but also for the global supply chain and the broader world economy.

Trump's tariff policy on Asian countries

Trump's tariff policy has had a substantial impact on several Asian nations previously, with Vietnam at 46%, Thailand at 36%, and Japan and Malaysia at 24%. Currently, Trump announced a 'pause' for those countries with the baseline 10% tariffs only. Additionally, the US has engaged in a tit-for-tat tariff exchange with China, further escalating the trade tensions.

China is once again facing significant tariffs from the US. Trump has announced a 90-day pause on 'reciprocal' tariffs, with China notably excluded—a move that has further intensified the trade war. The tariffs on China have now been upped to 125%.

Asian countries response

Countries in the Asia Pacific are actively mitigating the current tariff by reducing the trade imbalance with the US.

Vietnam

According to the Diplomat, Prime Minister Pham Minh Chinh called an emergency cabinet meeting, expressing his belief that the tariff did not reflect the strong bilateral relations between the two nations. Despite the setback, he reaffirmed that Vietnam's ambitious 8% GDP growth target would remain unchanged.

The Ministry of Foreign Affairs echoed this sentiment, stating that the tariff decision was inconsistent with the mutually beneficial economic and trade cooperation between the two countries and failed to reflect the spirit of the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership established in 2023. In response, Vietnam's Ministry of Trade urged the Trump administration to reconsider the tariff and engage in further discussions regarding Vietnam's trade surplus with the US. Additionally, Deputy Prime Minister Ho Duc Phoc was dispatched to the US to attempt to broker a deal.

Prime Minister Chinh also framed the tariff as an opportunity to restructure Vietnam's economy, emphasizing plans to diversify export markets, expand product markets, and boost localization. This decision follows Vietnam's proactive efforts to engage with the US government to address the trade imbalance, which included unilaterally lowering tariffs on several US imports, granting permissions to US companies like Elon Musk's Starlink, and even approving a Trump-affiliated resort project.

Despite these efforts to foster cooperation, Vietnam was hit with a much higher tariff than expected, especially when compared to other countries Trump often criticizes, further fueling frustration in Hanoi.

Thailand

Prime Minister Paetongtarn Shinawatra issued a statement expressing her government's readiness to engage with the US to address the trade imbalance, aiming to adjust the balance fairly for both countries and minimize the impact on affected sectors. Thailand announced plans to increase imports from the US, reduce certain high taxes on American goods, and address non-tariff barriers, as the government aims to negotiate more favorable terms under the new US tariffs.

Additionally, Thailand is exploring ways to boost imports of US agricultural products to help narrow its US$45.6 billion trade deficit with the US, including plans to purchase 1 million tonnes of American ethane in the second quarter of this year.

Moreover, Paetongtarn encouraged Thai exporters to explore new markets to reduce their dependency on the US market, while assuring that the government had prepared mitigation measures to support those sectors heavily reliant on US exports.

Japan

Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba spoke with US President Donald Trump on Monday night from Tokyo in a 25-minute phone call to discuss tariffs that have unsettled the markets and may cause significant economic damage globally.

The government has so far maintained a measured approach in response to the US tariffs. Ishiba signaled earlier in the day that Tokyo would not seek confrontation with Washington over the tariffs.

Ishiba's diplomatic stance is coming under fire domestically, with opposition leaders and even some members of his own Liberal Democratic Party accusing him of being too passive and urging a stronger response to Trump's tariffs.

Lawmakers have agreed to hold a special session of the budget committee later this month to discuss the tariff measures, with the prime minister set to attend.

Futher, as for a lawmaker call for the selling Japan's US Treasury, Japanese Finance Minister Katsunobu Kato dismissed the idea of using Japan's US Treasury holdings as leverage in response to Trump's decision to impose tariffs on imports from Japan."We manage our US Treasury holdings from the standpoint of preparing for in case we need to conduct exchange-rate intervention in the future," not from the standpoint of bilateral diplomacy, Kato told parliament on April 9.

Malaysia

Malaysia is set to send a delegation to Washington to discuss new US tariffs, criticizing them as contradictory to WTO principles. In response, manufacturers are readying for possible shifts in production and strategy. The Malaysian delegation plans to visit the US by late April to discuss the new tariffs' potential impact.

Meanwhile, the Ministry of Investment, Trade and Industry (MITI) is assessing the economic effects. MITI Minister Zafrul Aziz pointed out Malaysia's average tariff on US imports is 5.6%, differing significantly from the US's cited 47%, indicating discrepancies in tariff calculations.

Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim has called for enhanced cooperation among ASEAN nations, after discussions with regional leaders indicated a shared belief in coordinating positions during negotiations with the US, despite each country sending its own delegation. Malaysia aims to leverage its 2025 ASEAN chairmanship to advocate for more intra-regional tariff liberalization and stronger ties with external partners.

China

"When challenged, we will never back down," said China's foreign ministry spokesperson, Lin Jian. The commerce ministry said: "China will fight to the end if the US side is bent on going down the wrong path." Further countermeasures have been promised by Beijing.

The tit-for-tat measures could spark fears of a race to the bottom, with ordinary people suffering as prices rise and fears of a global recession grow.

Effective from 12:01 PM on April 10, 2025 (Beijing Time), China will adjust the additional tariff rate on US-origin imports. All other provisions will continue to be implemented by Announcement No. 4, 2025.

China urges the United States to immediately correct its wrongful actions, lift all unilateral tariff measures against China, and resolve differences through equal and constructive dialogue based on mutual respect. However, there seems to be no sign of peaceful talks as Trump pushed up to 125% tariffs on China.

Global attention now turns to potential countermeasures from Beijing. Furthermore, Beijing has previously allowed online nationalists to fuel grassroots movements for boycotts, a strategy that significantly damages the US market. The resulting turmoil shows no signs of subsiding anytime soon.

Further action from the governments

The shifting tariff policies have significantly affected the stock markets across the region, prompting countries to devise strategies to strengthen their domestic economies. In response to the turmoil, many Asian nations are likely to pursue regional integration and seek alternative trading partners, which could be key to stabilizing the regional supply chain and markets. Additionally, ongoing diplomatic negotiations with the US will be a crucial point of focus for global authorities.
