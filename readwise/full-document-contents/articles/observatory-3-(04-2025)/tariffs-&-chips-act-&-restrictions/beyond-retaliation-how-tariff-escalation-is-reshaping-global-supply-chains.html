With President Trump threatening tariffs on Chinese goods as high as 245%, China signals it will no longer play along — raising fears of lasting damage to global trade.

On April 16, US President Donald Trump reaffirmed his stance on trade with China, proposing tariffs that could reach an eye-watering 245% on Chinese imports. The move, he suggested, is aimed at pressuring Beijing into making concessions on trade. But in both Washington and Beijing, the effectiveness of tariffs as a negotiating tool is now being questioned.

Since early April, US announcements of successive tariff increases have drawn a noticeably muted response from markets and foreign governments. China's Ministry of Commerce has now openly stated that it will not follow the US's steps on further tariff escalations.

On April 12, in direct response to the latest US tariff hike, China raised import duties on American-origin goods from 84% to 125%. Officials made it clear: at these levels, US exports to China have little to no market viability. If Washington continues to impose further tariffs, Beijing said it will simply ignore them.

A tariff war without end

Yongnian Zheng, the former Director of the Advanced Institute of Global and Contemporary China StudiesThe Chinese University of Hong Kong (Shenzhen), noted that once tariffs exceed 60%, further increases become largely symbolic. At such high rates, normal trade becomes impossible, he pointed out.

Indeed, beyond retaliatory tariffs, the U.S. has, in recent years, slapped China with a variety of trade barriers — including Section 301 duties and penalties under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA).

For example, Chinese-made goods such as televisions and Bluetooth headphones are now subject to layered tariffs under these frameworks: 7.5% under Section 301, 20% under IEEPA, and an additional 125% in retaliatory tariffs, totaling 152%.

Shifting supply chains and strategic relocation

To mitigate these impacts, many Chinese manufacturers have begun shifting production to countries like Vietnam and India, where products face a much lighter tariff burden, typically around 10%. Others are rerouting operations to Mexico, where goods that qualify under the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) may enjoy duty-free access to the American market.

Still, these workarounds underscore deeper concerns in China: a weakening yuan, growing pressure on the export sector, and an accelerating exodus of manufacturing. Moreover, recent US efforts to tighten "country-of-origin" rules — aimed at preventing Chinese goods from being rerouted through third countries — are further straining China's industrial base.

Tariffs: a blunt tool with broader risks

While aggressive tariffs may deliver immediate gains in revenue, economists warn they also carry long-term costs: weakening US bond credibility, slowing economic growth, fueling inflation, and undermining trust with key trade partners. Many experts now argue that the risks and ripple effects could far outweigh the short-term fiscal benefits.

So what happens when tariffs lose their leverage, but the trade war continues to heat up? The consequences may be far-reaching — not just for China and the US, but for the global economic system itself.

Senior Minister of Singapore, Hsien Loong Lee, recently summed up the dilemma succinctly: the US, he said, is intent on preserving global supremacy and preventing China from closing the gap. China, for its part, sees its development as a rightful goal — one that should not be curtailed by punitive trade measures.

As this structural rivalry deepens, the prospect of stabilizing US-China relations grows more remote, while the risks to the global free trade system mount.

A call for multilateralism

Lee urged nations, particularly ASEAN and the European Union, to stand firm in defense of an open, rules-based multilateral trading system. He also called on governments and business leaders to prepare for a new era — one that may be defined by fragmentation, higher risk, and greater strategic uncertainty.

Scholars and industry experts echo that sentiment. They recommend that companies reevaluate their supply chains, pursue diversification strategies, reduce dependence on any single market, and develop tax and origin compliance protocols. In an era of trade weaponization, supply chain agility and cost control are no longer competitive advantages — they are necessities for survival.
