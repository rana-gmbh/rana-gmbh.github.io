US Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick indicated Sunday that recent tariff exemptions for electronics and semiconductor products are merely temporary, with comprehensive semiconductor tariffs likely arriving "within a month or two." The move aims to repatriate manufacturing of critical components to US soil.

"We need semiconductors, we need chips, we need panels—we need to manufacture these things in America," Lutnick said. "We cannot rely on Southeast Asia to make all our products."

President Trump reinforced this position early Monday, stating on social media that his administration will "comprehensively review semiconductors and the entire electronics supply chain" as part of national security tariff investigations.

According to DIGITIMES Deputy General Manager Eric Huang, the administration's focus has expanded beyond semiconductors to encompass the entire electronics supply chain, including downstream products and key components. The strategy targets not only Chinese production but also manufacturing bases throughout Southeast Asia and potentially Taiwan, Japan, and South Korea.

Industry intelligence

Huang expects the coming policy shift will accelerate three distinct supply chain trends: migration out of China, increased production in North America, and expansion of China's domestic manufacturing capacity for its own market.

Companies with existing non-Chinese production facilities in countries like Taiwan, Vietnam, and Malaysia may gain first-mover advantage, though establishing entirely new production sites remains time-intensive. Meanwhile, manufacturers targeting US markets will likely accelerate investment in US and Mexican facilities to comply with USMCA regulations.

China's response

For China, the dual pressures of US tariffs and domestic self-sufficiency goals will likely accelerate its "China for China" manufacturing strategy, particularly in semiconductor development where critical technology gaps remain.

Market fragmentation ahead

The resulting global market appears headed toward fragmentation into three distinct spheres: a US-centric supply chain with minimal Chinese components, a self-contained Chinese ecosystem with reduced American technology, and third markets served by cost-competitive producers leveraging existing supply networks.
