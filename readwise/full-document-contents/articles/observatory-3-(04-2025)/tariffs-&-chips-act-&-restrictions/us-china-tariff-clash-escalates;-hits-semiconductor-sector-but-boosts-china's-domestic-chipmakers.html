Since President Donald Trump declared April 2 as "Liberation Day" and unveiled sweeping reciprocal tariffs, the US-China trade war has sharply intensified. Trump's changing tariff policy on China has now been up to 145%. China's State Council Tariff Commission responded in kind: beginning April 12, all US-origin goods entering China would face the same 125% tariff.

These sweeping tariffs cover a vast range of products, including semiconductors. Analysts say the tariffs are already disrupting trade flows and pricing structures, particularly for American analog chipmakers, which are heavily reliant on China as a key market.

According to Chinese business outlet National Business Daily, the new tariffs will drive up prices for analog chips from US suppliers, potentially accelerating the adoption of domestically produced alternatives. Local industry insiders noted that Chinese analog chipmakers, as well as foundries with robust analog manufacturing capabilities such as Huahong Semiconductor, stand to benefit the most.

Design and manufacturing are closely linked in the analog sector, remarked a Chinese semiconductor executive in a report from China Starmarket. Chinese industry insiders noted that US companies like Texas Instruments (TI) heavily depend on domestic wafer fabs for production, which makes relocating capacity overseas challenging in the short term.

Chinese media outlets Yicai further reported that while digital chips have seen limited impact so far, many are still produced in Taiwan and other parts of Asia — analog chips designed and fabricated in the US are bearing the brunt of the tariffs. This creates an opening for domestic players. These products are critical in sectors such as industrial electronics, automotive systems, and power management.

In response, the China Semiconductor Industry Association issued guidance on April 11 that customs declarations for semiconductors — whether packaged or unpackaged — should use the location of wafer fabrication as the country of origin. This clarification could further tighten restrictions on US-produced chips.

Tariffs to hit US fabs hardest

In theory, if you define the place of wafer fabrication as the origin, then chips manufactured in US-based fabs will be subject to the full tariff, said one anonymous executive at a Chinese analog chip firm. That's a direct hit to companies like TI, whose fabs are mostly in the US.

Distributors echoed this view, noting that origin definitions were previously more relaxed and based largely on product labeling. Many chip packaging and testing operations are located in Southeast Asia, which previously helped buffer against tariffs. But under the new definition, chips fabricated in US wafer fabs — including those by Intel, TI, and others — could face steep new costs.

Demand would inevitably begin shifting toward local suppliers, said one chip channel executive to Yicai. Domestic firms that could offer competitive analog solutions will quickly fill the gap.

According to Shengxin Yang, senior analyst at Sigmaintell, chipmakers most exposed to the new tariffs are those with design finalized and fabrication located in the US, yet whose products are sold to mainland Chinese clients — including Intel, TI, Onsemi, and Micron.

A recent research note by Citi echoed this concern, stating that rising tariffs will erode the cost advantages of US firms like TI in China. The note estimated that Chinese analog chipmakers currently hold only 6–8% of the global market share, leaving considerable room for growth amid a shift toward localization.

Huaxi Securities also flagged analog chips as the most vulnerable segment in this round of trade conflict. Industry leaders TI and Analog Devices (ADI), both headquartered in the US with primary manufacturing there, are expected to be directly affected. Should higher costs push up end-user prices, Chinese analog alternatives — particularly those tailored for automotive applications — could gain ground and accelerate domestic substitution.
