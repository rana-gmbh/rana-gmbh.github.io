US President Donald Trump's escalating tariff policies—culminating in a 90-day tariff suspension that excluded China—have rattled global supply chains. With major brands staying silent, most suppliers are adopting a cautious stance, delaying key decisions amid growing uncertainty.

Analysts suggest Trump's universal tariff strategy targets China, aiming to block companies that rebrand Chinese-made goods through third countries. The goal is to curb what the US views as widespread circumvention of trade rules.

China's manufacturing dominance in essential components

Following the Cold War and China's WTO entry, global manufacturing pivoted sharply to Asia. China became the center of this shift, attracting investment with its vast labor force and market scale—especially in low-margin, high-risk, and high-pollution sectors.

Industry players highlight China's dominance in mid- and low-end passive components—basic but essential parts of nearly all electronics. Any push to decouple from China would face a critical supply gap in these foundational components.

China's stronghold on foundational manufacturing stems from a mix of government subsidies, fierce local competition, and ultra-thin profit margins—factors that nudged foreign rivals toward more profitable, high-tech segments.

Reverting to low-margin production or expecting small firms to scale up overnight poses serious feasibility challenges for suppliers that have long shifted to higher-value manufacturing.

Relocation challenges extend beyond economics

Processes involving hazardous or polluting steps—like coating or etching—remain concentrated in China, even among firms with offshore operations. High costs and the lack of scalable alternatives elsewhere have deterred many from relocating these processes.

Large system integrators may have the volume to justify US-based plants, but for small and micro-sized component suppliers, razor-thin margins make such relocation efforts financially risky—even with firm order commitments.

Without cost-sharing from assemblers, component makers have little reason to shift production to the US. Yet with system integrators also squeezed by low margins, aligning incentives across the full supply chain remains a complex challenge.
