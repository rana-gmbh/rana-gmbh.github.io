US President Donald Trump's impact on the supply chain has been deep and far-reaching, with his tariff policies from 2017 having a lasting effect to this day.

Trump recently delivered an address to Congress criticizing the CHIPS Act, calling for an end to the US$52 billion in semiconductor subsidies. The President also said that reciprocal tariffs would be imposed on China, South Korea, India, and Brazil starting on April 2.

Taiwan manufacturing index lowered

According the manufacturing industry summary for January 2025 released recently by the Taiwan Institute of Economic Research (TIER), while Taiwan's semiconductor industry related to AI, high-performance computing (HPC), and AI server assembly are doing well, they are being offset by the slowing momentum in traditional industries, such as petrochemicals and plastics.

Taiwan's manufacturing industry index has shifted from a neutral green light to a sluggish yellow-blue light in January. Industries that downgraded to a blue light—indicating contraction—include precision machinery, metal products, mineral products, chemical products, and plastic products.

In the electronics components industry, both 12-inch wafer foundries and IC packaging and testing maintained positive growth. The production of servers and semiconductor testing equipment in the computer industry also continues to increase.

Southeast Asian exports surge

Taiwan's exports to major markets in January 2025 showed a stark contrast between strong and weak performers.

According to customs statistics from Taiwan's Ministry of Finance (MOF), Taiwan's exports to China and Hong Kong have fallen; however, exports to countries in the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) have increased 21.5% due to strong demand for electronics and ICT products. This is largely due to the trade war Trump started with China during his first term, which caused a large number of foreign and Taiwanese downstream assembly factories to relocate to Southeast Asian countries such as Vietnam, Thailand, and Malaysia, thereby increasing the demand for Taiwan's intermediate goods, such as electric components.

An analysis by officials from Taiwan's Ministry of Economic Affairs (MOEA) found that strong demand for AI, HPC, and cloud data processing is driving the production momentum in Taiwan's IT and electronics industry. Among them, the IC industry production index in January 2025 reached 22.9% growth compared to the same period last year, a record high for the period.

China trade continues to decline

The upcoming tariff increase on Chinese goods to 20% starting April 2 will likely accelerate factory relocations from China. The decoupling of the US-China supply chain, strategic technological competition, technology controls, and government intervention in US business investments in China will only continue to intensify.

Taiwan's total trade with China, including Hong Kong, increased 3.3% compared to the previous year to US$231.2 billion in 2024, accounting for 26.6% of Taiwan's total foreign trade. Taiwan's exports to China fell 1.1% compared to the previous year to US$150.6 billion, accounting for 31.7% of Taiwan's exports, down from 35.2% in 2023, the lowest level in 22 years. Before Trump took office, Taiwan's exports to China accounted for nearly 40%, highlighting the long-term impact of the supply chain restructuring caused by Trump's first term on Taiwanese manufacturers' trade, investments, and production.
