Facing economic headwinds and escalating trade tensions, China is refocusing on its tech sector, with President Xi Jinping recently convening a rare symposium with high-tech entrepreneurs. This move signals Beijing's renewed commitment to technological innovation and industrial upgrades as key drivers of economic growth.

Over the past four years, stringent regulatory measures have disrupted China's tech industry, leading to widespread uncertainty. However, as the country's real estate bubble bursts, the government now sees tech innovation as the new engine for economic recovery.

According to Bloomberg Economics, the high-tech industry contributed 15% of China's GDP in 2024. By 2026, it is expected to surpass real estate as the dominant pillar of the Chinese economy.

Tech self-sufficiency becomes a national priority

With the US ramping up tariff pressures and imposing stricter technology sanctions, China is increasingly reliant on its domestic tech firms to drive innovation and develop independent capabilities. Strengthening technological self-sufficiency is seen as critical to enhancing China's leverage in trade negotiations.

This strategic focus was reflected in the high-profile attendees at the symposium, including Huawei founder Ren Zhengfei, BYD founder Wang Chuanfu, Alibaba founder Jack Ma, Tencent founder Pony Ma, and Xiaomi founder Lei Jun. Also present were Liang Wenfeng, founder of the rising AI startup DeepSeek, and Wang Xingxing, founder of robotics developer Unitree Robotics.

The presence of leading figures from sectors like semiconductors, new energy vehicles, AI, and robotics underscores Beijing's commitment to countering US technology restrictions. Executives from these industries received assurances of government support, which could improve market financing opportunities and policy backing for private enterprises.

Notably, Jack Ma's participation and handshake with Xi Jinping were widely interpreted as a signal that China's intense regulatory grip on the tech sector may be easing. Since the abrupt suspension of Ant Group's IPO in 2020, Ma has largely retreated from the public sphere.

Xi Jinping seeks corporate consensus on tech strategy

Reports from Reuters, South China Morning Post, Nikkei Asia, and Bloomberg highlighted Xi Jinping's efforts to unify corporate leaders around the government's vision of technological self-sufficiency and supply chain security.

In the reports, Neil Thomas, a researcher at the Asia Society Policy Institute's Center for China Analysis, said that the lineup of attendees at the symposium reflects Beijing's attempt to rally private enterprises in support of its tech repositioning. Alfredo Montufar-Helu, head of The Conference Board's China Center, noted that while China continues to favor a state-led economic model, it is increasingly recognizing the indispensable role of private firms in advancing technological independence.

Meanwhile, Robin Xing, a Chinese economist at Morgan Stanley, pointed out that Beijing is now repositioning private enterprises as a key pillar of national competitiveness. He suggested that Jack Ma's renewed visibility could signal a turning point in the government's regulatory stance toward the tech industry.

Regulatory uncertainty still weighs on innovation

Despite Beijing's apparent policy shift, uncertainties remain. Some sources suggest that regulators advised Xiaohongshu (China's Instagram-like platform, Little Red Book) to introduce state-backed investment to facilitate its IPO approval. George Magnus, a researcher at the University of Oxford's China Centre, interpreted this as a sign that private enterprises must still closely align with government directives to grow their business, meaning their autonomy remains constrained.

For years, regulatory unpredictability has constrained investment and innovation in China's private sector. While the government now acknowledges the importance of private businesses in global competition, it continues to exert control over their direction to ensure alignment with national strategic goals.

Analysts caution that while the symposium highlighted Xi Jinping's renewed support for private enterprises, concrete policy changes remain uncertain. Many believe that for China's tech sector to achieve sustainable growth, the government must loosen regulations, strengthen capital markets, and foster a competitive business environment.
