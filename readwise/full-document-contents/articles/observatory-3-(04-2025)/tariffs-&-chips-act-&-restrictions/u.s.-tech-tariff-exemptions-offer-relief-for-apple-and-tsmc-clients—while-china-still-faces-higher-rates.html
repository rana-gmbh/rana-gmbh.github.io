The United States Customs and Border Protection (CBP) released updated tariff guidelines on the evening of April 11, setting a uniform 10% duty on major electronic products—including notebooks, smartphones, and servers. This replaces the previously proposed country-specific reciprocal tariffs. However, China remains subject to a higher rate.

U.S. National Security Advisor Steven Miller stated on X that while China received partial relief from the reciprocal tariff structure, it will still face a 20% tariff under the original provisions of the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA).

According to DIGITIMES analyst Luke Lin, the revised exemption list clearly prioritizes American business interests. On the end-product side, major brands such as Apple, Dell, and HP benefit directly. Upstream, U.S. semiconductor firms like AMD, Intel, Nvidia, Qualcomm, and Broadcom are also included—most of which are key customers of Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC(2330.TW)).

DIGITIMES President Colley Hwang noted that Taiwan was initially subject to a steep 32% tariff rate, which ran counter to U.S. industrial goals. He originally expected relief to come only after Trump met with Japanese and South Korean leaders. The early policy adjustment, however, suggests that U.S. policymakers are more attuned to industrial supply chain realities than previously assumed.

DIGITIMES Vice President Eric Huang highlighted three key implications of the revised announcement:

1. Semiconductors as a Humanitarian Corridor

The inclusion of semiconductors on the exemption list reflects their new status as "humanitarian corridor products"—a strategic compromise amid ongoing trade tensions. U.S. CBP bulletin CSMS #64724565 reinforces this, listing semiconductors as essential items.

In the short term, this indicates that despite escalating tariffs, both countries acknowledge that cutting off semiconductor flows would be self-defeating. Domestic self-sufficiency is still far from reality. Long-term, the move underscores the strategic importance of semiconductors, with greater self-reliance likely becoming a core national objective for both the U.S. and China.

For China, this will mean intensifying R&D across the semiconductor ecosystem—especially in AI chips, autonomous vehicle chips, and core process technologies like 28nm and 7nm nodes. At the same time, China is expected to push harder for local manufacturing and sourcing requirements to access its domestic market.

2. Policy Gaps in the U.S. Manufacturing Strategy

While the exemption list covers downstream electronics and upstream semiconductors (e.g., discrete components, ICs, LEDs), it lacks coverage for key midstream components like display panels, printed circuit boards (PCBs), passive parts, and SMT equipment.

These omissions could undermine the broader "Made in America" agenda. Without incentives or exemptions for the entire electronics production chain, attracting global players to set up shop in the U.S. may remain an uphill battle. The 90-day grace period could offer an opportunity to correct this misalignment.

3. Short-Term Relief, Long-Term Uncertainty

The policy shift gives the tech supply chain some breathing room—especially for inventory planning around Q3 and events like Computex. Taiwanese companies can proceed with their shipment schedules, assuming current exemptions hold.

Still, uncertainty remains, particularly regarding goods shipped from China. Many global brands are accelerating their "non-China" strategies to hedge against future risks. As Eric Huang puts it: "No one knows what will happen after 90 days. And no one can predict whether Trump will change course again within that window."
