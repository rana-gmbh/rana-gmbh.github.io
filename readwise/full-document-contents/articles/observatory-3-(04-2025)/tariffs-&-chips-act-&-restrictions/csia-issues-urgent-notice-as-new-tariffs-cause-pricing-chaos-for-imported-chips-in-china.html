The escalation in the tariff war has affected the global chip supply chain, causing significant disruption in pricing within China's imported chip market. As a result, some OEMs have temporarily suspended price quotes and are instead adopting a wait-and-see approach, which is expected to impact IC distributors first.

China's Customs Tariff Commission of the State Council announced that the tariff on all imported goods originating from the US would be raised to 125% from 84% starting on April 12. The rate had previously been raised from 34% to 84% on April 10.

The China Semiconductor Industry Association (CSIA) issued an urgent notice on April 11, reminding companies to have supporting documents such as purchase orders (PO) ready when making declarations to comply with customs inspections. It also repeatedly urged chip importers to familiarize themselves with relevant policies to avoid import delays and cost increases due to customs declaration issues.

The CSIA clearly stated that when integrated circuit (IC) products are declared for import, the country of origin is determined by the chip's tape-out location. In other words, the country of origin will be determined by where the chip was first fabricated, regardless of where the chip was packaged.

Market quotes spiral out of control

The CSIA issued the urgent notice informing the industry how to determine the country of origin in response to the disruption in IC pricing caused by the high tariff imposed on April 10.

Semiconductor players pointed out that the first shock wave following the implementation of the new tariff policy has already hit, causing IC market prices to spiral out of control almost immediately. According to Chinese media outlet Jiemian News, a chip buyer in Shenzhen stated that a price could be quoted one minute and increased in the next. This phenomenon has already been seen in analog chips, memory, and automotive power semiconductor components.

It has also been reported that chip OEMs such as Micron Technology, Texas Instruments (TI), and Analog Devices (ADI) have stopped providing real-time quotes to Chinese distributors. The market has fallen into a state of uncertainty and chaos, even triggering a wave of panic buying for existing spot inventory, with prices changing every day and even increasing by the minute.

Pressure mounts for chip distributors

IC distributors are most likely to bear the brunt of the impact of the recent changes. Since OEMs are still holding off on quoting prices, with some even suspending quotes or planning significant price adjustments, IC distributors are unable to secure stable prices for downstream customers, making procurement decisions difficult and throwing market transactions into disorder. As a result, many companies have opted to suspend shipments and wait to see how things unfold.

Unlike the chip shortage in 2021, this wave of price increases is not due to actual demand growth, but short-term chaos driven by tariff policies. The industry generally believes this upward trend will be difficult to maintain in the long term, and there is still a risk that it may eventually fall due to being artificially hyped.

Since a chip's country of origin will now be under more stringent supervision and inspection by Chinese customs, and because IC manufacturing covers three major stages—design, production, and packaging and testing—which are often done in different countries, companies are required to provide comprehensive supply chain traceability data to ensure the accuracy of the origin of imported ICs.

This will require more time for customs brokers and customs officials to settle any differences regarding their interpretation of the country of origin, which will inevitably increase corporate compliance costs and risks during customs declarations.

Chinese chipmakers see demand increase

IC insiders revealed to the Chinese outlet ICrank that due to the impact of the tariff policy, several chip products are facing price increases. This includes high-end CPUs such as US-manufactured Xeon server processors from Intel, which are expected to see an 8-12% price increase, while Micron memory prices are anticipated to increase 15-20%. Analog and power management chips from TI and Broadcom, which are used in automotive and consumer electronics, have also simultaneously experienced price increases.

Additionally, Apple's radio frequency (RF) modules are also subject to tariffs since they use components from Skyworks Solutions and Qorvo that originate from the US. As a result, the price of iPhones may rise further.

On the other hand, the market volatility has made domestic Chinese chips a safe haven. For example, high-performance chips continue to sell well due to the rapid expansion of AI servers. As such, Chinese alternatives such as Cambricon Technologies, Hygon Information Technology, Will Semiconductor (WillSemi), and SmartSens Technology have seen domestic demand rise, becoming a stabilizing force for local chip prices.

Industry players pointed out that in the face of continuously escalating tariffs, the situation is still rapidly evolving. Relevant companies are strengthening their cooperation with non-US suppliers to reduce their reliance on American-made chips. At the same time, they are signing temporary price adjustment clauses with customers to cope with short-term price fluctuations and delivery uncertainties.
