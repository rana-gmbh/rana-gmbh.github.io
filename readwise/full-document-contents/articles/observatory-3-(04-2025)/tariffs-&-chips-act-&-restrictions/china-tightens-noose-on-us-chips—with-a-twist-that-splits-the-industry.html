As US-China tariff tensions intensify, Beijing is taking aim at American-made semiconductors with new origin rules that could redraw the global chip supply map.

Origin-based rules take aim at US semiconductor policy

Beijing has fired back at the Trump administration's latest tariff blitz—125% duties on Chinese-origin goods—by rewriting chip origin rules. Under the new framework, the country where a chip undergoes tape-out—the point at which the final design is sent to a foundry—will determine its origin. This redefinition raises costs for US-designed chips and targets Washington's broader reshoring strategy for semiconductor production.

The fallout is immediate: US IDMs such as Intel, Texas Instruments (TI), Micron, and GlobalFoundries (GF)—key players in the "Made in USA" agenda—now face steeper trade barriers in the Chinese market.

Rather than splitting the industry into geopolitical camps, China is packaging the rule under a pro-globalization narrative—a subtle but pointed jab at Trump's protectionist doctrine.

China splits the supply chain—but welcomes some US players

The revised rules create a clear divide. US firms with domestic fabs or dependence on US-based foundries—such as TI, Onsemi, ADI, Intel, and Micron—bear the brunt of the penalties.

Fabless firms like Qualcomm, Nvidia, and AMD, which predominantly tape out at offshore foundries such as TSMC and Samsung, are largely shielded from the new tariffs.

China is wagering that higher tariffs on US-designed and US-fabbed chips will push American firms to diversify production—toward Southeast Asia, Europe, or other non-US regions. Beijing is also extending an olive branch, signaling that access to its market may hinge on decoupling from US fabs. Industry sources indicate some chipmakers are being encouraged to explicitly avoid US-based production for chips sold in China.

This emerging dual-track approach is accelerating the fragmentation of the global chip supply chain, fueling what's now widely described as the G2 tech divide.

Strategic calculation: punish the US, protect China's OEM edge

Despite some progress by Huawei and SMIC in 7nm—and claims of 5nm—China still lacks sufficient advanced-node capacity. Most leading-edge chip designs continue to come from US firms.

This puts Beijing in a delicate position. Hefty tariffs on Qualcomm or Nvidia could raise costs and erode the global competitiveness of Chinese OEMs like Lenovo, Xiaomi, Oppo, and Vivo—all of which rely on US-designed chips for overseas markets.

Beijing's move also reflects the shifting policy winds in Washington. Biden's 2022 CHIPS Act funneled US$39 billion into domestic chipmaking subsidies.

But with Trump's return to office in 2025, the playbook has changed. He's pushing to repeal the CHIPS Act and instead leverage tariffs to coerce global players like TSMC into expanding US-based production.

Different tactics, same goal: both Trump and Biden want semiconductor production back on US soil. China's latest move is a direct shot at stalling that bipartisan ambition.
