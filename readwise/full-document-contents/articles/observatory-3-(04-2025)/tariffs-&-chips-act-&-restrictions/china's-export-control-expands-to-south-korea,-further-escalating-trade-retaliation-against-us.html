China has expanded its export controls on rare earth minerals and magnets, intensifying its trade retaliation against the United States and threatening to disrupt key industries. The new restrictions affect seven rare earth elements and specialized magnets used in electric vehicles, wind turbines, and military aircraft—resources on which the US remains heavily dependent.

Disrupting production plans

Tesla CEO Elon Musk revealed that China's tightened controls on rare earth magnet exports have disrupted the company's production plans for its humanoid robot, Optimus.

According to Reuters, Chinese authorities are seeking assurances that exported rare earth magnets will not be used for military purposes. Musk said Tesla is currently working with Beijing to secure the necessary export licenses.

"China wants some assurances that these are not used for military purposes, which they're not. They're just going into a humanoid robot," he said during an earnings conference call, adding that it's not a weapon.

Broader retaliation strategy

As part of its broader retaliation to US tariffs, China has imposed stricter export restrictions on key rare earth resources essential for manufacturing weapons, electronics, and consumer goods. The controls now extend beyond raw materials to include magnets and other hard-to-substitute finished products.

Exporters must now apply for licenses from China's Ministry of Commerce. The process is opaque, with approval timelines ranging from six to seven weeks or even longer.

Expansion to third-country sales

In a sign of escalating geopolitical tensions, China has reportedly issued warning letters to South Korean companies, cautioning them against using Chinese rare earth materials in products destined for US defense contractors. The move signals a potential expansion of China's export control regime to include indirect, third-country sales, mirroring long-standing US restrictions on semiconductors shipped to China via third parties.

According to The Korea Economic Daily, at least one South Korean manufacturer of power equipment recently received a formal notice from Chinese authorities. The document demanded assurances that the company would not export any products containing Chinese heavy rare earths to US military contractors or the US military itself. It warned that violations could result in sanctions.

Although Beijing has not outlined specific penalties, South Korean industry officials widely believe that companies found violating the directive could face an outright ban on future purchases of Chinese heavy rare earths—a move that could disrupt key sectors.

Industry-wide impact

Sources say the warning extends beyond the power equipment industry. Firms in a range of strategic sectors—including battery manufacturing, display panels, electric vehicles, aerospace, and medical devices—have reportedly received similar notices, indicating a broader enforcement effort by Chinese authorities.
