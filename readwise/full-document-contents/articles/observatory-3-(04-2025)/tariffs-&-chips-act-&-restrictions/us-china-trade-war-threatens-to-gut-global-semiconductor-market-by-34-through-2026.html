The US-China trade war shows no signs of de-escalation, with recent industry analysis warning that if both sides maintain tariffs exceeding 100%, the global semiconductor market could shrink by 34% by 2026, severely impacting the sector's growth prospects.

Wccftech reports that if global tariffs remain around 10%, the semiconductor market is projected to grow from US$777 billion in 2025 to US$844 billion in 2026, reflecting an annual growth rate of 8.6%.

If the US and China continue imposing tariffs above 100%, the global semiconductor market could shrink to US$696 billion in 2025 and further drop to US$557 billion in 2026—34% below projections based on a 10% tariff scenario.

If US tariffs on China fall to the 30–40% range and global average tariffs stabilize between 20–40%, the market is forecast to reach US$736 billion in 2025 and US$699 billion in 2026—still significantly lower than earlier projections.

TechInsights warns that if elevated tariffs become the new normal, the global semiconductor market will contract further, and the average US import tariff rate could rise to 40%, adding sustained pressure on supply chains and end-product prices.

The US has imposed tariffs of up to 145% on a wide range of Chinese imports. However, semiconductors, consumer electronics, and smartphones—including Apple's iPhone—have temporarily avoided the steep hikes, facing instead a 20% fentanyl-related special tariff.

The semiconductor supply chain is intricate, spanning raw material extraction, advanced manufacturing, and final assembly. As the backbone of industries from smartphones to automobiles, semiconductors are increasingly vulnerable to trade tensions, with US restrictions and China's rare earth export bans driving up global market risks.

The White House is reportedly weighing partial tariff exemptions for automakers to encourage supply chain shifts from Mexico and Canada. For now, vehicle and auto part imports from non-China regions remain subject to a 25% tariff.

China has responded with 125% tariffs on all US imports, banned rare earth exports to the US, and is drafting additional restrictions targeting the US military and its suppliers.

Rare earth elements are vital to many US industries, yet the country still lacks a fully self-sufficient domestic supply chain.

Global semiconductor market outlook and growth drivers

TECHx Media, citing internal Gartner forecasts, projects global semiconductor revenue will grow 14% to US$717 billion in 2025, fueled by rising demand for AI chips and a rebound in electronics production.

IC Insights forecasts global semiconductor sales will grow at a 7.1% compound annual growth rate, surpassing US$700 billion by 2026.

Data from the Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA) shows global semiconductor sales hit US$627.6 billion in 2024, a 19.1% increase from 2023, with double-digit growth expected through 2025.

AI and memory markets driving new momentum

The rapid growth of artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping semiconductor demand. Gartner projects GPU revenue will reach US$51 billion by 2025, up 27% year-over-year, while HBM revenue is forecast to surge 70% to US$21 billion.

China's 'five cards' face real-world limits

Up Media, citing a BBC report, notes that China holds five strategic "cards" in its trade battle with the US—including its vast domestic market, authoritarian system resilience, investments in future technologies, partnerships with the Global South, and its holdings of US Treasury bonds and rare earth resources. However, serious structural challenges remain.

First, China's domestic market potential has yet to materialize. In the first quarter of 2024, retail sales rose just 3.1% year-over-year, while the household savings rate climbed to 32%, signaling weak consumer confidence.

Second, the resilience of China's authoritarian system is finite. Rising youth unemployment and mounting social discontent suggest that prolonged economic strain could ultimately erode political and economic stability.

Third, despite heavy investment in AI, semiconductors, and renewables, China's self-sufficiency in cutting-edge semiconductor manufacturing (sub-7nm) remains under 15%. Key equipment like EUV lithography machines remains blocked by US, Japanese, and EU export controls, constraining China's ability to counter US sanctions.

Fourth, China's push to boost exports to the Global South faces structural limits. While BRI trade is expanding, per capita GDP in most partner countries remains under US$5,000, far below levels in the US and Europe, making it hard to offset the loss of high-value markets.

Finally, while China once leveraged its US Treasury holdings and rare earth dominance, these advantages are diminishing. According to Nikkei and Reuters, China's Treasury holdings have fallen nearly 40% since 2018, and large-scale sell-offs risk triggering capital flight and financial instability at home.

Meanwhile, Western nations are accelerating rare earth diversification, with the US and Australia ramping up production and weakening China's monopoly.

Overall, while China retains strategic assets, its ability to withstand prolonged tariff pressure and structural supply chain shifts faces mounting limitations.
