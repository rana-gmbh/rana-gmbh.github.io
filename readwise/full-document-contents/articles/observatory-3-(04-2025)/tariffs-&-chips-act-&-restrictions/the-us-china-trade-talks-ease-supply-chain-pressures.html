In a rare sign of easing tensions, the US and China have reached a temporary détente on tariffs, agreeing to a 90-day truce that significantly reduces mutual trade penalties. Under the agreement, Washington will lower tariffs on Chinese imports to a maximum of 30%, while Beijing will scale back its duties on American goods to 10%.

The move, announced in Geneva via a joint statement, marks the first substantial de-escalation in a trade war that had, over the past month, pushed tariff rates to historically high levels, reaching a combined 145% on some Chinese imports and 125% on US goods.

Analysts say the truce not only reduces near-term uncertainty but also hints at a new de facto framework for global tariff negotiations. The US reduction to 30% may signal a ceiling for future trade talks, with a potential negotiation range now emerging between 10% and 30%—a development they consider crucial in restoring stability to global supply chains.

Supply chain impacts

In recent weeks, supply chains have been under intense pressure. Memory chipmakers, in particular, had seen a surge of urgent orders, driven both by tariff anxiety and Samsung Electronics' planned phase-out of DDR4 production. With the trade environment calming and inventories built up, the frantic pace of stockpiling is expected to ease, returning the industry to a more stable rhythm in the second half of 2025.

Despite the reduced tariff rates, the 30% cap remains a significant burden for many Chinese exporters, whose competitiveness in the US market will remain under strain. The Trump administration, which took office in January 2025, has not backed away from its broader policy push to onshore manufacturing. However, the compromise may establish a working precedent for future bilateral and multilateral negotiations.

Future negotiation framework

Industry insiders note that while this reprieve offers relief, the long-term picture remains fraught with geopolitical uncertainty. Recently, the US signed a trade deal with the UK, which will subject most British goods to a new baseline US tariff rate of 10%. Given the close US-UK relationship and the UK's persistent trade deficit with America, this "floor" could become a reference point for future negotiations.

For economies like Taiwan, which previously faced potential reciprocal tariffs as high as 32%, this 10–30% corridor could open new avenues for dialogue. If tensions remain in check, the maximum tariff rate could represent the upper limit of risk, with some countries able to negotiate lower benchmarks.

Memory sector outlook

In the memory sector, suppliers are cautiously optimistic. The past two months saw a rush of expedited orders due to geopolitical risk and shrinking legacy supply, but that urgency may now give way to more normalized demand cycles. If customers had front-loaded annual procurement into the first half of the year, a sluggish second half might have followed. Instead, the easing of trade tensions could allow a steadier pace of shipments through the end of 2025.

NAND flash manufacturer Winbond recently stated that demand in the PC and smart home segments began recovering in March, with continued strength expected from automotive and wearable tech markets in the second half of the year. While geopolitical and currency risks persist, the rebound in urgent orders and flash product demand supports a cautiously optimistic outlook for the remainder of the year.
