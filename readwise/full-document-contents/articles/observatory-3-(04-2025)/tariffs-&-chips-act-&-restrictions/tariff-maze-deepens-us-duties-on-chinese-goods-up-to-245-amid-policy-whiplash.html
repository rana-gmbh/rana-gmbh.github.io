Since April, the Trump administration's tariff policies have undergone frequent changes, creating confusion for businesses and traders struggling to adapt. Although the White House announced on April 11 that smartphones, PCs, and other products would be temporarily exempt from reciprocal tariffs—offering tech giants like Apple some breathing room—Chinese exports to the US still face tariffs ranging from 20% to as high as 245%.

According to US government customs data, there were 12 tariff updates in 2023 and 2024—averaging once per month. However, in just the first three and a half months of 2025, tariff documents have already been updated nine times, with two updates issued on April 10 alone. This unprecedented frequency highlights the escalating uncertainty created by trade policies during Trump's second term.

Layered tariffs from Biden to Trump

Although tariffs imposed during Trump's first term in 2018 and 2019 have expired, the Biden and Trump administrations rolled out at least four new rounds of tariffs targeting China from 2024 to 2025.

In September 2024, the Biden administration invoked Section 301 to impose 25%, 50%, and even 100% tariffs on over 200 Chinese goods—including lithium batteries, hybrid and electric vehicles, semiconductors, face masks, and medical gloves. Some tariffs, such as those on solar cells and modules, have already taken effect, while others—like a 50% tariff on semiconductors—began in January 2025. Certain products, such as permanent magnets, have been announced for a 25% tariff but have yet to be enforced.

In February 2025, Trump imposed a 10% tariff on imports from China and Hong Kong (excluding Macau) under the pretext of combating synthetic opioids. This was replaced just a month later, in March, by a steeper 20% import tariff.

On April 2, the US introduced a global 10% baseline tariff and announced retaliatory tariffs ranging from 10% to 49%. While most of these retaliatory tariffs have been given a 90-day grace period, tariffs on China—which the US claims took retaliatory measures of its own—have escalated to 125%.

From Biden's September 2024 tariffs to Trump's April 2025 measures, Chinese exports to the US now face a complex tariff regime with rates ranging from 20% to 245%. As of April 11, 141 product categories—such as smartphones, tablets, and laptops—have received temporary exemptions, bringing their tariffs to 20%. However, hybrid and electric vehicles from China face the steepest tariffs, at up to 245%. High-purity silicon and silicon wafers are taxed at 195% as of April 14, while semiconductors are subject to a combined 70% tariff—50% from the Biden administration and an additional 20% from Trump.

In addition, the above tariffs apply only to US tariffs on Chinese goods and do not include duties on global electric vehicles, steel, and aluminum. These are subject to additional charges based on the steel and aluminum content of individual products. The 10% baseline tariff imposed globally by Trump must also be added separately.
