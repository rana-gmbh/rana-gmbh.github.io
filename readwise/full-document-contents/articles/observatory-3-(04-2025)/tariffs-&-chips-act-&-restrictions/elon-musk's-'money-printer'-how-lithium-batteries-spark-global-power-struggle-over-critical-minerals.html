In 2022, lithium battery shortages for electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage reached a peak. Tesla CEO Elon Musk urged global investment in lithium refining, calling it a "money printer" and touting guaranteed profits for those who entered the market.

By 2023, the lithium supply shortage had subsided, and Musk became less vocal about the issue. Tesla, however, stayed the course with its lithium investment strategy. Meanwhile, the industry faced a sudden oversupply crisis.

Demand fails to meet expectations, supply chain scrambles

Demand failed to materialize as quickly as Musk had projected. Sales of battery electric vehicles (BEVs) fell short of expectations, prompting automakers in the US and Europe to scale back production plans. The slowdown led to inefficiencies and left significant capital investments stranded. One of the hardest-hit companies is Northvolt, Europe's flagship battery manufacturer, now facing possible bankruptcy and restructuring.

China's cutthroat competition deepens the crisis

China, the powerhouse of the lithium battery supply chain, saw intense competition among domestic manufacturers. When shortages emerged, companies flooded the market, expecting easy profits. However, as production surged, prices collapsed, leading to a fierce market correction.

Industry giants like CATL and BYD weathered the storm, but many smaller manufacturers struggled or shut down. The volatile cycle drained both corporate and national resources, leaving long-term economic repercussions.

Lithium's strategic importance keeps it on the global radar

Despite short-term market fluctuations, lithium remains a critical strategic resource for governments worldwide. The biggest challenge: global supply chains still lag behind China's dominance.

EV demand has slowed in the short term, but energy storage solutions continue to expand. However, high production costs make domestic lithium battery manufacturing unviable for many countries. Meanwhile, China, despite its saturated domestic market, is aggressively expanding overseas.

China's full-spectrum lithium export strategy triggers global pushback

Three years ago, China led the global lithium battery market with cell production. Now, its reach extends beyond cells to power conversion systems (PCS), battery management systems (BMS), and energy management systems (EMS). This "full-chain" expansion has triggered alarm in Western countries, wary of China securing a monopoly over the industry.

Europe has responded with new regulations, such as battery passports and local content rules requiring at least 60% of rooftop energy storage components to be locally produced. These policies have driven Chinese battery firms to set up manufacturing plants in Europe.

Geopolitical shifts: the US eyes Ukraine's rich mineral reserves

Recent negotiations between former US President Donald Trump and Ukraine underscore the geopolitical stakes of securing battery materials. Ukraine has some of the world's largest graphite reserves, a key material for EV batteries. It also holds one-third of Europe's lithium deposits—essential for Tesla's so-called "money printer" strategy—along with significant reserves of nickel and cobalt, both critical to battery production.

Ukraine also holds 17 rare-earth metals essential for aerospace, defense, wind turbines, and electronics. These resources are key to nuclear reactor and weapons production—sectors where China already dominates thanks to its rich rare-earth reserves.

The race to control critical minerals is intensifying, with lithium at the center of a high-stakes geopolitical and industrial battle. The outcome will shape the future of battery supply chains, influencing not just EVs but also the energy and defense sectors.
