Intel and AMD reap nearly 30% of revenue from China, yet US semiconductor imports are just 3%—What's behind the discrepancy?

Credit: DIGITIMES

Washington's export controls and escalating US-China chip tensions have dominated headlines, but American semiconductor leaders continue to rely heavily on the Chinese market. In 2023, China drove 27% of Intel's global revenue, roughly US$14.6 billion, and 15% of AMD's, about US$3.4 billion. Nvidia reported China accounted for over 13% of its fiscal 2025 revenue, totaling approximately US$17 billion. Despite Beijing's push for self-reliance, these numbers highlight China's critical role for US chipmakers. However, Chinese customs data reveals a stark contrast, with US semiconductors comprising just 3% of China's total chip imports.

US chipmakers' outsized revenue in China

In 2024, Intel and AMD, two prominent American processor manufacturers, generated significant portions of their revenue from China, accounting for 29% and 24% of their total income, respectively. This translated to approximately US$15.5 billion for Intel and US$6.2 billion for AMD. Nvidia, a key player in the artificial intelligence sector, experienced a decline in its revenue share from China to 13.1% during its 2025 fiscal year, yet still reported around US$17 billion from the region. Together, these three US companies earned more than US$30 billion from the Chinese market, highlighting its continued strategic importance.

Chinese customs data reveals a markedly different scenario. According to China's General Administration of Customs, China imported only US$12.8 billion worth of semiconductors from the US in 2024, representing just 3.1% of its total semiconductor imports. Meanwhile, imports from manufacturing powerhouses Taiwan, South Korea, and Malaysia surged to US$142.5 billion, US$84.9 billion, and US$26 billion, respectively. This gap prompts a critical question: Why does the robust revenue of US chipmakers in China not align with official import figures?

Globalization obscures 'country of origin'

The explanation can be found in the complex and globalized production network of the semiconductor industry. Chips created by US-based companies such as Intel or AMD are frequently manufactured at foundries located in Taiwan or South Korea and then assembled or packaged in regions like Southeast Asia or China. Traditionally, the designation of "country of origin" for trade regulations has been based on the site of final assembly or testing rather than the location of design. As a result, semiconductors produced outside the US, including those from American companies, are not categorized as US imports by Chinese customs authorities.

Since 2025, China's Semiconductor Industry Association has advocated for defining a chip's origin based on where it is fabricated (wafer production). Under this standard, only chips manufactured in US foundries qualify as American. Processors made by AMD or Intel at TSMC's non-US facilities or Intel's Fab 36 in Ireland are excluded, further skewing trade data. If the origin were tied to the headquarters of the designing company, US semiconductor imports to China would far exceed the reported US$12.8 billion.

In 2024, China's semiconductor exports to the US reached US$31.8 billion, representing a small 1.53% share of its total global semiconductor exports. Among China's leading export markets, the US ranked 11th in terms of export volume. If the Trump administration were to impose tariffs based on the US$31.8 billion figure, the resulting revenue would likely fall short of expectations. This shortfall could lead to exemptions for electronic products such as smartphones and laptops, which contain semiconductor components but also face potential retaliatory tariffs.

Trade imbalance in high-value chips

A detailed examination of 2024 trade data highlights a pronounced imbalance in semiconductor trade between the US and China, especially in high-value segments. China imported semiconductors worth US$12.8 billion from the US while exporting US$31.8 billion in the same category. Integrated circuits (ICs) make up the bulk of this trade, with imports reaching US$11.75 billion and exports at US$2.22 billion. Within ICs, processors represent a significant portion, accounting for US$8.68 billion in imports and US$1.58 billion in exports, alongside categories such as memory, amplifiers, and other ICs. Smaller shares are held by sensors, power devices, optoelectronic components, and discrete devices. Products like smartphones and laptops, which incorporate these semiconductor chips, are also involved in trade but face mutual tariff challenges.

The uneven distribution in the processor sector reflects the significant revenue that Intel and AMD generate from China, nearing 30%. This situation highlights China's dependence on advanced chips designed in the US, a demand that local manufacturers have yet to satisfy comprehensively. While China is contemplating retaliatory tariffs in response to Trump's policies, semiconductor products fabricated at non-US foundries, including those made by AMD and Intel at TSMC, are expected to remain largely unaffected by these potential tariffs.

Supply chain complexity masks true economic ties

The semiconductor trade relationship between the US and China presents a complex picture. While US chipmakers reap substantial revenue from China, official import data understates their footprint due to globalized production and evolving origin standards. As tariffs and export controls intensify, China's push for semiconductor self-sufficiency gains urgency, yet its dependence on American technology persists. This intricate dynamic will shape the next chapter of US-China tech rivalry.
