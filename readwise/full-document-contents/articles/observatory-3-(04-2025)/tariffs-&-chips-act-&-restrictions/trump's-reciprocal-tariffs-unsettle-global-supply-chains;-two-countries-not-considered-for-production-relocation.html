According to sources within the notebook supply chain, meetings with brand manufacturers are intensifying as companies navigate the uncertainty created by the unpredictable nature of Trump's tariff policies. While no one can say for certain what direction the policy will take, two things are clear: assembly lines will not remain in China, and they will not return to the US.

Furthermore, brand manufacturers are deeply concerned that the US tariffs are not only meant to address trade imbalances but also to combat the shift of production—a practice that could result in significantly higher production costs.

Supply chain insiders reveal that brands are facing a multitude of issues related to Trump's reciprocal tariffs, including unclear exemption provisions related to the 20% US content requirement and whether goods can be shipped from ODM facilities in Mexico under the USMCA tariff exemption.

While many notebook brands have already diversified production to Southeast Asia and Taiwan, these moves will continue, though the actual extent of relocation will depend on the tariffs imposed on each country following the reciprocal tariffs. The one commonality across all manufacturers is the accelerated push to move finished goods out of China, though they will not be returning to the US due to prohibitive costs.

Historically, brand manufacturers have gradually shifted their US-bound production to Southeast Asia, with only a small portion going to Mexico. However, with the 25% tariff already imposed on China and notebooks excluded from any exemptions, manufacturers are now forced to reevaluate their production strategies. If the reciprocal tariffs go into effect, China's tariff on US-bound products will increase to 54%, potentially rising to 104%, making relocation more cost-effective than continuing production in China.

While many countries are now negotiating with the US on the reciprocal tariffs, only a few have openly clashed, with China being the most prominent example. Trump has threatened to raise tariffs by an additional 50% if China insists on retaliating, while the Ministry of Commerce of the People's Republic of China has signaled its readiness to fight back, indicating that these tensions may only escalate further.

As for the components sector, manufacturers have been attempting to move operations to Southeast Asia, but progress has been limited. The lack of tariffs on components allowed this shift, but production of final goods in Southeast Asia remains relatively low.

Components continue to be exported from China to their final assembly points, a practice unlikely to change in the short term. However, should the US implement reciprocal tariffs aimed at preventing "country-of-origin laundering," the components themselves may also need to be relocated, further complicating the situation.

Stephen Miran, Chair of the Council of Economic Advisers of the US, suggested five ways Taiwan could assist the US in shouldering its responsibilities, including accepting US tariffs without retaliatory measures, opening markets, increasing defense spending, investing in US factories, and even contributing directly to the US Treasury.

The Trump administration's approach to reciprocal tariffs appears to be less about fairness and more about eliminating trade deficits. As the supply chain braces for the consequences, industry leaders stress that while monetary solutions might be manageable, the real headache comes from the potential disruption of the global supply chain, especially if the US pushes forward with policies that force a full decoupling from China. This would create widespread chaos, and managing that risk could prove to be the most challenging aspect of the trade war.
