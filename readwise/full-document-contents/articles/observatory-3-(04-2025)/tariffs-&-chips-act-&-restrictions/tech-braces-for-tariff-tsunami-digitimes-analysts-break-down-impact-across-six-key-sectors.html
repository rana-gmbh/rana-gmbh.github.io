As Donald Trump ramps up his tariff-heavy trade agenda in a bid to bring manufacturing back to US soil, Taiwan's tech and manufacturing sectors are bracing for impact—again. From auto parts to semiconductors and servers to smartphones, a sweeping new set of tariffs threatens to unravel years of global supply chain planning.

DIGITIMES analysts share how these policies are playing out across six industries, from autos and semiconductors to servers and smartphones. Each expert outlines Trump's stance, the likely consequences, and what they'll be watching next.

Automotive: A direct hit to parts suppliers

Trump's latest move on the auto industry includes a 25% tariff on imported vehicles starting April 3, 2025, followed by a 25% tariff on 130 categories of auto parts from May 3, 2025. While USMCA countries are temporarily exempt, the broad scope of the parts list could expand further.

DIGITIMES analyst Jessie Lin says the new tariffs could significantly disrupt Taiwan's auto parts exporters—especially those that rely heavily on the US market but lack local production in North America.

"Suppliers with strong US sales will need to consider setting up operations locally to stay competitive," Lin notes. "Others with limited exposure may start looking more seriously at diversifying into other markets."

Servers: ASEAN-based EMS firms under pressure

For the server sector, analyst Tom Lo points out that the timing couldn't be worse. Many electronics manufacturing services (EMS) providers in ASEAN—excluding Singapore—have only recently ramped up or built new plants. Now, with new tariffs on the horizon, they face the prospect of relocating before recouping their investments.

"These factories are just starting to depreciate equipment, and they're already being forced to consider a scale-down or shift," Lo says. Meanwhile, the migration of low-margin components out of China is becoming a cost dilemma, one that may be passed on to clients.

In the short term, Lo suggests increasing production in the US and using Singapore's favorable tax regime as a buffer.

Over the longer run, the "China + US + 1" strategy—diversifying production across three regions—could become the norm. Lo also notes growing interest in expanding sales to countries developing sovereign AI infrastructure.

Semiconductors: strategic sectors face indirect risks

Though semiconductors haven't been hit with direct reciprocal tariffs, the industry isn't immune. Trump has called the sector "vital to national security" and is expected to introduce new policies aimed at boosting domestic IC manufacturing.

Eric Chen, who tracks the semiconductor space for DIGITIMES, says the most immediate concern is with semiconductor equipment, which will face higher tariffs when exported to the US—a blow to Taiwanese suppliers.

"There's a lot of uncertainty around tax incentives and how US tariffs on ICT products might ripple down to chip demand," Chen says. "If the broader economy slows, we'll feel it in semiconductor consumption."

Smartphones: High tariffs, but no clear policy

Unlike semiconductors, smartphones don't yet have a dedicated Trump policy—but that may not be good news. While there's no direct targeting, new reciprocal tariffs hit electronics from all major production hubs: 54% on China, 46% on Vietnam, 26% on India, 36% on Thailand, and 32% on Indonesia.

"There's no short-term way for Taiwanese firms to escape these tariffs by moving production," says analyst Luke Lin. "It's going to hurt."

What happens next depends on how Trump's team phases in the tariffs. A temporary 10% rate or phased rollout is still on the table. Analysts are also watching whether brands like Apple and Samsung pass costs to consumers and whether countries like Vietnam can strike better deals with Washington.

Telecom equipment: "Clean Network" policy shapes strategy

Trump's "Clean Network" push to eliminate Chinese telecom equipment from US infrastructure continues to reshape the telecom sector. Analyst Benson Wu says Taiwan-based network equipment makers are feeling the squeeze as Southeast Asian production hubs face heavy tariffs.

"The top Taiwanese players all rely heavily on North America, with many getting more than half their revenue from the region," Wu explains.

Ongoing trade talks between the US and Southeast Asian nations (expected to conclude by April 9, 2025) could determine just how deeply the new tariffs will be cut. Meanwhile, if US bans on Chinese consumer telecom devices expand, Wu believes Taiwanese brands may have a window to grab more market share.

Notebooks: Production hubs in jeopardy

The notebook industry may be facing the steepest tariffs of all. Trump's reciprocal tariff strategy slaps China with 54%, Vietnam with 46%, and Thailand with 36%—countries that together account for over 95% of the world's notebook production. About 27% of global notebook shipments go to the US.

Henry Chang, who covers the PC sector for DIGITIMES, says Taiwan's top ODMs are already delaying or halting expansion in Vietnam and Thailand at the request of major brand clients. Some brands have paused shipments to the US altogether.

"There's now a scramble to figure out how OEMs, ODMs, and retailers will share the tax burden," Chang notes. There's also talk of setting up new facilities in Central or South America—regions with lower tariff exposure.

What's next?

Across the board, DIGITIMES analysts agree: this new round of tariffs is forcing a reckoning. Some companies will pivot to local manufacturing, others will diversify. But all will need to rethink cost structures, supply chains, and pricing strategies.

Whether Trump's policies take full effect or get moderated through negotiations, the message to tech and manufacturing firms is clear—adapt fast, or risk falling behind.
