In his recent speech to Congress, US president Donald Trump claimed that South Korea's average tariff rate is four times that of the US, raising concerns that the Asian ally, with Samsung Electronics in particular, may become the next target of Trump's trade war.

South Korean officials have questioned Trump's claim. They said that except for some specific agricultural products, there are almost no tariffs on trade between the US and South Korea, thanks to the free trade agreement (FTA) signed between the two countries in 2012.

Many industry observers speculate that Trump is actually sending a trade war warning to South Korea.

According to reports from various Korean news outlets, Trump told the Congress that despite the strong US support it is receiving in military and many other aspects, South Korea still imposes a tariff rate four times as much as that of the US.

Some observers speculate that Trump might be referring to the average most-favored-nation (MFN) tariff rates among members of the World Trade Organization (WTO). In short, MFN tariffs refer to a WTO member's normal non-discriminatory tariffs charged on imports from fellow members. For example, in 2023, the average MFN tariff of the US was 3.3%, and South Korea 13.4%. In terms of MFN tariffs, South Korea seems to be charging four times as much as the US does.

However, in reality, trade between the two countries mostly follows the FTA they have signed. For example, in 2024, South Korea's average tariff on US imports was only 0.79% (effective tariff rate). If the subsequent tariff refunds were taken into consideration, the actual tariff might be even lower.

South Korean government sources noted that currently no tariffs are imposed on US industrial products. Except for some US agricultural products such as rice and oranges, 98% of the imported items from the US are subject to zero tariffs.

The sources further speculated that Trump might be heaping pressure on South Korea to facilitate future trade talks between the two countries.

Samsung at stake

In his speech to Congress, Trump also criticized the Biden Administration for providing subsidies to foreign semiconductor companies such as Samsung, calling on the US lawmakers to terminate them.

Samsung's investment in the US is falling behind schedule. South Korean industry observers believe that Samsung faces a bleak outlook for its US investment and subsidies.

South Korea exports large numbers of semiconductor chips, cars and other products to the US every year. In 2024, China remained the biggest export market for South Korea, with the value of exports rising 6.6% annually to US$133 billion. The US was the second largest with US$127.8 billion, up 10.5% year on year to hit a record high for the seventh consecutive year.

The Korea Institute for Industrial Economics and Trade (KIET) has estimated that if the US implements a tariff of 10-20%, South Korea's exports to the US will decrease by 8.4-14%, and South Korea's GDP may decline by 0.1-0.2pp.
