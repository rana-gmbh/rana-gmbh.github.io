At the beginning of 2025, the US Department of Defense blacklisted China's lithium battery leader, Contemporary Amperex Technology (CATL), followed by US President Donald Trump imposing a 20% tariff on Chinese products, including lithium batteries. Recently, the US further prevented the US Department of Homeland Security from purchasing batteries manufactured by Chinese companies.

On Monday, the US House of Representatives approved a bill, titled the "Decoupling from Foreign Adversarial Battery Dependence Act", that would prohibit the US Department of Homeland Security from acquiring batteries produced by six Chinese manufacturers, including CATL, BYD, Envision Energy, EVE Energy, Hithium, and Gotion.

The evaluation focuses on critical issues such as cybersecurity, border security, supply chain vulnerabilities, and cooperation with government agencies.

Supply chain sources indicate that this restriction applies only to government procurement and is expected to have limited impact on Chinese battery manufacturers. The procurement of electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage products in the US will likely remain unchanged.

However, the expected surge in US energy storage demand may face hidden obstacles. The series of sudden developments—from the blacklist to tariffs to procurement bans—make it difficult to anticipate the full impact.

Earlier this year, Asian supply chain reports highlighted that tariffs on US energy storage lithium batteries would increase to 38.4% by 2026, driving manufacturing costs up by 50%. Due to these cost considerations, 2025 was expected to be the peak for the US energy storage rush. However, there are differing opinions, as the speed at which tariffs and bans accumulate might be faster than expected.

Therefore, businesses seeking to capitalize on this rush need to stay particularly alert to avoid setbacks. As for the EV sector, with Trump's plan to cut the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) subsidies, US market demand may experience turbulence.

Industry insiders note that US automakers and energy storage companies have increasingly felt the strain of rising US-China tensions in recent years and are actively seeking alternative solutions, particularly those tied to domestic manufacturing supply chains.

US domestic battery supply chain expands

According to media reports, in 2025, ten new battery plants are expected to come online in the US, with a total capacity of 422 GWh—almost double the current production rate. Companies such as LGES, SK On, Panasonic, Samsung SDI, and AESC are expected to supply major automakers like Toyota, Ford, Hyundai, and Honda. In addition, Toyota's North Carolina plant, Tesla, and Ford are also investing in domestic battery cell production. If these new capacities come online, they are expected to support the demand for millions of new energy vehicles.

Interestingly, most of these battery plants are located in states that lean Republican, despite Trump's opposition to EVs.

However, the market still favors ternary lithium batteries, while demand for lithium iron phosphate (LFP), which is the key for energy storage and affordable EVs, remains lower. Nonetheless, LFP production is gradually ramping up, with LGES set to begin large-scale LFP production in the US later this year.

Furthermore, AESC, a Chinese battery cell manufacturer, operates three plants in the US, supplying products for both EVs and energy storage. Originally part of Nissan's business, AESC was sold to Envision Group in 2018. The company is capable of producing both ternary and LFP batteries, with the latter seeing significant interest.

Gotion, one of the top five Chinese LFP manufacturers, also operates a plant in the US. However, its establishment has been contentious. Local officials in Green Township, Michigan, who initially supported the project, were later voted out of office amid protests from residents opposing the plant's construction. As a result, Gotion has taken legal action, citing a previously signed agreement. The case is currently undergoing trial, but the township is obligated to honor the original development agreement.
