China's foundry industry has seen explosive growth in mature semiconductor manufacturing over the past five years—a trend reflected in ASML's financial disclosures, where Chinese businesses once accounted for as much as 40–50% of revenue. But by early 2025, that figure had dropped to about 25%.

According to a report by SEMI, between 2019 and 2024, 128 new wafer fabs were constructed worldwide, with China claiming 30%-its expansion peaking between 2019 and 2021.

The US push for "Made in America" semiconductors

The global semiconductor race is being redrawn—this time with Washington leading the charge. In a rare show of bipartisan resolve, both the Biden and Trump administrations have thrown their weight behind rebuilding America's chipmaking muscle.

Biden put the spotlight on domestic semiconductor production during his 2022 State of the Union address, where former Intel CEO Pat Gelsinger was seated prominently next to the First Lady—a symbolic nod to the administration's commitment to reviving US manufacturing. After assuming Intel's helm in February 2021, Gelsinger positioned himself as a vanguard of advanced chip production in the US, citing the supply chain disruptions during the COVID pandemic as a catalyst for enhancing domestic capacity.

The push to bring semiconductor manufacturing back to American soil hasn't lost steam under the Trump 2.0 administration—it's just taken a different path. Instead of subsidies, Trump has leaned into tariffs and aggressive trade measures to pressure global chipmakers into setting up shop in the US.

The approach sharply contrasts with President Biden's CHIPS Act, which uses billions in federal incentives to attract semiconductor giants. However, both aim to secure America's tech supply chains and reduce reliance on foreign chip production amid growing geopolitical uncertainty.

China's strategic response: Localization and mature process dominance

While the US and Europe prioritized advanced-node investments through legislative incentives, China pursued a two-pronged strategy: accelerating mature-node capacity to meet domestic demand and leveraging its relative insulation from US export controls on legacy equipment.

Over the past five years, Chinese integrated circuit (IC) design companies have considerably expanded their presence in the mid-to-low-end consumer electronics and telecommunications markets, positioning themselves as credible competitors against established firms from Europe, the US, Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan.

This growth is driven by a strategic focus on a "Local for Local" approach, encouraging Chinese chip designers and integrated device manufacturers (IDMs) to rely heavily on domestic supply chains, which has led to major increases in local wafer foundry capacity.

While Washington and Brussels bet big on bleeding-edge chip technology through their respective CHIPS Acts in 2021 and 2022, Beijing has played a different hand—focusing squarely on mature process nodes, especially the all-important 28nm level. It's a smart move: the tech gap at this node is much narrower, and unlike advanced manufacturing tools, the equipment needed is largely outside the reach of US export controls.

That strategic focus is already paying off. Chinese foundries have ramped up production to meet the growing demands of local designers and manufacturers, while global equipment makers such as ASML and top Japanese suppliers are seeing windfalls from China's steady march in mature chipmaking.

Two-phase evolution of China's foundry industry

China's wafer foundry sector has evolved through two distinct phases over the past five years. Between 2021 and 2022, global capacity shortages prompted IC design firms to redirect production to Chinese foundries, particularly for components such as LCD drivers, CMOS image sensors, microcontrollers, WiFi chips, power devices, and bipolar-CMOS-DMOS (BCD) technologies. This shift was catalyzed by the COVID pandemic, which exposed vulnerabilities in global supply chains and spurred Chinese chip designers to pivot toward domestic production.

The second phase, spanning 2023 to 2024, was marked by heightened geopolitical pressures, particularly following successive US export control amendments by the Biden administration on October 7, 2022, October 17, 2023, and December 2, 2024. These restrictions intensified China's resolve to achieve semiconductor self-sufficiency, prompting domestic IC designers to accelerate the localization of mature process production and foster collaborative R&D with Chinese foundries.

Global players embrace China's manufacturing ecosystem

Notably, China's appeal as a manufacturing hub extends beyond domestic players. Several international IC design firms and IDMs have opted to produce in Chinese foundries, driven by cost efficiencies, supply chain compatibility, and the need to navigate geopolitical restrictions.

In late 2024, European semiconductor titans STMicroelectronics (STM), NXP Semiconductors, and Infineon Technologies announced plans to deepen their engagement with China's manufacturing ecosystem. For instance, on November 21, 2024, STM revealed a partnership with Chinese foundry Hua Hong to produce 40nm microcontrollers domestically by the end of 2025, citing cost-effectiveness and risk mitigation as key considerations.

China's wafer foundry industry has demonstrated remarkable resilience and adaptability, navigating global supply chain disruptions and geopolitical headwinds to cement its position in mature process manufacturing.
