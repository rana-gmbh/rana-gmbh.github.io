TSMC's recent announcement of an additional US$100 billion investment in US manufacturing has sparked sharp criticism from Beijing. Chinese media and government officials frame the expansion as US-coerced offshoring of Taiwan's semiconductor crown jewels, with the Chinese State Council's Taiwan Affairs Office explicitly characterizing the chipmaker's decision as "gifting" critical technology to American competitors.

Chinese media viewed TSMC's US investment as a sign of technology outflow and the weakening of Taiwan's semiconductor industry. Commentators argue that the US is pushing TSMC to build facilities within its borders and seeking confidential technical data and advanced technologies, potentially compromising Taiwan's technological edge and competitiveness globally.

The Chinese state-run Global Times claims that the US intends to relocate Taiwan's semiconductor sector by utilizing TSMC's technology to enhance its industry, like Intel. This could shift Taiwan from a key player in the global semiconductor supply chain to a technology supplier for the US rather than a technology leader.

Furthermore, during a speech to Congress, Donald Trump urged lawmakers to repeal the CHIPS Act and proposed higher tariffs to encourage semiconductor firms to move to the US. Chinese media view this as an attempt to force TSMC to relocate without subsidies, with discussions suggesting a possible 100% tariff on Taiwanese chips to pressure suppliers like TSMC to move their production to the US.

The People's Daily suggests that the US is gradually weakening Taiwan's influence in the global supply chain. Guancha reported that TSMC's US$100 billion investment aligns with the US strategy to centralize the global semiconductor industry through tariffs and policies, thereby diminishing the role of Asian semiconductor sectors.

This strategy significantly affects Taiwan, pressures South Korean firms like Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, challenges Malaysia's IC backend industry, and increases pressure on Japanese and European semiconductor companies from the US.

A Chinese semiconductor think tank, IC Wise, highlighted that despite significant investments by Samsung and SK Hynix in the US, Trump announced that South Korea would not be exempt from his tariff policies, criticizing South Korea for having average tariffs four times higher than those of the US and stating that reciprocal tariffs would be enforced on allies and adversaries alike. Malaysia should also be concerned as it's the second-largest import source of semiconductors into the US.

Trump aims to establish the world's leading chip manufacturing base, seeking control over the global semiconductor supply chain. This strategy impacts companies like TSMC and pressures key international semiconductor firms. With tariffs and enforced relocations, significant restructuring in the global semiconductor industry is anticipated in 2025.
