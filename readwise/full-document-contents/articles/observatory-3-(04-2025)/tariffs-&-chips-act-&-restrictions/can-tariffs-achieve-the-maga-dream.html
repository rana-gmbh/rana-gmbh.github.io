The past week has seen a series of twists and turns in US reciprocal tariff rates, with the latest announcement on the US Customs and Border Protection's Cargo Systems Messaging Service (CSMS) listing exemptions for 20 HTS product categories, including notebooks, smartphones, tablets, and processors. Market watchers are paying close attention to forthcoming new tariff rates for semiconductors and pharmaceuticals, and perhaps more importantly, how the US's trade war on China will develop.

Much controversy has surrounded the methodology used to calculate the tariff rates, which was based simply on the US's trade surplus or deficit with each individual country. As many analysts believe that the Trump administration is using tariffs as a means to an end, market watchers should be more concerned that the US is leveraging tariffs to restructure the global economic order and bring manufacturing back to the US, as part of the administration's Make America Great Again (MAGA) agenda.

Analysis of US tariff strategy

Former Foxconn vice president Terry Cheng has given an insightful analysis on his Facebook page of the strategic implications of the reciprocal tariffs, which warrants a close read.

In summary, Cheng argues that any emerging country faces immense hurdles when advancing to become a developed country. During the initial phases of its reform and opening-up policy, China managed to attract global investments with its population, preferential policies, and vast internal market.

Over three decades, China instituted multiple stages of development, such as import substitution, localization, and generating currency reserves through exports, ultimately leapfrogging Europe and Japan to become the world's second largest economy after the US. In Cheng's opinion, China managed to achieve a rare economic miracle.

In comparison, Trump's tariff policy aims to achieve the above three stages in one fell swoop. In particular, the high tariff rates are intended to force the reshoring of the manufacturing industry—in essence, import substitution. Moreover, the high tariffs are applied not just to imports of finished products, but upstream components as well, with the goal of bringing home entire industrial supply chains as well, thus achieving localization.

Finally, based on the methodology used to calculate reciprocal tariff rates, the US creates the impression that countries may see a reduction in tariffs if they reduce their trade surpluses by buying more from the US. This will become an incentive and advantage for businesses that do return manufacturing to the US, helping to generate both exports and currency reserves.

Are high tariffs the answer?

Following from Cheng's commentary, it is evident that the current global integration of the manufacturing sector relies on a number of key factors: liberalized trade, favorable policies, robust infrastructure, corporate R&D and management capabilities, industrial clusters and logistics management, regional labor forces, and the cost of acquiring land, labor, and resources.

These highly intricate and orderly manufacturing, trade, and value chain systems were developed through decades of learning from experience, evolving alongside developments in the tech industry and shifts in the manufacturing sector. However, the US is now citing trade imbalance as a reason for the reciprocal tariff policy. It is using as a bargaining chip, even going so far as threatening China with ever increasing levies.

It is no surprise, then, that the US has drawn widespread concern and criticism for its heavy-handed tactics. Furthermore, the US's current "winner-takes-all" mindset has put global markets on high alert, as the threat of an economic decline looms large with the demise of the concept of mutual benefit.

Although we are in no position to dictate how the US should achieve its MAGA ambitions, the fact that the tariff policy is linked with efforts to reshore manufacturing signals a fundamental truth: businesses chase profit. However, in the absence of a comprehensive framework, financial incentives, favorable conditions for manufacturing, long-term vision, or concrete commitments, will tariffs alone be enough to drive foreign businesses to set up factories in the US? We may already have the answer.
