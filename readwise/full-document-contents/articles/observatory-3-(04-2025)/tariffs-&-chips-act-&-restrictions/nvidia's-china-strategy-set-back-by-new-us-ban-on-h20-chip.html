Nvidia Corp. disclosed that the U.S. government has imposed new export restrictions on its H20 AI chip bound for China, a setback for a product line specifically developed to comply with previous trade rules.

In a regulatory filing on Tuesday, Nvidia said it was notified the day prior that the H20 chip would now require a license for export to China "for the indefinite future." U.S. authorities cited concerns that the chips could be "used in, or diverted to, a supercomputer in China."

The move has forced Nvidia to take an estimated $5.5 billion charge in its fiscal first quarter, attributed to inventory, purchase commitments, and related reserves tied to the H20 product. Shares of the company slid approximately 5% in after-hours trading following the announcement.

Nvidia stated the following in its Form 8-K filing:

On April 9, 2025, the U.S. government, or USG, informed NVIDIA Corporation, or the Company, that the USG requires a license for export to China (including Hong Kong and Macau) and D:5 countries, or to companies headquartered or with an ultimate parent therein, of the Company's H20 integrated circuits and any other circuits achieving the H20's memory bandwidth, interconnect bandwidth, or combination thereof. The USG indicated that the license requirement addresses the risk that the covered products may be used in, or diverted to, a supercomputer in China.

On April 14, 2025, the USG informed the Company that the license requirement will be in effect for the indefinite future.

The Company's first quarter of fiscal year 2026 ends on April 27, 2025. First quarter results are expected to include up to approximately $5.5 billion of charges associated with H20 products for inventory, purchase commitments, and related reserves.

The new export licensing requirements also apply to AMD's MI308 chip, further tightening the restrictions on advanced AI hardware exports to China

DIGITIMES previously reported that demand for the H20 chip had surged in China, driven by major internet companies ramping up AI inference capacity—particularly since DeepSeek sparked a new wave of infrastructure buildout.

Alibaba was also reported to have made substantial purchases of the H20 chip to support Apple's "Apple Intelligence" AI features for the Chinese market. However, these orders may be jeopardized by the new export controls.

Since its launch in the second half of 2024, the downgraded H20 chip for China has faced mounting uncertainty. Both the Biden and Trump administrations had signaled the possibility of tightening export rules further. Just as demand began to rebound—thanks in part to DeepSeek's influence—China's National Development and Reform Commission reportedly stepped in to dissuade domestic firms from purchasing the chip.

According to Nvidia's channel partners, the H20 chip has recently become "extremely hard to get." If the new restrictions are enforced, production will be halted entirely. This would leave outstanding orders, including those from Alibaba, unfulfilled.

In late January 2025, Reuters reported that sources familiar with U.S. policymaking indicated the Trump administration was considering broader export control measures on Nvidia's China-specific chip lineup, including the H20.

The latest development marks a significant blow to Nvidia's China strategy and may further escalate tech tensions between the U.S. and China, particularly in the AI sector.
