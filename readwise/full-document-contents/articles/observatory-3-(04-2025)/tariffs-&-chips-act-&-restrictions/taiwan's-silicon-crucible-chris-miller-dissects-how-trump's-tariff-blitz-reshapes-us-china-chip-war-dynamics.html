In the shadow of broader trade tensions, tariffs on Chinese goods have quietly doubled during the first two months of Donald Trump's second term—a significant economic shift that has gone largely unnoticed. Renowned "Chip War" author Chris Miller brought this trend to light during a recent forum hosted by Taiwan's think tank DSET in Taipei. "This has happened at a time when Trump has been imposing tariffs on other countries, so we barely even noticed," the economic historian explained.

Credit: AFP
Credit: DIGITIMES

For Taiwan, whose economy depends heavily on global trade and whose semiconductor industry powers 90% of the world's advanced chip production, these escalating trade tensions could have far-reaching consequences, even if Taiwan itself avoids direct tariffs. "Any major shift in the world trading regime is bound to have a negative impact" on countries deeply embedded in global supply chains, Miller cautioned.

New era of semiconductor politics

Trump's recent White House meeting with TSMC Chairman C.C. Wei highlighted the increasingly political nature of the semiconductor industry. During the event announcing TSMC's expanded $100 billion investment in Arizona, Trump referred to the Taiwanese chipmaker as a "monopoly." This language not only raised eyebrows among industry observers but also acknowledged TSMC's extraordinary market dominance.

Credit: AFP
Credit: AFP

"No tech CEO likes to hear their company described as a monopoly," Miller observed. "But I think if you look historically, every technology company with such a level of market share has faced some degree of competition regulation or antitrust discussion with the US government." With TSMC(2330.TW) commanding approximately 90% market share in advanced chip manufacturing, such scrutiny seems inevitable.

The Arizona investment represents a significant expansion of TSMC's US footprint. Initially announced during Trump's first term, the amount was subsequently expanded under Biden and further ballooned after Trump's return to office. This continuity across administrations underscores the semiconductor industry's elevated importance in US strategic thinking, regardless of which party holds power.

Taiwan's chip dominance grows despite political pressures

Furthermore, Miller firmly dismisses concerns about Taiwan's semiconductor industry being "hollowed out" through overseas expansion. "Taiwan's position when it comes to semiconductors will remain very strong like it is today," he asserted.

Miller suggests the fundamental reason for Taiwan's continuing dominance is its technological leadership rather than political considerations. As long as Taiwan continues to advance semiconductor manufacturing capabilities faster than competitors, it will maintain its central position in the global supply chain.

Challenge for Taiwan under Trump's "hard-elbow diplomacy"

Beyond semiconductor manufacturing, Miller believes Taiwan faces a new diplomatic environment where economic, technological, and security discussions will become increasingly intertwined under Trump's transactional approach to foreign policy – a pattern already evident in US-European relations.

"President Trump has illustrated that he is keenly focused on questions of policy. He's focused on questions of power and leverage," Miller said, pointing to Trump's blunt assessment of Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy that "you don't have the cards" as emblematic of his approach.

Credit: AFP
Credit: AFP

This transactional view could challenge Taiwan as Trump demands greater defense spending from allies and partners while using economic pressure as leverage – a tactic Miller says has already yielded results with Germany's trillion-euro commitment to defense and infrastructure spending. "We should expect similar discussions with Taiwan," Miller warned, hinting at upcoming negotiations that will mix trade, technology, and security considerations.

Export controls set to tighten further

The tech competition between the US and China is intensifying under Trump's second term. Export controls on advanced semiconductors and manufacturing equipment—pioneered in Trump's first term and strengthened under Biden—are poised to become even more restrictive. "The next move on export controls would be to make them more restrictive, not less restrictive," Miller noted.

New restrictions could target cloud computing access, R&D know-how, and even legacy chips, where China is flooding markets with subsidized production. For Taiwan, this escalation presents both opportunities and challenges. While TSMC's dominance in cutting-edge chips aligns with US interests, Taiwanese firms navigating tightening US controls could face growing compliance challenges.

China's flood of legacy chips creates market vulnerability

One area where China is making rapid progress is in legacy chip production, creating a growing vulnerability for global supply chains. Western companies are increasingly hesitant to invest in these older-generation technologies due to Chinese pricing pressure.

Credit: AFP
Credit: AFP

"Western companies decline to invest more in legacy chip production because they were afraid if they did invest more, they wouldn't make any money, given the Chinese pricing," Miller explained.

This dynamic creates vulnerabilities in supply chains for automotive, industrial, and consumer electronics that rely heavily on these older-generation chips. Addressing this issue presents complex challenges, especially as direct semiconductor imports from China are relatively low, making tariffs less effective.

Miller outlined several potential responses – including component-level tariffs, targeted bans in sensitive applications like drones and connected cars, and subsidies – but acknowledged that none offers a perfect solution without coordinated action across multiple countries.

"Applying component tariffs at scale would be a logistical nightmare," Miller warned. "If you impose component tariffs by 2030, you'd have time to adjust," Miller suggested, but coalition-building with Europe and Japan remains a hurdle.

China's unrelenting push for semiconductor self-sufficiency

Despite US efforts to contain China's semiconductor rise through export controls, Miller emphasized that Beijing remains committed to achieving technological self-sufficiency under President Xi's leadership.

"I think about President Xi for a variety of reasons. My conclusion, at least, is that he believes that self-sufficient chips are both core economic and security goals," Miller said. "And as a result, China's willing to spend vast resources within the ability of self-sufficiency."

Miller dismissed notions of China as merely a copycat, pointing to its growing self-sufficiency in most manufactured goods. Semiconductors—especially high-end chips and the lithography tools to make them—remain a significant gap, but one that China is determined to close.

Credit: AFP
Credit: AFP

While China faces significant hurdles in advanced technologies like extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography, Miller estimates it could take a decade or more for China to indigenize such capabilities – but its determination shouldn't be underestimated. Public reports of ASML engineers being poached and hacked designs underscore China's resolve in this area.

"If you ask who's willing to sustain expensive policies over the long run, to build an economically inefficient and unprofitable chip industry, the answer is China," Miller said.

Taiwan's AI supply chain crossroad

Beyond traditional semiconductors, Taiwan's role in the emerging AI supply chain remains an open question. "There's no guarantee Taiwanese firms will monetize above the chip level," Miller cautioned, noting that software giants could reap AI's biggest profits.

As Trump pushes for US AI dominance, any increase in chip production costs—whether from tariffs or forced onshoring—could hamper that goal. "If AI hardware costs twice as much, you'll have less of it," Miller pointed out, highlighting a trade-off Washington has yet to fully address.

The chip industry, once a quiet technological backwater, now finds itself at the center of geopolitical competition, with semiconductor politics entering what Miller calls "another level" of intensity.

Two leaders, two strategies

Trump appears focused on building a legacy of trade restructuring and technological supremacy. "He's willing to take economic risks—stock dips, angry CEOs," Miller observed, citing Canada tariffs as evidence. Yet the administration's responsiveness to pushback, like softening auto tariffs after industry outcry, suggests pragmatism beneath the rhetoric.

Credit: AFP
Credit: AFP

Xi, meanwhile, remains steadfast in his pursuit of technological self-sufficiency. "We haven't talked enough about President Xi," Miller admitted, warning of Beijing's opportunism amid Western policy disagreements. China's strategy of driving wedges between the US, Europe, and Japan exploits Trump's unilateral tendencies. "China's policy is set," Miller concluded. "It'll spend vast sums even if its firms stay unprofitable."

Taiwan's path forward

For Taiwan, navigating the next four years will require diplomatic agility and continued technological innovation. TSMC's technological edge and close relationship with the US provide strategic advantages, but increasing tariffs, export controls, and security demands present significant challenges.

Miller's advice is to focus less on political rhetoric and more on structural forces shaping the industry. "If you look back at the first Trump term, the key lesson is that structure matters more than tweets or headlines," he said. "Understanding those forces will be a better guide to the future."

For Taiwan, maintaining its technological edge, diversifying its AI ecosystem, and navigating US demands with strategic pragmatism will be essential as the global semiconductor landscape continues to evolve. As Miller concluded, "Managing this politicization will be a real challenge," leaving Taiwan to navigate a world where semiconductors power not just devices, but global geopolitical competition itself.
