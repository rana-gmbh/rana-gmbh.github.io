Following the Trump administration's export controls on Nvidia's H20 AI chip for China, there's speculation that Chinese AI chip manufacturers might fill the gap. Although China's long-term prospects for domestic AI chip production look promising, the short-term scenario appears more complicated and challenging.

China has been actively developing domestic AI chips and enhancing AI data center sales, resulting in rapid growth in AI data center construction. However, these centers face issues of underutilization and inefficiency. According to Securities Daily, Beijing's AI data centers have expanded significantly since 2023, with growth continuing through the first half of 2024. Research from CCID shows that by mid-2024, more than 250 AI data centers will be either completed or under construction nationwide. This expansion was partially driven by preparations for increased inference demands anticipated with the upcoming launch of the DeepSeek AI model in early 2025.

Despite significant investment in AI data centers driven by Beijing's policy incentives, questions remain about their effectiveness. Many local governments have invested heavily without thorough demand assessments, leading to reports of low utilization and idle computing equipment.

Leiphone, a Chinese tech news platform, reports that the China Academy of Information and Communications Technology (CAICT) found the average computing power usage in China's AI data centers to be only 32%. Some industry experts believe it could be even less, dipping below 30%. Operators of multiple AI centers call it a paradox: despite a bustling market, thousands of centers face challenges in utilizing their computational resources fully.

China's domestic AI chips face significant inefficiencies, primarily due to their limited capabilities. A data center operator reports that these chips are mostly suitable for personal use, lacking the necessary stability and power for commercial applications. Technical issues, like server crashes from single errors while running large language models, result in frequent service interruptions.

China's push for AI self-reliance complicated by deals and inflated tactics

Beijing is pushing for locally produced AI hardware, with Xinhua News Agency noting that China Mobile's AI data centers have over 90% of their chips sourced domestically across eight different models. Despite this, industry insiders suggest a more complex situation. Many local chip providers are proposing assistance in selling computing power to win AI data center contracts and often contribute 10 to 20% of the capital needed for these centers, entangling their finances with those of the infrastructure operators.

This arrangement often leads to unmet promises and contractual disputes, with domestic chip providers failing to honor sales commitments and AI data centers delaying payments. These problems underscore the challenges within the rapidly changing and heavily subsidized domestic AI technology market.

AI chip startups gearing up for initial public offerings (IPOs) are under pressure to showcase strong product performance and secure substantial orders while also preparing for large-scale computational clusters. This IPO frenzy has led to questionable business practices aimed at boosting revenues and securing contracts. One chip manufacturer reportedly worked with LLM vendors and labs, investing around CNY100 million (US$13.70 million) to generate revenues of CNY30-40 million (US$4.11-6.70 million), thereby inflating earnings to hit financial targets.

Industry insiders note that certain chip companies promise guaranteed usage rates to AI data centers in order agreements, but retract this support after a six-month period. This leads to significant declines in computing power utilization, jeopardizing the long-term viability of these projects.

China is pushing for domestic AI chip development, which is marked by quick infrastructure growth but hampered by operational inefficiencies and performance issues. It's uncertain if the sector can overcome these challenges to meet Beijing's goals for technological self-reliance and market competitiveness.
