China-based start-up Butterfly Effect claims to have launched the world's first General AI agent, Manus. The company aims to capitalize on the momentum of "Chinese-branded AI" following DeepSeek's success. However, industry response over the past week has been muted, as experts believe the startup lacks the crucial components needed to outshine its predecessor.

Beyond the marketing hype, a fundamental question remains: Why does the market need the most advanced AI, and what do both sides of the US-China tech rivalry hope to achieve?

The assertion of being "the first General AI agent" has drawn skepticism from industry observers. Critics note that AI agents are not novel innovations, with many suggesting Manus appears to be a repackaged function that OpenAI or Google could easily replicate—offering clear application scenarios like résumé screening and stock analysis.

Furthermore, Butterfly Effect hasn't demonstrated any foundational breakthroughs. Instead, it merely layers existing AI models in a "shell-style" application. Access remains invitation-only, and the team openly acknowledges relying on open-source models, with plans to release a "post-training" version. Whether this approach leads to actual innovation remains uncertain.

In contrast, DeepSeek's traction stems from software algorithm breakthroughs, recognition from AI experts even in the US, and its commitment to open-source development. Yet, its DeepSeek-R1 model only matches OpenAI's o1 without surpassing it, with its competitive advantage mainly in cost efficiency and accessibility.

Manus' tepid reception reflects China's eagerness to establish a national AI champion amid the broader market's appetite for stronger AI. Chinese and US firms alike frequently tout claims of "surpassing OpenAI" or "building the strongest model," but few withstand scrutiny.

Why the strongest matters

For Chinese companies, developing the world's strongest AI carries political weight—signifying a break from US tech dominance and a long-term bid to lead in AI and computing. For American firms, it's primarily about commercial advantage, mirroring past platform battles in PCs and mobile.

Measuring real capability

As AI agents proliferate, the market lacks a clear benchmark for measuring their automation capabilities. Self-reported scores are unreliable, as companies don't disclose failures, and AI models have absorbed all existing benchmarks. User feedback and expert reviews remain the best indicators, despite credibility challenges.

Beyond the competition

The obsession with the strongest AI underscores another reality—no truly transformative AI benefits everyone yet. OpenAI's ChatGPT leads in adoption, but claims of achieving Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) still face skepticism.

While companies will continue vying for the AI crown, users—whether consumers or enterprises—should focus on whether these tools solve real problems. Recent trends suggest breakthroughs may come not from a singularly powerful AI but from integrating multiple models and optimizing them for platforms like PCs and mobile devices. That could be the real game-changer.
