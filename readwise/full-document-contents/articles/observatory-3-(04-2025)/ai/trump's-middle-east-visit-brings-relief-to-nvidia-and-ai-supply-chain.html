Industry insiders revealed that just as the market believed the rapid growth of AI was slowing down and Nvidia's performance would no longer be spectacular, US President Donald Trump demonstrated a dual strategy of "carrot and stick" by leading several top American business leaders, including Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, on a visit to Saudi Arabia. The move proved highly beneficial for Huang.

Nvidia secured a five-year contract to supply hundreds of thousands of AI GPUs to Humain. Meanwhile, the Trump administration announced it would revoke the chip export tier-3 control plan introduced during Joe Biden's tenure. It also appeared to heed Huang's warnings about the growing threat posed by Huawei, further tightening global bans on using Huawei Ascend AI chips, which has significantly reduced China's shipments.

Supported by substantial funding and accelerated AI capabilities from multiple Middle Eastern countries placing large orders, Nvidia and its upstream and downstream AI supply chains are seeing clouds clearing for the short term, with demand forecasts for AI products expected to be revised upward.

Nvidia's rapid rise and the US-China geopolitical drag

Since 2023, Nvidia's operations have surged dramatically, dominating the AI GPU market almost exclusively. However, escalating US-China tensions have hit Nvidia hard despite its strong profits over the past two years.

In addition to the US government tightening AI chip export controls citing national security concerns, Biden's final days in office saw the release of tier-3 export controls. After Trump took office, challenges intensified with immediate tariff wars, strict rules on origin labeling, aggressive enforcement of "Made in America" goals, and additional restrictions on H20 chip sales to China.

Supply chain sources noted that Nvidia is increasingly blocked both overtly and covertly from supplying AI GPUs to China, resulting in significant shipment declines there. Closely linked AI server suppliers have had to urgently relocate production bases.

Nvidia was forced to announce plans to invest US$50 billion over four years alongside TSMC, Foxconn, and Wistron to build AI infrastructure in the US, forcing the entire supply chain to bear the higher costs of domestic manufacturing.

Huang has repeatedly called on the US government to carefully consider these policies and has participated in meetings with both US and Chinese officials seeking common ground to minimize losses.

Middle East diplomacy yields game-changing contracts

Unexpectedly, Trump's unpredictable approach included a recent 90-day truce in US-China negotiations accompanied by major tariff reductions. He then led a grand delegation including Huang, Tesla's Elon Musk, and AMD's Lisa Su on a Middle East tour, where Huang secured the massive Humain order for AI GPUs.

Humain, backed by government and sovereign wealth funds, unveiled an ambitious AI plan to collaborate with Nvidia to build an AI factory deploying hundreds of thousands of Nvidia's most advanced GPUs within five years. The first phase will feature an AI supercomputer composed of 18,000 GB300 Grace Blackwell chips and InfiniBand networking technology.

Suppliers believe Trump's combination of pressure and incentives has raised Nvidia's production costs while sharply reducing shipments to China, impacting profitability. Yet, he also offered opportunities, demonstrating his ability to manage AI supply and demand.

Nvidia can now expand orders outside China, particularly in the Middle East, partially offsetting lost revenue. Additionally, the Trump administration's global ban on Huawei AI chips has somewhat balanced Nvidia's position amid declining Chinese shipments.

Nvidia and US tech firms caught in tariff crossfire

With Trump's shifting stance, Nvidia and other US companies along with their supply chains have become pawns in his tariff-driven negotiation tactics.

Over the past month of chaos, Trump achieved intimidation effects but recognized that antagonistic tariff policies and AI restrictions were harming US interests.

Thus, the 90-day ceasefire and tariff cuts serve as temporary measures, with AI export relaxations aimed at appeasing disgruntled US industries. Prominent figures like Huang have warned that restricting AI chips and technologies could trigger long-term negative consequences, prompting Trump to offer concessions and regain support from major corporations.

The US's tightened AI restrictions on China will likely have lasting impacts on Nvidia, including reduced profits and shipments. Huang has consistently highlighted that such policies may accelerate the rise of Chinese local players like Huawei, posing serious threats.

Trump's alignment with Huang's views by blocking Huawei AI chip exports overseas provides Nvidia some breathing room.

Cautious optimism in the AI supply chain

For the AI supply chain, rising Nvidia orders outside China combined with sustained capital expenditures from cloud service providers suggest a positive revision of supply-demand outlooks.

Market rumors of Nvidia cutting shipments over the next three years have complicated TSMC's advanced packaging capacity planning, but this situation is expected to improve, stabilizing expansion plans.
