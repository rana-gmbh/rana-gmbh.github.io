In the prior article, we compared the evolution of the Internet to that of today's large language models (LLMs), particularly examining Internet packets and LLM tokens. We noted parallels in infrastructure and business model development. By analyzing packets' evolution, we aimed to forecast the future trajectory of tokens in AI, projecting potential impacts on product design, service models, and industry value chains.

Another instructive parallel is the innovation and transformation sparked by applications (Apps) during the mobile network era. Today, propelled by multimodal large vision models (LVMs) and LLMs, an application ecosystem centered around "agents" is gradually taking shape. Building on our earlier discussion, we explore how the rise of apps can illuminate potential paths for agents and edge AI going forward.

Mobile application revolution

Starting in 2007, mobile apps enabled real-time interaction and high-speed data exchange, fueling a surge in mobile users. This growth was driven by declining costs of packet transmission over networks, alongside advances in smartphone computing power and battery life. The emergence of the App Store transformed previously fragmented and complex web interactions into graphical, user-friendly interfaces, significantly lowering barriers to use and making digital services truly mobile and ubiquitous.

Applications evolved beyond single-function tools; through features like push notifications, location services, and social integration, they became deeply embedded in users' daily lives. This shift also triggered a "long tail effect"—not only a few viral hits but countless niche apps meeting diverse and personalized needs.

This transition from web pages to applications was more than just an interface upgrade—it gave rise to a new application-driven ecosystem that revolutionized digital service production and distribution models while rapidly boosting demand for mobile devices, peaking in 2017. The App Store established a two-sided market mechanism connecting developer innovation with global users, enabling previously unscalable services to become commoditized, localized, and globalized.

Emerging digital services such as Uber Technologies, LINE, and Instagram all relied heavily on this app ecosystem. The application boom also stimulated hardware demand across chips, sensors, modules, batteries, and memory, reshaping the mobile device supply chain and fostering the rise of new brands and ODM/OEM manufacturers.

According to Statista, global application downloads grew from 6 billion in 2010 to 143 billion in 2023, reflecting the powerful economies of scale and network effects behind the application model. Taiwanese companies actively participated throughout this mobile wave—from IC foundry, packaging & testing, and system integration to application development—building a comprehensive supply chain and ecosystem network.

This journey not only restructured the mobile industry but also offered valuable lessons for the emerging AI agent model. As user interfaces evolve from apps to agents, can we anticipate key advantages in understanding user needs, computing architectures, standards setting, and hardware integration?

If applications were the primary user interface of the mobile network era, then agents powered by large language and vision models may become the central entry point in the AI era. Agents do more than understand natural language and various sensor inputs—they must possess task planning and execution capabilities, upgrading from simple conversation partners to full-fledged digital assistants.

Through multimodal reasoning and toolchain integration, agent applications are rapidly expanding into automated workflows, professional consulting, education and training, and knowledge assistance.

Rise of agent stores

In the foreseeable future, we will likely see the emergence of "Agent Stores," ecosystems akin to the App Store, aggregating diverse, composable intelligent modules to meet varied demands. This will accelerate the division and integration of hardware and software, promoting the deployment of smart agents across vertical sectors such as industrial, medical, small and medium enterprises, and consumer markets.

Recent rapid advancements in efficient inference models and the flourishing open-source LLM ecosystem further propel these possibilities. Meanwhile, actual end-market demands are surfacing—for example, knowledge management and automation in SMEs, and real-time inference capabilities in industrial control—addressing tangible market needs.

Hardware specifications of endpoint devices are increasingly capable of supporting the AI computing power and memory requirements needed for agents. With fast progress in open-source LLM communities, models at the 13-billion-parameter level can now run smoothly on typical mobile devices, and token generation speeds are approaching practical application thresholds.

The barrier to Edge AI implementation is rapidly falling. Market forecasts predict the Edge AI chip sector will grow from US$2.4 billion in 2023 to US$25.2 billion in 2033, representing a compound annual growth rate of 26.5%.

Major system and chip vendors are actively investing in AI smartphones, automotive SoCs, and AI PC platforms. In the future, agents will naturally embed themselves within diverse endpoints including smartphones, laptops, AR glasses, true wireless stereo (TWS) earbuds, and robots, becoming the next-generation interface for language interaction and task-oriented operations.

Of course, the widespread adoption of agent technology faces many challenges. Beyond user data authorization and usage concerns, increasing autonomy raises significant issues related to security, privacy, regulation, and ethics. The technical complexity itself should not be underestimated.

Interestingly, these intertwined challenges and opportunities mirror the early days of the mobile network era in 2007—where application models, ecosystems, hardware demands, and supply chain structures were all poised for transformative change. The development of agents signals a familiar yet subtle message, heralding the start of another major paradigm shift in the industry.

About the author

Winston Hsu is a Professor at National Taiwan University's Department of Computer Science and Information Engineering, specializing in visual recognition, machine intelligence, large-scale image/video retrieval, and robot learning. He served as CTO and vice president of MobileDrive, a FIH/Foxconn-Stellantis joint venture, from 2021 to 2024 and co-founded the AI/IoT startup thingnario, focused on renewable energy. Hsu leads the MiRA research group and co-leads the Communication and Multimedia Lab.

With a Ph.D. from Columbia University (2007), he has held roles at Microsoft Research and IBM Watson Research Center, contributing to the first AI-produced movie trailer, and founded the first Nvidia AI Lab in Asia at NTU. He has received multiple awards in multimedia and computer vision, was a founding engineer at CyberLink Corp, and serves as Associate Editor for IEEE journals. Recognized among the "World's Top 2% Scientists" in 2023, his current research centers on Embodied AI, combining perception, action, and learning for automotive and robotics applications through collaborations with global tech firms.
