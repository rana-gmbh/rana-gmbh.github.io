Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang recently announced the company's fourth-quarter fiscal 2025 (4QFY25) financial results, reinforcing investor confidence in AI's long-term growth, even amid industry shifts sparked by DeepSeek's emergence.

At Stanford Graduate School of Business's View From The Top with Lisa Su: Chair and CEO of AMD—one of Nvidia's fiercest rivals in the AI GPU market—Su outlined AMD's strategic vision for AI, offering a counterpoint to Nvidia's dominance.

The power of open-source in AI innovation

Lisa Su argued that DeepSeek's significance isn't in its low cost but in its open-source approach, which integrates advancements from multiple contributors to drive AI forward. The industry has been asking one key question: How can DeepSeek be used? This question highlights the broader potential of open-source innovation.

Su reinforced that open-source is core to AMD's identity. Unlike Nvidia's tightly controlled, vertically integrated ecosystem, AMD embraces open-source software—a fundamental differentiator in the evolving AI market.

Navigating opportunities and challenges in AI

Looking back at the semiconductor industry's rapid transformation, Su highlighted AMD's remarkable resurgence. When she took over as CEO in 2014, AMD was on the verge of collapse. A decade later, the company's market cap has surpassed US$300 billion, earning her Time magazine's CEO of the Year 2024 title.

AI is redefining the global tech landscape, creating both opportunities and challenges for AMD. Su called this one of the most exhilarating times in technology, citing AI's explosive adoption over the past 18 months—an acceleration unseen in over 50 years. AI is expanding beyond cloud computing, reaching edge devices and everyday consumer electronics like smartphones.

AI models vary widely—large and small, open-source and proprietary—each requiring tailored computing power. Rather than concentrating solely on AI training or inference, AMD is pursuing a broader AI strategy designed to address diverse computational needs.

Competing against Nvidia's vertical integration

AMD is often compared to Android, while Nvidia is likened to Apple—an analogy that underscores AMD's open, flexible approach versus Nvidia's tightly integrated ecosystem. The challenge for AMD is persuading customers that flexibility outweighs Nvidia's seamless end-to-end integration.

Su recognized Nvidia's ability to deliver a complete AI stack but countered that no single AI solution can monopolize the industry.

AMD's open-source strategy prioritizes interoperability and adaptability—an approach that demands more effort but cultivates a larger developer ecosystem. DeepSeek's swift integration with AMD's ROCm platform underscores how open-source initiatives can drive AI innovation. AMD aims to make AI development simpler and more accessible.

Steadying the ship in AI competition

An audience member asked Su whether AMD intends to challenge Nvidia's grip on AI training hardware—and, if so, when.

Su dismissed the notion of technological dominance, emphasizing that AI training, inference, and reinforcement learning operate along a continuum with shared technological foundations. AMD is already supporting AI training workloads and will expand its role further.

Instead of racing to claim AI hardware dominance, AMD is charting a measured, strategic course.

DeepSeek's rise has ignited debate over AI's trajectory. While some responses across the industry have been cautious, Su sees its long-term potential in driving down AI infrastructure costs and broadening accessibility.

AI adoption is still in its infancy. Despite today's breakthroughs, they barely scratch the surface of what's to come. With Moore's Law decelerating, computing demand will only intensify, favoring open ecosystems over closed solutions. AMD is positioning itself to capitalize on this shift with AI-focused hardware and software.

AMD key milestones

● 2006: AMD acquires ATI, laying the foundation for its AI GPU business and positioning itself as a challenger to Nvidia.

● 2008: AMD officially splits into two firms—one focused on chip design and the other on manufacturing.

● 2009: AMD and Abu Dhabi's ATIC establish GlobalFoundries (GF) as a joint venture.

● 2011: AMD's stock price falls below US$5; the company sells off its remaining stake in GF, fully exiting the fab business.

● 2012: Lisa Su joins AMD as senior vice president and general manager; at the time, AMD's stock price hovers around $3.

● 2014: Lisa Su is appointed CEO of AMD.

● 2023: The rise of generative AI strengthens AMD's HPC market positioning, a key focus for Lisa Su.

● 2024: Riding the AI boom, AMD's market capitalization surpasses US$300 billion for the first time.

● 2025: The emergence of DeepSeek sparks industry shifts; Jensen Huang and Lisa Su express confidence in the continued growth of AI computing demand.
