The European Commission has launched the "AI Continent Action Plan" to compete with the US and China in AI. This initiative aims to enhance AI data and computing infrastructure while easing business regulations and transforming Europe's talent and industries into AI innovation leaders. Key elements of the plan were presented by EC President Ursula von der Leyen at the Paris AI Action Summit in February 2025.

Expanding AI infrastructure

The EU plans to develop a network of AI gigafactories, significantly expanding existing AI infrastructure. Each gigafactory will house around 100,000 advanced AI chips, quadrupling current capacities.

Massive investment commitment

In February 2025, the EU pledged to boost public and private investments in the AI sector with a total target of EUR200 billion (US$219.03 billion). Over 20 investors have already committed EUR150 billion (US$164.28 billion) for the next five years. Furthermore, the EU will create a EUR20 billion fund to construct up to five AI gigafactories.

Enhancing cloud capacity and data access

The EU plans to introduce the Cloud and AI Development Act to boost private investment in cloud capacity and data centers, with the goal of doubling European data center capacity in five to seven years, focusing on sustainability. It also aims to establish data labs to gather extensive data within AI factories, enhancing startups' access to quality training data while respecting privacy laws.

Regulatory support

Additionally, an AI Act Service Desk will be established to help businesses comply with the AI Act, offering guidance to the public, investors, and companies.

Balancing innovation and regulation

Key priorities in the EU include boosting AI skills, developing algorithms, and encouraging AI use in strategic sectors. However, tech companies have criticized EU regulations as overly burdensome, stifling innovation.

CNBC reported that at the Paris AI Summit, Chris Lehane, Global Affairs Chief at OpenAI, highlighted concerns that European leaders fear missing AI development opportunities, urging regulators to shift focus away from technological risks. Additionally, US Vice President JD Vance has critiqued Europe's cautious stance on AI regulation.
