Alibaba's Qwen artificial intelligence models are making significant inroads in Japan, driven by their open-source framework that encourages customization and rapid innovation, according to a report by Nikkei. One standout example is Tokyo-based startup ABEJA, which on April 17 unveiled its QwQ-32B Reasoning Model—built on Qwen's architecture. The model praised for its advanced chain-of-thought logical reasoning, has already demonstrated impressive performance, underscoring Qwen's growing influence in Japan's AI landscape.

Qwen achieves top global ranking

A new performance review by Nikkei's Digital Governance has placed Alibaba Cloud's Qwen2.5-Max in sixth place out of 113 leading AI models from the US, China, and Japan. The top five spots were dominated by American giants, including OpenAI's o1 and o1-preview, Anthropic's Claude 3.7 Sonnet Thinking and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, as well as OpenAI's GPT-4.5-preview.

Outperforming Google and Meta

Alibaba's Qwen isn't just gaining ground—it's outpacing some of Silicon Valley's biggest names. The open-source "Qwen2.5-32B" model landed in 26th place in global rankings, beating Google's "Gemma-3-27B" at 32nd and Meta's "Llama-3-70B-Instruct" at 57th.

Open-source models like Qwen enable enterprises to train and customize AI systems, driving diversity and innovation in AI applications. In fact, "ABEJA-Qwen2.5-32B" is now the top-ranked model among Japanese companies and 21st overall.

Widespread adoption among Japanese startups

Qwen's influence is expanding rapidly across Japan's tech landscape. University of Tokyo spin-off ELYZA launched a new Qwen-based model on May 1, while other startups such as Lightblue and Axcxept have also adopted Qwen technology. Analysts at Nomura Research Institute highlight Qwen's ability to maintain high accuracy with smaller datasets, making it particularly effective for Japanese language processing.

Globally, more than 100,000 Qwen-derived models have been shared on Hugging Face, and Alibaba Cloud is offering localized, secure deployments for Japanese businesses. Alibaba Cloud plans to introduce over 1,000 Qwen-powered applications in Japan within three years, leveraging Alibaba's proven success in China. The company is actively engaging with local partners to accelerate adoption.

Qwen3 sets new global benchmark

Alibaba is making waves in the global AI race with the debut of Qwen3, its most powerful open-source model yet. Unveiled on April 29, Qwen3 is trained on a staggering 36 trillion tokens—twice the volume of its predecessor, Qwen2.5—and supports 119 languages and dialects. It's also the company's first hybrid reasoning model, designed to tackle complex tasks while swiftly handling simpler ones.

According to the latest LiveBench rankings, Qwen3 has dethroned DeepSeek's R1 to become the world's top open-source AI model—a title R1 had held since January.

But Qwen3 isn't just dominating the open-source space. In performance tests, it outpaced top-tier closed-source models, including OpenAI's o3 High and o4-Mini High, as well as Google's Gemini 2.5 Pro, according to the South China Morning Post. LiveBench, widely regarded as the AI industry's first "cheat-proof" benchmark, validated Qwen3's superiority in instruction-following and problem-solving. Moreover, Qwen3 offers these advanced features at a substantially lower cost, priced at just CNY4(US$0.55) per million tokens, compared to OpenAI's charge of US$10, enhancing its accessibility alongside its performance.

China's open-source AI sector, led by Alibaba's Qwen and DeepSeek, is rapidly closing the gap with US giants. Its collaborative, open-source model accelerates innovation and industry adoption, marking a new chapter in global AI rivalry.
