China's rapid expansion of domestic memory chip production is creating market volatility and forcing established players to adjust their strategies amid already challenging geopolitical tensions, according to semiconductor industry veteran Marco Mezger.

"They scaled up from virtually zero to producing 300,000 wafers per month," said Mezger, Executive VP and COO at Neumonda, referring to China's Changxin Memory Technologies (CXMT). The Hefei-based company is projected to reach production levels rivaling US-based Micron Technology by 2025, despite being virtually unknown just six years ago.


Credit: DIGITIMES

While US sanctions prevent CXMT from selling overseas, its DRAM production is being absorbed by China's massive domestic market, squeezing out global giants. "That adds volatility elsewhere. If Samsung or Micron can't sell in China, they flood other markets. Prices fall. The market destabilizes," Mezger explained.

The rise of a Chinese national champion in an industry long dominated by a South Korea–US triopoly also hints at broader shifts. "This was an oligopoly. Now it's fragmenting. China made it happen by focusing national resources on one player."

Trillion-dollar trajectory despite volatility

Despite these disruptions, the semiconductor industry remains on an unprecedented growth trajectory. Global semiconductor revenue, which took 60 years to reach US$500 billion, is projected to double to US$1 trillion within just the next decade. "There's no other industry right now with this kind of growth trajectory," Mezger said.


Source: TechInsights, McKinsey, SEMI.org

Some analysts forecast a further doubling between 2030 and 2040, largely fueled by AI demand, he added.

But beneath the growth lies complexity. Mezger, who has three decades of experience in memory technology, emphasized the sector's structural volatility. "Memory is one of the most cyclical parts of the semiconductor industry," he said, citing US$30 billion in combined losses from DRAM makers in 2023 alone. "We used to talk about US$500 million losses per quarter, bankrupting a company. Now, the numbers are magnitudes higher—but the stakes are also national."

AI-driven memory boom creating market imbalance

The exploding demand for high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips used in AI applications is reshaping the economics of the memory sector. HBM accounted for 17% of memory industry revenue in 2024 despite representing only 4% of production volume, a share that Mezger projects will rise to 42% by 2027 on just 10% of output.

"The yield is poor—around 70%—but customers are paying the price. And when it's a seller's market, manufacturers don't feel the loss," Mezger said. This premium market is helping memory makers recover from staggering losses.

However, this shift is creating an uneven playing field. "For one person, it is good. For other people, it is bad," he noted, explaining that industrial and automotive sectors using older process nodes face supply challenges as manufacturers prioritize more profitable AI-related products.

"Legacy nodes are becoming less attractive. Big players are sunsetting products earlier. That creates opportunities for smaller Taiwanese companies—Nanya, Winbond, Powerchip—to grab niche industrial and automotive markets," Mezger added.


Source: Memphis Market Intelligence

'Second wave' of AI could broaden ecosystem

Looking ahead, Mezger predicts a "second wave of AI" focused on edge computing that could diversify the ecosystem beyond the current dominant players.

"Edge AI, when you work with sensors, when you process data on the edge, it's a huge market. They don't need an Nvidia chip," he said, suggesting specialized AI silicon designed for power efficiency will create new opportunities. "Not everyone needs an Nvidia chip. For edge applications—sensors, low-power systems—you need specialized AI silicon."

This shift could benefit smaller players and open space for innovation, potentially including European companies that have largely missed the first wave of AI development. "There's a big party going on—and some are just watching through the window," Mezger noted, pointing out how the continent remains focused on industrial and automotive sectors that remain weak. "AI is dominated by US hyperscalers, Nvidia, and TSMC. Europe has to find its own path."

Emerging memory and the next frontier

Mezger also emphasized the potential of emerging memory technologies like FerroRAM, which offers non-volatility at DRAM-like speeds. "Flash is too slow for many next-gen applications. FerroRAM has been around, but we're seeing serious movement now."

He announced Neumonda's strategic partnership with Dresden-based Ferroelectric Memory Company to accelerate commercialization. "Our goal is to bring memory design and production capabilities back to Europe—especially Saxony," Mezger said, referring to Germany's budding chip cluster.
