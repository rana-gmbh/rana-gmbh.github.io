Following the US government's export control of Nvidia's China-specific H20 chips, rumors have surfaced that the flagship gaming graphics card RTX 5090D, also tailored for the Chinese market, may face sales restrictions. Although no official announcement has been made, insiders reveal that Nvidia has notified all AIC graphics card manufacturers about suspending supplies of the RTX 5090D, though this does not equate to an outright ban on sales.

Whether the RTX 5090D will follow the same path as the H20 chip remains uncertain. However, AI data centers in China holding large inventories of the China-specific Nvidia 4090 are reportedly engaging in "reselling cards from inventory." They dismantle and refurbish these cards before selling them as new units, generating multiple times the profit.

Refurbishing and monetizing existing stock

The China-specific 48GB RTX 4090 is widely circulating in the Chinese market, with prices nearly doubling. On JD.com, its price ranges between CNY20,000 and CNY40,000 (US$2,735 to US$5,470).

Due to the scarcity of RTX 5090D stock, Chinese AI data centers with substantial Nvidia 4090 chip inventories are modifying and refurbishing these cards to monetize them in the market.

Leiphone, citing the head of shared computing resource platform Berlin Cloud, stated that although the purchase price of the 4090 has almost doubled, rental supply remains sufficient and rental costs have not increased. Compared to renting out computing power with a payback period of three to five years, directly selling cards to earn double profits makes the 4090 a lifeline for data centers.

Industry experts pointed out that China's 4090 models are primarily consumer-grade fan-cooled cards. When deployed in AI data centers, they must first be converted into blower-style cards, which offer better centralized cooling than fan-cooled versions. Therefore, most 4090 compute cards entering the secondary market undergo modification.

Idle computing power drives card resale

Beyond profitability, the main reason Chinese AI data centers dismantle and resell graphics cards stems from severe "idle computing power" issues.

Fundamentally, two key metrics define AI data center efficiency: "rack rate" and "activation rate." Rack rate refers to equipment installed, racked, and powered on; activation rate measures whether the equipment is actively executing tasks.

Ya-Dong Jin, AI lead at iSoftStone, explained that if an AI data center's rack rate falls below 70 to 75 percent, it cannot turn a profit. Currently, activation rates remain under 20 percent.

Moreover, low utilization of actual computational tasks results in massive idle capacity and challenges in recouping investments. Consequently, "selling cards to recover cash" has become an open secret within the industry.

Call for maturity in a growing sector

Insiders disclose that many AI data centers' order revenues fail to cover bank loan interest payments. Orders from other large language model (LLM) operators cause even greater losses, leaving AI data centers unprofitable despite ongoing transactions.

While subsidies from the Chinese government serve as the backbone supporting the country's AI computing industry, these incentives have attracted opportunists who disrupt the normal development of AI data centers.

Jin noted that such subsidy dynamics resemble those seen in electric vehicles (EVs), where initial support targets the supply side. Once supply reaches adequate scale, demand-side subsidies follow, culminating in market-driven selection that identifies the most competitive players on both sides.

However, how much longer the "growing pains" caused by the chaotic expansion and card-resale phenomenon in China's AI data centers will last remains unclear. The sector urgently needs downstream AI applications to take off in order to realize true benefits.
