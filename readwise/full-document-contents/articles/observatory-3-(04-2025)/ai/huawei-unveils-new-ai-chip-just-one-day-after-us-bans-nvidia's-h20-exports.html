The US Department of Commerce (DOC) recently notified Nvidia that future AI chips H20 intended for the Chinese market will require an "indefinite license" for export to Hong Kong, Macau, and mainland China, effectively amounting to a sales ban. This sudden move raises concerns regarding whether local Chinese chip manufacturers have alternative solutions ready. In fact, Huawei seems to have already sensed unfavorable changes in the wind.

After Nvidia received notification from the US on April 9, Huawei announced technological advancements for its next-generation AI chip, Ascend 920, at its partner conference on April 10. The timing of this announcement leads one to speculate whether Huawei had foreseen the imminent end of the H20 "special supply era" and was preparing to step up.

H20 export restrictions prompt Huawei's contingency plans

According to sources within China's semiconductor industry, the Ascend 920, as the successor to the Ascend 910C, is reportedly expected to enter mass production in the second half of 2025. Industry experts are optimistic that it will fill the market gap left by H20, especially given the constraints on AI training chip supplies, making it a key player in advancing China's domestic AI computing capabilities.

Chinese industry insiders noted that during the Huawei partner conference, the company also unveiled its new generation CloudMatrix 384 ultra-node solution.

This system centers around the Ascend 910C, boasting a single card performance of up to 781.25 TFLOPS, paired with eight HBM2e memory modules, achieving a memory bandwidth of 3200 GB/s, supporting high-speed transmission and large-scale parallel computing. Although limited by packaging processes, its GPU interconnect bandwidth remains at 400 GB/s, optimized for efficient communication between ultra-nodes.

Huawei's AI chips undergo comprehensive upgrades

To overcome US restrictions on advanced processes and EDA tools, Huawei has opted for a system innovation approach, utilizing a chiplet design to combine two integrated training chips into a single dedicated training chip, achieving efficient computation under 7nm process conditions.

However, this chip is manufactured by SMIC, which has yet to disclose any information regarding this collaboration.

The Ascend series continues its dual strategy of "integrated training + pure training": the former, such as the 910A and 910B, caters to general scenarios, while the latter, like the 910C, focuses on large deep-learning tasks. As an upgraded version of the 910C, the Ascend 920 is expected to adopt a 6nm process, increasing transistor density by over 30% and reducing power consumption by approximately 15%.

Specifications include a single card performance exceeding 900 TFLOPS, enhanced memory bandwidth of 4,000 GB/s, and an upgrade to HBM3 modules, catering to larger and more complex model training needs.

The pure training version 920C will retain the chiplet design and enhance tensor operation accelerators, optimizing for Transformer and MoE models, with overall training efficiency expected to improve by 30-40% compared to the 910C. Furthermore, it will support PCIe 5.0 and the next generation of high-throughput interconnect protocols, further optimizing resource scheduling and cross-node collaboration.

US-China AI chip competition enters confrontational phase

Coinciding with Huawei's significant display of technical prowess is the dramatic policy shift from the Trump administration.

Just recently, Trump's camp verbally permitted H20 exports to China, but this week they made a sharp turn, with the DOC personally ordering the initiation of the "indefinite license" procedure, placing the H20—dubbed the last resort in the "gray zone" of US-China technology exports—at the center of a new wave of bans.

The H20 was originally designed by Nvidia as a downgraded alternative specifically for the Chinese market following the implementation of new US sanctions in October 2023. Now blocked, it is an indication that the US-China chip race has officially transitioned from gray areas to open confrontation.

Faced with the abrupt reversal of Trump's policies, Chinese chip enterprises no longer harbor illusions of retreat, shifting from a passive stance to actively engaging in the battle. The Ascend 920 is viewed as a crucial step towards achieving autonomy and control in China's AI computing supply chain.
