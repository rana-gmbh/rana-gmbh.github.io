Alibaba's fourth-quarter results for fiscal 2025 missed market expectations, sending shares down 7.5% in premarket trading. Revenue rose 7% year over year to CNY236.5 billion (US$32.8 billion), narrowly missing the CNY237.2 billion consensus. While net income jumped 279% to CNY12.4 billion, analysts attributed the surge to non-operational gains, including asset disposals and equity revaluations, underscoring lingering pressure on core business fundamentals.

For the full fiscal year, revenue reached CNY996.3 billion, up 6% from the prior year. Operating profit increased 24% to CNY140.9 billion, and net income attributable to shareholders rose 77% to CNY129.5 billion. However, non-GAAP net profit remained flat at CNY158.1 billion. Operating cash flow dropped 10% to CNY163.5 billion, while free cash flow plunged 53% to CNY73.9 billion, reflecting the company's expanded investments in cloud infrastructure.

AI-driven cloud revenue hits new high

Alibaba Cloud, part of the company's Cloud Intelligence division, posted fourth-quarter revenue of CNY30.1 billion, an 18% increase year over year and an acceleration from the previous quarter. CEO Eddie Wu cited surging AI demand as the primary growth driver, with AI-related cloud usage achieving triple-digit growth for seven consecutive quarters.

Wu noted that industries such as manufacturing, agriculture, and small commodity exporters in Europe are shifting from on-premise setups to Alibaba Cloud. These customers are leveraging the company's GPU computing and IaaS platforms for AI inference workloads and post-training on open-source models tailored to enterprise needs.

Morgan Stanley's Gary Yu highlighted Alibaba Cloud as a key bright spot in the quarter, citing its alignment with broader AI investment trends. UBS analyst Kenneth Fong added that growing post-DeepSeek demand and rising AI infrastructure adoption further reinforced Alibaba Cloud's long-term growth trajectory.

Big bets on AI infrastructure and open models

Beyond revenue gains, Alibaba is ramping up its long-term AI infrastructure. In April 2025, it launched Qwen3, its flagship foundation model, which the company claims performs on par with global peers. Lighter Qwen models are being deployed on smartphones and IoT devices, while large-scale models—32 billion parameters and above—are hosted on Alibaba Cloud to support enterprise-scale applications.

Eddie Wu explained that this hybrid approach helps drive additional demand for Alibaba Cloud's compute resources, as edge deployments often generate complementary backend workloads in the cloud.

Over the next three years, Alibaba plans to invest more than CNY380 billion in expanding its AI infrastructure, including next-generation data centers. Chairman Joe Tsai, however, warned of potential overexuberance in the AI sector and cautioned investors about emerging bubble risks.


Alibaba Chairman Joe Tsai. Credit: Alibaba Group

Cloud monetization gains ground despite early challenges

Turning AI into a scalable cloud business has been a challenge, but Alibaba made notable progress in the fourth quarter. Management cited increased enterprise migration of AI workloads to the cloud, driven by the need for scalability and growing sophistication in model deployment.

Wu noted that many new clients had previously relied on in-house systems for AI inference but are now shifting to Alibaba Cloud for greater flexibility. This is especially crucial in sectors where proprietary datasets demand customized post-training and tight API integration.

While heavy investment in cloud infrastructure pressured EBITA margins, analysts viewed the spending as essential for scaling. Adjusted EBITA rose 36% year over year to CNY32.6 billion in the fourth quarter, despite a sharp drop in free cash flow.

Investor outlook: Cloud and AI in the spotlight

Despite concerns over missed revenue targets, Alibaba's AI and cloud strategy remains on a fast track. UBS and Morgan Stanley reiterated bullish outlooks, citing strong AI revenue growth, continued share buybacks, and a US$2 per ADS special dividend as signals of management's confidence.

As China's digital economy evolves, Alibaba's future hinges on its ability to lead in AI infrastructure, inference services, and enterprise cloud integration. Whether these bets will offset slower commerce growth remains uncertain, but the company is shifting from selling goods to enabling the systems that drive them.
