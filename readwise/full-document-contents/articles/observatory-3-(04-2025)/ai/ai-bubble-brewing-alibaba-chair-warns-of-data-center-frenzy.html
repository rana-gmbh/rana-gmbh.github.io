Alibaba Group Chairman Joe Tsai warned that the ongoing AI data center boom risks creating a market bubble. Speaking at the HSBC Global Investment Summit, he said construction is likely outpacing actual demand for AI services.

Tsai observed that while investments are pouring in, much of the capital is being duplicated. He pointed to a rise in SPAC-funded data center projects launched without signed customers—what he called "ownerless investment" fueling bubble risk, according to Bloomberg and Reuters.

He also flagged aggressive expansions by tech giants and funds in the US and Asia, often proceeding without clear demand. This kind of speculative buildout, he said, risks misallocating industry resources.

He cited Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta, which have pledged US$100 billion, US$75 billion, and US$65 billion respectively toward AI infrastructure by 2025. "People are talking about US$500 billion, even trillions, in AI investment. I don't think that much is necessary," Tsai said, warning that excessive spending could create dangerous overcapacity despite strong interest in AI applications.

Tsai categorized AI companies into three groups: model developers like OpenAI and DeepSeek, infrastructure providers, and hybrids like Alibaba. He added that Alibaba is already using AI to draft its annual report, now 65% automated, according to MoneyDJ.

Warning signs emerge as industry leaders split on AI infrastructure demand

At last week's GTC conference, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang struck a bullish tone, announcing that the company's next-gen Blackwell platform is now in full production. He cited "unprecedented" demand for AI computing and increasing adoption of AI tokens across sectors including transportation, healthcare, and daily life.

But signs of overcapacity are already surfacing. Microsoft has reportedly canceled or deferred data center projects across the US and Europe, amounting to roughly 2 gigawatts of capacity, according to Bloomberg, citing TD Cowen. The pullback includes stepping away from new OpenAI-related business, despite Microsoft's US$13 billion stake in the company. Analysts say the move points to a supply-demand imbalance in AI compute clusters.

TD Cowen also noted that Google and Meta have absorbed some of the vacated leases, but the broader signal is one of strategic restraint. Microsoft confirmed it will slow AI capex in the next fiscal year, shifting focus from new builds to upgrading existing infrastructure.

Supply chain insiders say these developments validate concerns raised by Joe Tsai. While firms like Alibaba, AWS, Microsoft, and Google lease out computing, much of their infrastructure expansion is aimed at supporting internal AI workloads.

In contrast, smaller cloud providers relying solely on rented capacity are more vulnerable to demand fluctuations. Some are already seeing signs of oversupply and underutilization.

AI investment and the economics of "anticipated demand"

DIGITIMES Research Center Deputy Director Jason Tsai echoed the concern, noting that AI companies often invest heavily before demand materializes. This risky "anticipating demand" strategy is key to staying competitive.

DIGITIMES senior analyst Jim Hsiao compared today's cloud infrastructure surge to the early 2000s dot-com era. Capex, he said, is inevitable when big players race to supply compute early. "Demand rises slowly, but investors rush in fast—it's a classic arms race."

Hsiao said US cloud providers are also bracing for long-term enterprise demand, not just current AI models. "This phase is about building capacity before it's needed—a strategic buildup."

Though practical use cases are still evolving, Hsiao pointed to advances in agentic and reasoning models. Multimodal systems, he said, are already gaining traction in animation and film.

Hsiao agreed that a mild bubble exists, but said it's manageable. "Once demand kicks in, it gets absorbed quickly." He added that the 40–50% capex surge among cloud giants is still focused on core infrastructure—power, space, and servers. "It's on track. Scale brings the cost down."

Despite concerns over AI infrastructure overcapacity, OpenAI is doubling down on future-facing investment. In 2024, it expects US$3.7 billion in revenue but nearly US$5 billion in losses due to heavy R&D and compute costs, as WSJ reported. The company also seeks a US$40 billion funding round that could push its valuation to US$300 billion, signaling strong investor faith in its long-term potential.

The US$500 billion Stargate initiative exemplifies this future-first strategy. Backed by OpenAI, SoftBank, and Oracle, the project aims to build advanced AI data centers across the US to power applications in healthcare, education, and enterprise analytics.

However, the project's size and funding demands have raised doubts about its viability—particularly if real-world AI demand doesn't scale as expected.

The rise of open-source models reshapes infrastructure demand

The launch of DeepSeek, a Chinese open-source AI model, has raised concerns about slowing AI infrastructure spending. As open-source models grow more capable, many companies are training or fine-tuning models without costly, high-end hardware—dampening demand for premium AI computing.

Foxconn Chairman Young Liu noted that although the AI server market is still expanding, its structure is shifting. Once led by hyperscale cloud providers, it's now drawing more small and mid-sized enterprises, many of which are buying servers to train in-house models.

China's economic momentum: recovery and renewed confidence

Commenting on China's outlook, Joe Tsai said Alibaba sees signs of a bottom and plans to restart hiring to support growth. He stressed that consumer recovery depends on renewed business confidence and stronger investment in talent.

Tsai noted that business sentiment has improved since President Xi Jinping met with private-sector leaders in January. Alibaba is urging more Chinese firms to invest and expand. With housing prices stabilizing, Tsai is optimistic about domestic demand.

In February, Alibaba pledged to go all-in on AI, committing over CNY380 billion (US$52.36 billion) in investment over the next three years.

AI race heats up: DeepSeek narrows gap with US

Hong Kong outlet The Standard reported that DeepSeek recently launched version 3 of its model, showing major upgrades and signaling its aim to outpace global rivals.

Kai-Fu Lee, CEO of 01.AI, said China has closed the AI gap with the US to just three months in some areas—crediting startups like DeepSeek for optimizing chip use and algorithm design.

US investors are taking note. Analysts cited Microsoft's February decision to cancel some US data center leases as a sign of overheated demand. DeepSeek's rapid rise has intensified doubts about the long-term sustainability of AI infrastructure growth.
