China is making strides in AI chip self-sufficiency with the open-sourcing of Chitu, a large-scale model inference engine developed by Tsinghua University-backed AI startup Qingcheng.AI. The company has also launched a full-scale deployment solution, positioning Chitu as an alternative to Nvidia's Hopper series and a crucial driver in China's evolving AI ecosystem.

Breaking hardware dependence and strengthening China's AI ecosystem

According to reports from 36Kr, Sina, and the South China Morning Post, Chitu is a high-performance inference engine optimized for large language models (LLMs), including DeepSeek R1. It is the first inference engine to natively support FP8 precision models on non-Nvidia Hopper GPUs and various domestic AI chips, a major step in reducing reliance on foreign hardware.

Qingcheng.AI CEO Xiongchao Tang highlighted that China lacks a scalable, production-grade open-source inference solution. Chitu's launch fills this gap, representing a major milestone in China's AI infrastructure development. The company also plans to release optimized versions for domestic AI chips, driving broader industry adoption.

FP8 precision models are becoming the industry standard, largely due to DeepSeek's expanding adoption. However, most FP8 models remain dependent on Nvidia's high-end GPUs, creating challenges for Chinese enterprises facing the US export restrictions and high infrastructure costs.

Chitu eliminates this bottleneck by enabling native FP8 model deployment on non-Hopper GPUs and domestic AI chips, lowering adoption barriers and reducing dependency on foreign hardware.

Performance breakthrough: 3x faster inference, 50% less GPU usage

According to Qingcheng.AI, Chitu delivers a 315% inference speed increase on Nvidia A800 GPUs while cutting GPU usage by 50%, outperforming other open-source architectures. The Economic Daily News reports that this efficiency boost allows enterprises to optimize computing power while reducing hardware costs.

Unlike conventional optimization methods such as quantization, pruning, or distillation, which can compromise model accuracy, Chitu leverages mathematically equivalent transformations at the computational and coding levels to enhance efficiency without performance loss. It is also fully compatible with ComfyUI, a widely used open-source image generation framework, ensuring seamless integration.

Chitu supports leading LLMs, including Alibaba Cloud's Qwen2, Meta's Llama 3, and Kolors AI, as well as 7B, 13B, 34B, 70B, and 130B parameter models. Designed for multi-platform compatibility, it runs on both Nvidia GPUs and China's domestic AI chips.

Industry collaboration and investment fuel AI expansion

Founded in December 2023 by Tsinghua University professor Jidong Zhai, Qingcheng.AI is backed by a team of top-tier AI researchers. It has established strategic partnerships with China's leading AI chip firms, data centers, and LLM developers, including Moore Threads, Enflame Technology, Iluvatar CoreX, and MetaX.

Qingcheng.AI has raised two funding rounds, backed by the Beijing AI Industry Investment Fund, Zhongke Chuangxing Technology Investment Co., and Koala Fund.

As the US-China tech tensions intensify, Washington's export restrictions have blocked Nvidia's most advanced AI chips from being sold to Chinese firms. This has compelled China to seek domestic alternatives. Chitu emerges as a cost-effective, high-performance inference engine, driving China's AI self-sufficiency.

ByteDance's AI research team recently reported a 170% improvement in LLM training efficiency, saving millions of GPU hours through system optimizations.

The open-source release of Chitu represents a major advancement in China's AI inference technology, reducing reliance on foreign high-end GPUs while expanding the adoption of domestic AI chips. As Qingcheng.AI refines Chitu's architecture and deepens industry collaborations, China's AI self-sufficiency is poised to accelerate, reshaping the global AI market landscape.
