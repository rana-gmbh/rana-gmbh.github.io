Key Takeaway

It was widely anticipated that the Mate 70 and/or Mate 70 Pro, the latest series of smartphones released by Huawei, would feature the Kirin 9100 application processor, widely speculated to be manufactured using SMIC’s 5nm process (referred to by some as their N+3 process technology). Adding to the chatter was the expectation that the apps processor was not the 9100, but rather the 9020. This included anticipation that this version of the processor would be manufactured using a SMIC 6nm process, and adding to the confusion was the fact that no one clearly knew what that was supposed to be; it was not disclosed in any technology roadmap from SMIC. TechInsights suggested that if this 6nm process was identified, it would likely be a minimally scaled version of their 7nm (N+2) process.

Our teardown analysis of Mate 70 Pro smartphone and the die analysis presented in TechInsights reports (HiSilicon Kirin 9020 from Mate 70 Pro Plus - Die Analysis, TechInsights Platform) revealed that the apps processor is indeed the Kirin 9020 and manufactured using the same SMIC 7nm (N+2) process, featuring the same key critical dimension parameters as previously observed in the Kirin 9000s (HiSilicon Kirin 9000S ACE-2309-801 TechInsights Platform) and Kirin 9010 (HiSilicon Kirin 9010 DFR-2404-802, TechInsights Platform)—the processors used by the Mate 60 and Pura 70 smartphones, respectively. Details of SMIC 7nm (N+2) process are discussed extensively. Below are some SEM XS and plan-view images of the device that provides a view of the key critical dimensions of this device.

Figure 4, below, shows a compilation of logic gate pitch relative to scaled minimum/effective standard cell height for various process nodes and manufacturers. This clearly demonstrates that SMIC 7nm (N+2) process does indeed belong in the family of 7nm processes. Digging further into the critical dimensions of SMIC 7nm (N+2) process and comparing to TSMC 7nm (N7 and its variants) (as shown in table 1), the SMIC 7nm (N+2) process is relatively underscaled relative to TSMC N7 and variants.

Without further scaling to increase functionality, Huawei/HiSilicon has had no choice but to increase the die size by almost 20%. This trend of increasing die sizes to accommodate additional functionality in the design will eventually reach a practical limit before the reported yield issues with this 7nm (N+2) process makes it impractical to manufacture larger die sizes. This was quite evident in TechInsights’ recent die analysis report ([Huawei

Ascend 910B AI Trainer - Die Analysis](https://library.techinsights.com/hg-asset/23d95da6-7c04-4e9e-a82b-a6702a8757df#moduleName=Search&reportCode=FCT-2410-804&subscriptionId=null&channelId=null&reportName=Huawei+Ascend+910B+AI+Trainer+-+Die+Analysis)) of the Huawei Ascend 910B, an AI processor, with a whopping die size of 664.5 mm2, revealed to have been manufactured by TSMC using N7HPC process as opposed to the expected SMIC 7nm (N+2) process.

There are suggestions that the next version of the Kirin app processor will be manufactured using moderately scaled 6nm version from SMIC. It is not clear what a potential SMIC 6nm process would look like; would it be an optical shrink or would it be an integration improvement in an effort to reduce the mask count for yield improvement. Since SMIC 7 nm (N+2) is underscaled relative to TSMC N7, there is an opportunity to further scale the N+2 process to closer match the TSMC N7 process. Scaling strategy used by TSMC for their N7 and N6 (New Tape-Out and Re-Tape-Out) processes, can provide insight into SMIC’s 6nm scaling options. Following their first gen N7 process, TSMC introduced a number of different design technology co-optimization (DTCO) changes to achieve smart scaling, namely:

a) introduction of single diffusion break (SDB) replacing double diffusion break (DDB) to achieve active gate density improvements without reducing gate pitch,

b) introduction of EUV to pattern some or all minimum pitch metals. This reduced the number of masks used that is either, (i) a cost saving measure, or (ii) resulted in scaled minimum metal pitch, particularly metal 1 pitch that enabled the increase of gate: metal 1 pitch gear ratio from 1.0 to 1.5. This increased gear ratio provided additional routing resources at metal 1 and necessitated a redesigned standard cell library and place and route.

Critical Implications

Scaling gate pitch without degrading transistor performance is a challenge for most foundries and it will be no different for SMIC, particularly after their first attempt to scale a 7nm process from (N+1) to (N+2) resulted in a gate pitch scaling of only 4%. An even bigger challenge is to scale minimum pitch metal (40/42nm), which is already at the DUV immersion lithography cliff limit of 40 nm. Beyond this limit, overlay and line shorting issues will likely cause significant yield challenges despite the use of various spacer assisted multi-patterning techniques. The current path forward for SMIC for scaling metal pitch is facing serious hurdles without access to EUV lithography. These two issues will pose significant challenges for SMIC in being able to scaling towards a true 5nm process capability.

We conclude that the Kirin 9020 is manufactured using the same SMIC 7nm (N+2) process and that further marginal scaling will likely come from an optical shrink; commercialization of any major scaling beyond that would be very difficult (if not impossible), without access to EUV lithography.
