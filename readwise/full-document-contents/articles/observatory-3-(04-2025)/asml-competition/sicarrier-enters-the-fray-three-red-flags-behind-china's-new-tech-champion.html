As US-China tech tensions escalate, Beijing is doubling down on semiconductor self-reliance—particularly in the strategically vital field of chipmaking equipment.

Now entering the spotlight is SiCarrier, a newcomer that's making waves with bold public claims. Analysts liken the buzz to earlier headline-grabbing moves by DeepSeek and Huawei, positioning SiCarrier as China's next symbolic leap in semiconductor independence.

SiCarrier's high-profile debut triggers three major industry concerns

Despite the company's aggressive media blitz, industry veterans have flagged three key areas of concern.

  1. Rather than taking on global incumbents, SiCarrier is going head-to-head with domestic heavyweights like Naura Technology and AMEC, raising fears of intensified infighting over government funding and deepening China's internal "involution" dilemma.

  2. Industry sources say SiCarrier's product lineup appears cobbled together from reverse-engineered designs, both local and foreign, raising doubts about its performance, reliability, and long-term prospects.

  3. Despite its bold ambitions, SiCarrier's only confirmed customer so far is SiEn (QingDao) Integrated Circuits, a Huawei-affiliated foundry, fueling skepticism over its ability to attract broader market adoption.

Background, talent pool, and technical claims

Founded in August 2021, SiCarrier operates under Shenzhen Major Industry Investment Group, a fully owned subsidiary of Shenzhen's local SASAC branch. Industry insiders believe Huawei is quietly backing the venture.

In just four years, SiCarrier has rolled out a sweeping portfolio spanning lithography, thin-film deposition, and etching. The centerpiece: a self-developed 28nm stepper for 12-inch wafers that has drawn industry notice.

That said, how is the rest of the semiconductor equipment world viewing this meteoric rise?

The company's leadership reportedly includes former engineers from ASML, Applied Materials (AMAT), Tokyo Electron (TEL), and TSMC. SiCarrier has also set up a network of R&D and production hubs across China.

What surprises many is that SiCarrier launched during China's pandemic lockdowns in 2021 and is already pitching itself as a domestic rival to ASML and Applied Materials. Sources say the firm has aggressively sourced high-end tools from Europe, the US, Japan, and Taiwan—often through intermediaries—stoking questions about its long-term game plan.

Global suppliers remain divided. Some continue filling SiCarrier's orders, chasing short-term profits despite IP concerns. Others are backing out, wary of being linked to potential knockoff operations and losing long-term footing in the Chinese market.

China's path to semiconductor autonomy: only a matter of time?

Huawei stunned the global tech industry in 2024 with its return to the 5G smartphone market, powered by a 7nm processor fabricated by SMIC using only deep ultraviolet (DUV) lithography.

Then came DeepSeek in early 2025, shaking global AI markets with a low-cost, high-performance large language model and tanking Nvidia's stock in the process.

Together, these developments signaled China's resolve to overcome US tech sanctions. At recent semiconductor trade shows, officials amplified nationalistic rhetoric, spotlighting SiCarrier as the third major milestone in its tech self-sufficiency push, after Huawei and SMIC.

SiCarrier says it offers a fully integrated value chain, from materials research and component production to full system integration. The company claims its 28nm tools are already on par with those from global competitors.

Its product lineup includes atomic layer deposition (ALD), chemical vapor deposition (CVD), physical vapor deposition (PVD), etching, and annealing systems—key technologies in modern semiconductor fabrication.

Despite mounting skepticism and internal pushback—including rumblings from executives at Naura and AMEC—many expect China's chip equipment industry to enter another phase of hyper-competitive, possibly unsustainable, growth.

Others caution against writing China off. SMIC has already achieved volume production at 7nm—and reportedly even 5nm—without EUV machines. As one insider put it, "If it can ship, money is no object."

DeepSeek is now a frequent reference among tech executives, having demonstrated that cost-effective innovation can match top-tier performance.

Observers point to China's deep talent pool, rapid iteration—even if imitation-driven—and massive domestic market as structural advantages. For them, the question isn't if China will break through, but when.
