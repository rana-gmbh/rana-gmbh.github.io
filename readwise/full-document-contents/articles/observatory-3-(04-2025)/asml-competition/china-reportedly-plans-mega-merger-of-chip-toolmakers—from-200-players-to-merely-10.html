China is pushing forward a major consolidation plan to restructure its fragmented semiconductor equipment sector, reportedly cutting more than 200 domestic firms down to just 10 core players. The initiative aims to sharpen national competitiveness and accelerate self-sufficiency amid intensifying US sanctions under President Donald Trump.

Targeted consolidation strategy to counter Trump-era sanctions

Reports from ZDNet Korea and ETNews reveal that Beijing is spearheading the overhaul with a "selection and concentration" approach, channeling resources into a smaller group of technologically advanced and scalable companies, departing from the current subsidy-reliant model.

Industry insiders note that China's socialist market framework is enabling the government to drive this top-down consolidation. The aim: simplify the industrial ecosystem, empower domestic champions, and push back against Washington's export controls and tech blockade.

Massive state investment and capacity expansion drive

The Korea Institute for International Economic Policy reports that from 2014 to 2024, China invested over CNY1.331 trillion (approx. US$182.2 billion) to localize its semiconductor sector. Yet, domestic self-reliance in chipmaking stands at only 23%, highlighting continued shortfalls in advanced manufacturing capabilities.

Leading the consolidation wave is Naura Technology—one of China's largest semiconductor equipment firms. In March 2025, it acquired a 9.5% stake in coating equipment provider Zhejiang Jingsheng Mechanical and Electrical Co. for CNY1.69 billion, with plans to expand its holdings to gain full control within a year.

Ranked among the world's top 10 in deposition, etching, and cleaning tools, Naura is using the acquisition to diversify into the coating segment, marking a strategic extension of its product portfolio.

Daum reports that industry experts see Naura's deal as the first major move in China's consolidation drive, likely paving the way for more M&A as top players race to broaden their offerings and scale up operations.


Credit: AFP

Geopolitical workarounds and shifting global supply chains

To sidestep US export controls, Chinese manufacturers are relocating final assembly operations to Singapore and Malaysia, strategically altering the country-of-origin label on high-tech gear.

South Korean media note that the US President Trump's administration has slapped tariffs as high as 145% on Chinese semiconductor equipment and added many Chinese firms to the US Entity List.

In response, Chinese firms are accelerating global expansion, posing a competitive threat to South Korea but also creating potential openings in the supply chain realignment.

The US push to exclude China from every link in the semiconductor chain—from raw materials to finished components—is expected to drive substitution demand. For South Korea, it's a rare chance to expand exports of materials, parts, and production tools.

SEMI's September 2024 forecast shows a sharp rebound in global 300mm wafer fab equipment spending—up 4% to US$99.3 billion in 2024, then surging 24% to US$123.2 billion in 2025, breaking the US$100 billion threshold for the first time.

Expenditures are projected to hit US$136.2 billion in 2026 and US$140.8 billion in 2027. China remains the world's largest single market, with US$45 billion in equipment sales forecast for 2024 and more than US$100 billion in investments planned through 2027.

From January to November 2024, China's semiconductor equipment imports from the Netherlands soared 34% year-over-year to US$8.175 billion.

The shipments included 228 lithography systems, highlighting booming demand for advanced tools. ASML's fourth-quarter earnings revealed EUR7.088 billion in new orders, a 169% jump from the previous quarter, underscoring the global equipment boom.

China's bigger picture: Building a resilient semiconductor supply chain

At its core, China's consolidation drive aims not just to cut redundancy or boost efficiency, but to build globally competitive national champions, according to Sina.

With companies like Huawei claiming near-5.5nm production capabilities, the strategic importance of China's domestic equipment vendors is surging.

Beijing's goal is clear: support firms that are tech-savvy, scalable, and globally competitive, concentrating resources to reinforce the broader semiconductor ecosystem.
