In the US-China trade war, the focus of US pressure has been on curbing the development of China's high-tech sectors—especially semiconductors, artificial intelligence (AI), and quantum computing, with the first two being closely intertwined.

In semiconductors, US controls almost span the entire industry chain—from design tools (EDA), products, and manufacturing equipment to material embargoes. Naturally, China has responded with a full-spectrum domestic substitution strategy to boost self-sufficiency.

China's weak spots in semiconductor equipment include electron beam testers (e-beam testers), ion implanters, and lithography equipment.

The electron beam tester is a measurement tool primarily used for defect detection and debugging, not part of the manufacturing process.

Ion implanters—especially high-energy (~1 MeV) ones—are crucial for manufacturing high-voltage silicon carbide (SiC) MOSFETs. Today's electric vehicle voltages have already progressed from 600V to 800V and 1200V. Without high-energy ion implanters, it is impossible to produce automotive-grade high-voltage power devices, which significantly affects China's self-sufficiency rate in electronic components.

Naturally, the biggest focus is lithography equipment.

The capability of a lithography machine determines the ultimate resolution of advanced manufacturing processes, which are directly tied to advanced technology nodes. One key application of advanced manufacturing is various XPUs (processors), which are highly related to computing power—particularly GPUs dedicated to artificial intelligence applications. Another application lies in high-bandwidth memory (HBM), also used in AI chip architectures.

China's new DUV lithography machine marks early but significant progress

Since September 2024, various reports about progress in China's lithography equipment have steadily emerged—naturally becoming top news for the global semiconductor industry.

First, China's Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) unveiled a DUV (deep ultraviolet) lithography machine in September 2024.

Essentially, this is a dry DUV lithography machine using an argon fluoride (ArF) excimer laser as its light source, with a laser wavelength of 193nm. The resolution of the machine is 65nm. Assuming other system properties are optimized, the numerical aperture (NA) of its objective lens (the main lens used to collect light and form images) is estimated at around 0.75. To further improve resolution, additional effort will be needed to reach the industry's current frontier NA level of 0.93.

In other words, the optical system is still at an early stage of development.

Its overlay accuracy (the alignment precision between upper and lower layers) is 8nm. To support a process with a 65nm critical dimension (CD), the overlay accuracy needs to be about 20% of the CD—around 13nm. If double exposure is required, the overlay accuracy would have to improve to 13/2 = 6.5nm.

Clearly, the current resolution of this lithography machine is fixed at 65nm, and it cannot enhance resolution further via double exposure.

China eyes immersion lithography to boost resolution

Looking ahead, in addition to improving the objective lens's numerical aperture, development must also move towards immersion lithography. By utilizing water's higher refractive index (1.44 compared to vacuum's 1), the overall resolution of the lithography machine can be increased—potentially reaching 28nm resolution. For sophisticated components like FinFETs, some process steps would require double or even multiple patterning.

Immersion lithography introduces new challenges, such as controlling water purity and maintaining consistent water temperature. Research in this area is already underway—for example, the Chinese Academy of Sciences' Changchun Institute of Optics, Fine Mechanics, and Physics is developing an immersion objective lens with a numerical aperture of 0.8.

The light source task is handled by Beijing RSLaser Opto-Electronics Technology, a company incubated by the CAS Institute of Optoelectronics and the Institute of Microelectronics. A team at CAS Anhui Institute of Optics and Fine Mechanics is developing the power module. Parallel development across multiple technical fronts is a plausible approach.

Since 2023, occasional reports have suggested that Shanghai Micro Electronics Equipment (SMEE) has begun delivering its 28nm immersion DUV system, model SSA/800-10W, with an overlay accuracy of 1.9nm. The most recent rumor suggested delivery on January 7, 2025.

However, this model does not appear in SMEE's official product catalog, nor does it have any official announcement or credible media coverage Based on the "10W" designation, this model is likely only a prototype, as its light source power is insufficient to support the throughput needed for mass production.

There have been no follow-up reports of prototype machines being sent to wafer fabs for in-line process adjustment, so it remains unclear whether actual deliveries have occurred. Moreover, transitioning from a prototype to a production model always takes considerable time.

As for the previous-generation 90nm DUV system, several units have been delivered since 2022. The main early issues were system instability, excessive downtime, and low throughput due to insufficient light source power (20W).

Thus, this recent news holds more significance for China's efforts toward domestic substitution of lithography equipment than for any imminent breakthrough in advanced technology.
