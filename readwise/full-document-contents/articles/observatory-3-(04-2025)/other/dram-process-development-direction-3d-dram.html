Since the 20nm node, advancements in DRAM process technology have decelerated, with development from 19 to 11nm occurring in small, 1 to 2nm steps across several nodes labeled from 1x to the forthcoming 1d.

Planar DRAM processes are still utilized, but manufacturers have significantly enhanced performance and power efficiency by adopting vertical stacking on wafers. While advancements in chip density progress slowly, they remain crucial for applications like high-bandwidth memory (HBM), with only certain process generations meeting the rising capacity demands through higher-density chips.

Slowing returns on shrinking dimensions

Advancing the process no longer reduces the cost per bit due to increased complexity. Samsung Electronics' 1b process uses five layers of extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography, leading to high manufacturing costs.

The DRAM market is not expected to vanish soon, but slow process advancements threaten its status as a cutting-edge technology sector. High-tech industries thrive on rapid progress for premium profits, making DRAM manufacturers concerned about the current stagnation.

In the sub-10nm range, DRAM companies are generally focusing on 3D DRAM technology. Samsung, however, plans to implement vertical channel transistors (VCT) after the 1d process node. These transistors reorient the planar structure vertically, decreasing the memory cell footprint from 6f2 to 4f2, where "f" indicates the semiconductor process feature size.

This process improvement represents a one-time advancement beyond the 10nm scale, with no further extension possibilities. Vertical channel transistors are unrelated to future 3D DRAM processes, making research efforts only temporarily beneficial. As a result, not all DRAM companies choose this approach.

The puzzle of delayed 3D DRAM adoption

The main question about introducing 3D DRAM isn't why 3D processing is adopted now, but why the delay? Major DRAM manufacturers have had 3D NAND technology since around 2013-2014, when DRAM processes were already nearing 25 to 20nms, moving towards sub-10nm challenges. Using established 3D process technologies to enhance these stagnant DRAM processes appeared to be a logical step.

The inherent structural features of DRAM present particular challenges in manufacturing. For a circuit to be successfully produced using 3D processes, it must meet specific criteria, including a high level of repetition. Due to this characteristic, memory arrays such as DRAM are especially well-suited for 3D processing techniques.

Materials between memory layers should be shareable. In TCAT (Terabit Cell Array Transistor) 3D NAND technology, gate control materials like polysilicon and charge trap materials like silicon nitride are shared across layers. This approach simplifies vertical integration, allowing for the production of 32-layer memories with only four or five photomasks.

3D DRAM structures face challenges due to the need for complete isolation of capacitors to prevent signal crosstalk between memory cells. High-mobility channels cannot utilize lightly doped polysilicon in the deep, high aspect ratio trenches required. Shared materials across layers are limited to either word lines or bit lines, depending on whether the design focuses on simpler vertical processing or minimal area usage.

DRAM demands higher performance and lower latency than NAND, with induced capacitance from stacked layers degrading performance and signal integrity. These challenges make 3D DRAM more complex than 3D NAND, delaying its adoption.

The future of DRAM's status in the high-tech industry largely depends on 3D DRAM. Alternative concepts like capacitor-less DRAM often face challenges with data retention times that do not meet current DRAM standards.

Early glimpses into 3D DRAM architectures

In July 2023, ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT) and Samsung unveiled their advancements in 3D DRAM technology at different industry conferences, highlighting their long-term preparation efforts, with differences primarily in the timing and venues of their disclosures.

CXMT's design shifts the capacitors and transistors from a vertical stack in 2D DRAM to a horizontal orientation in 3D DRAM, creating individual memory cells.

CXMT's simulations reveal that horizontally arranged capacitors measure around 500nms, transistors about 200nms, and, together with word and bit lines, each memory cell covers nearly 1 micrometer across.

Their process involves vertical word lines extending to the contact plane below. This slightly enlarges the memory cell area but simplifies vertical integration, aligning with the early-stage development focus on manufacturability before optimization.

Peripheral circuits near the memory array are created on separate chips and hybrid bonded to the multi-layer 3D NAND memory array.

The initial process features word line and bit line metal pitches of 70nms, aligning with a 35nm node using past DRAM definitions. This is similar to the 30–40nm processes seen at the advent of 3D NAND.

Stacking 32 layers of 3D DRAM achieves memory capacities akin to Samsung's 1b 2D process. Increasing to 64 layers nears the capacity of first-generation sub-10nm 0a processes. This method enables consistent scaling while maintaining DRAM's key industry characteristics.

Samsung plans to launch 3D DRAM between 2026 and 2028 alongside existing 2D processes, similar to its approach during the early phases of 3D NAND's introduction.

Future landscape and geopolitical implications

If 3D DRAM becomes viable, two key considerations arise. Firstly, will high-bandwidth memory (HBM) continue to evolve using existing techniques? Currently, HBM increases capacity by stacking multiple DRAM chips with advanced packaging, a process that significantly adds to overall costs. In contrast, enhancing memory capacity through monolithic 3D processes could eventually lower costs, although this is a longer-term solution.

The geopolitical issue at hand involves CXMT, which claims to be the first to unveil 3D DRAM technology despite other companies exploring it quietly. CXMT's motivation is likely driven by its current process node of around 1z, which lags two to three generations behind top competitors. By adopting 3D DRAM processes, CXMT could quickly narrow this technological gap, initiating a new competitive phase.

CXMT's most promising breakthrough lies in 3D processing, shifting the focus from lithography to etching amid limited EUV resources. Although it's challenging for outsiders to evaluate 3D DRAM development progress among companies, it's likely that CXMT is dedicating significant resources to this innovation.
