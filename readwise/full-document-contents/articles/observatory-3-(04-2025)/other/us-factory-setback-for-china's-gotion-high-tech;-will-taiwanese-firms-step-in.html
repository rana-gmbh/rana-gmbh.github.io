Gotion High-Tech, one of China's top lithium battery manufacturers, had positioned itself as a trailblazer among Chinese companies expanding into the US market. However, the company's foray into America has not been without hurdles. Recently, reports from international media revealed that operations at its US factory have been halted. In response, Gotion stated that its projects in Illinois are still progressing steadily.

According to industry sources, Gotion's journey in the US has been closely watched by the market, as the company navigates multiple challenges. There are now rumors circulating that Gotion may be looking to find new partners for its US operations, or possibly sell the facility as a means of completing this chapter. Among the potential buyers, Taiwanese manufacturers are said to be contenders.

Why Taiwanese manufacturers?

Observers believe that Taiwanese companies could have several motivations for taking over Gotion's US facility. First, Taiwanese companies are seen as less controversial due to their international standing, which might ease geopolitical tensions. Second, the rapidly growing US market for electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage presents a prime opportunity. For Taiwanese firms that have entered the industry later than their Chinese counterparts, acquiring a US facility could serve as a springboard to quickly integrate into global supply chains.

The growing significance of Taiwan's semiconductor industry in US-Taiwan relations underscores a broader trend, where Taiwan plays a key role in shaping future tech sectors such as 5G, AIoT, and new energy vehicles. The rise of generative AI has also amplified the potential for these industries to impact markets far beyond traditional expectations.

Despite US President Donald Trump's characterization of Taiwan as a key player in disrupting the American semiconductor industry, the reality remains that the US still relies heavily on Taiwanese support to maintain its leadership in semiconductor technology.

In addition, Taiwanese giants in the electronics industry continue to collaborate with American brands, cementing their foothold in sectors like consumer electronics, data centers, and autonomous vehicles. This partnership is seen as a competitive advantage, potentially bolstering the US-Taiwan manufacturing relationship, particularly in lithium battery production.

Hesitation to take over

DIGITIMES also reached out to Taiwanese companies regarding rumors of interest in Gotion's US operations, but executives from the purported companies swiftly denied the allegations.

Several reasons are cited for the hesitation. First, the current geopolitical landscape is turbulent, with the US imposing tariffs on global automotive parts and frequently adjusting tax rates. In this environment, a cautious wait-and-see approach may be the most prudent course of action.

Second, building a factory in the US is not an immediate priority. While the US market is seen as full of potential, opportunities are also emerging in other international markets such as Japan, South Korea, Southeast Asia, and Europe.

Taiwan's lithium battery manufacturers, though behind their Chinese counterparts in terms of scale and timing, are carving out a competitive niche in the industry. This moment presents a critical opportunity for them to prove their capabilities in international markets.

Looking back to 2023, Gotion, particularly in the lithium iron phosphate (LFP) segment, ranked just behind industry giants like CATL and BYD in China. The company also garnered significant attention from major automaker Volkswagen, which invested in the company and became its largest shareholder.

At the time, US-China relations were already tense, but Gotion moved forward with its US plans, bolstered by its partnership with Volkswagen, positioning itself as a "hybrid" entity with both Chinese and Western backing. This positioning helped mitigate the geopolitical risks often associated with purely Chinese investments.

However, the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) introduced further complications. The Act stipulates that companies owned or controlled by governments of certain countries, including China, Russia, North Korea, and Iran, face restrictions on providing battery materials and components for US-made electric vehicles. These limitations also apply to joint ventures where Chinese entities hold more than 25% ownership.

Gotion's US factory was likely seen as a strategic move to meet these conditions, with significant investments being made based on the belief that it could navigate these restrictions. However, the recent rumors of halted operations suggest that even the hybrid model has been unable to shield the company from the growing turbulence in the US-China relationship. The increasing tensions between the two countries have made Gotion a potential pawn in this geopolitical chess game.
