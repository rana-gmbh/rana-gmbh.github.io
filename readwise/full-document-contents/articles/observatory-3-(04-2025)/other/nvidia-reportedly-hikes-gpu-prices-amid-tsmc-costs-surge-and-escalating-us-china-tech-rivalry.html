Nvidia is navigating a turbulent stretch, as soaring production costs at its foundry partner TSMC have reportedly prompted the company to raise prices across its GPU product line. It is also grappling with the effects of a US ban on the export of downgraded H20 chips to China, a restriction that contributed to a US$5.5 billion loss reported in its latest quarterly earnings. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang has been actively engaging with both US and Chinese authorities, striving to mitigate the impact of these restrictions and advocate for the relaxation of AI chip export controls.


Credit: Nvidia

Escalating costs at TSMC's US facility drive price hikes

According to industry sources, Nvidia has implemented price increases across a wide range of its products as it seeks to protect profit margins amid rising manufacturing costs and regulatory challenges. The company has reportedly authorized partners to follow suit, leading to noticeable price increases on both consumer and enterprise-grade products.

Among the notable adjustments is the flagship RTX 5090 graphics card, which launched earlier this year at approximately US$2,600 and is now being sold at around US$3,000 in retail outlets, representing an increase of more than 10%. Similar price hikes have been observed for GPU modules such as the H200 and B200, as well as related server products, with increases ranging between 10% and 15%.

Although export controls have limited the sale of specific AI chips to China, demand for these products in other regions remains robust. Cloud service providers are notably increasing their capital investments, which is contributing to ongoing growth in Nvidia's data center segment.

Analysts expect the company's upcoming earnings to show solid profitability within its guidance range, fueled by unrelenting global demand for AI infrastructure and accelerated computing solutions.

Geopolitical headwinds and export restrictions

Nvidia is facing increased challenges due to stringent US export controls, particularly following US President Donald Trump's tightened AI chip ban in April 2025. These regulations, which prohibited the sale of Nvidia's downgraded H20 chips to China, resulted in a US$5.5 billion write-down in the company's quarterly earnings.

This setback has positioned Nvidia as a primary target in the escalating US-China technology rivalry. In response, Huang has engaged in high-stakes diplomacy, shuttling between Washington and Beijing to mitigate the fallout and advocate for a relaxation of the restrictions.

Huang has publicly cautioned that overly restrictive policies could undermine US technological leadership, citing the rapid advancements of Chinese competitors like Huawei, which has intensified its AI chip development despite being blacklisted by the US In a recent appearance alongside Trump, Huang emphasized the need for policies that bolster American competitiveness rather than stifle innovation through export curbs.

Strategic investments in US manufacturing

To align with Trump's "America First" manufacturing agenda, Huang is spearheading a massive US$500 billion, four-year investment plan in collaboration with industry heavyweights TSMC(2330.TW) and Foxconn. The effort includes building advanced AI server production facilities across the US, with a centerpiece project focused on producing next-generation 4nm Blackwell chips at TSMC's Fab 37 plant in Arizona.

Nvidia's financial outlook remains robust despite prevailing challenges, supported by strong global demand for artificial intelligence chips. Large cloud service providers are continuing to increase capital spending, which helps sustain steady demand for Nvidia's products.

For the fiscal fourth quarter ending January 26, 2025, the company reported revenue of US$39.3 billion, surpassing expectations with a 12% increase from the previous quarter and a 78% rise compared to the same period last year. The data center division, a key driver of Nvidia's expansion, posted revenue of US$35.6 billion, marking a 16% sequential gain and a 93% annual increase, while the overall gross margin stood at 73%.

Looking ahead to the fiscal first quarter of 2026, Nvidia forecasts revenue of approximately US$43 billion, representing a 65% increase year over year, although this growth rate is expected to slow from the previous year's 262% surge. The company also anticipates maintaining a gross margin near 70%, highlighting its capacity to offset cost pressures while preserving profitability.

Industry-wide ripple effects

Nvidia's recent price increases have impacted the supply chain, leading partners such as Asus to revise their pricing approaches. The RTX 5090 has attracted significant demand, especially from Chinese buyers who have purchased available units at higher prices ahead of an export restriction.

In response to decreased demand from China, manufacturers have adopted bundled sales strategies, combining GPUs with power supplies or complete system assemblies to sustain profitability. Additionally, server vendors have raised their pricing by 10-15% in response to Nvidia's increased chip and module prices.

Ahead of its late May earnings release, Jensen Huang is scheduled to meet with supply chain partners and industry leaders in Taiwan. On May 12, 2025, he is scheduled to meet with key supply chain partners in Taiwan. Later, on May 17, he will attend a high-profile dinner with prominent industry leaders, an event reportedly referred to as the "trillion-dollar banquet" because of the combined net worth of those present.

Huang's visit will conclude on May 19 with a keynote speech. As the AI race intensifies, Nvidia's diplomatic and operational maneuvers will be critical in sustaining its position at the forefront of the industry.
