On April 17, TSMC reported that there were no significant changes in its growth momentum and investment plans. The company stressed that customers have not made any adjustments in terms of market demand. However, this perspective seems overly optimistic to the market, as the current lack of change is primarily due to customers being on standby and is unlikely to persist.

Recently, IC design houses have stated that they will continue to observe concerning tariff-related issues, essentially following the lead of downstream customers. If customers do not adjust their strategies, companies will refrain from making changes. Given that tariff policies can shift significantly every few days, it is impractical to constantly adapt; instead, stakeholders prefer to wait until things settle down before addressing these matters.

According to chip manufacturers, aside from some preemptive stocking needs during the 90-day exemption period, the demand situation for the second half of the year does not appear to have undergone substantial adjustments. It can be said that the entire supply chain is still in a state of anticipation.

Industry insiders note that the second quarter is typically a slow season, and key new products are unlikely to see large-scale shipments initiated during this time. Therefore, the supply chain does not need to rush into order adjustments.

This scenario is not entirely favorable for businesses. Even if overall demand remains largely unchanged under the impact of tariffs, early stockpiling in the first half of the year implies reduced order volume in the latter half. Consequently, the effects of the peak season are expected to weaken, which explains why the market has not fully embraced TSMC's confidence boost.

IC design houses also pointed out that since nearly all advanced process products are handled by TSMC(2330.TW), especially cloud AI chips or networking chips that are not directly linked to consumer markets, their performance is likely to surpass that of the broader market. This trend has been consistent amid fluctuating market conditions over the past few years.

TSMC's market position has become one of the last remaining firms affected by headwinds, so maintaining confidence is quite normal. However, for IC design houses focused on consumer markets, there is little room for complacency regarding the market conditions in the second half of the year.

The general consensus in the market suggests that while the US is negotiating trade terms with various countries and some positive developments have emerged, most opinions remain pessimistic about the market conditions post-2025. Predictions of a hard landing for the US economy continue to increase.

In such circumstances, major economic entities may enter a more conservative growth phase. Even without a significant recession, projected growth rates are likely to be revised downward. For chip manufacturers, market indicators are no longer driven by leading firms but rather by each announcement from the White House.
