US President Donald Trump's sweeping new tariffs are already forcing semiconductor firms to rethink production strategies. Supply chain sources say high costs at TSMC's Arizona fab had previously made US clients like Apple hesitant to commit to large-scale orders.

In response to the new tariff push, Apple, AMD, and Nvidia have quickly rolled out updated "Made in USA" plans, expanding wafer orders at TSMC's US fab. With limited 4nm capacity at the Arizona site, demand has spiked, and competition for production slots is heating up.

The rush in demand has allowed TSMC(2330.TW) to shift costs. Reports suggest the company plans to raise foundry prices by up to 30% to offset losses at its Arizona facility.

Limited TSMC capacity sparks pricing surge as clients scramble to localize production

Apple, AMD, and Nvidia have now formally committed to ramping up US-based chip production. Apple remains TSMC's top customer, but all three are accelerating orders ahead of the looming tariffs.

On April 14, Nvidia revealed that its next-gen Blackwell chips, built on TSMC's 4nm node, are already in production at the Arizona fab. It is also collaborating with Amkor and Siliconware Precision on backend packaging.

Foxconn's Houston site and Wistron's Dallas facility are preparing to ramp up large-scale AI server production within the next 12 to 15 months.

Over the next four years, Nvidia's partnerships with TSMC, Foxconn, Wistron, Amkor, and Siliconware Precision could drive up to US$500 billion in AI infrastructure buildout in the US, according to industry sources.

AMD CEO Lisa Su—typically low-key during Taiwan visits—made a rare public statement on April 15 ahead of a speech at National Taiwan University, confirming AMD's plans to boost AI server chip production in the US.


AMD CEO Lisa Su. Credit: DIGITIMES

AMD's fifth-gen EPYC server chips have already cleared validation at TSMC's Arizona fab. A new EPYC processor, codenamed "Venice" and built on TSMC's 2nm node, has also reportedly taped out—marking AMD's first HPC product on the process.

Trump's tariff strategy is rattling global markets. Backed by IEEPA and Section 232 of the 1962 Trade Expansion Act, his unpredictable and aggressive trade posture has put governments and supply chains worldwide on edge.

Apple, Nvidia, and AMD—previously in wait-and-see mode—are now actively executing US-based manufacturing plans.

Limited TSMC Arizona capacity fuels 30% price hike amid order surge

TSMC's Arizona fab currently produces just 20,000 to 30,000 wafers per month. Amid an influx of orders, the company has hiked foundry pricing by 30%—a cost burden customers are absorbing.

Industry insiders say TSMC's cost-transfer strategy is gaining traction. Though initial forecasts assumed pushback on US pricing, Trump's tariff pressure has rapidly increased fab utilization, helping ease losses at the Arizona site.

TSMC will hold its first-quarter earnings call on April 17. While the US has issued a 90-day grace period before tariffs take effect, more clarity on semiconductor duty rates is expected imminently.

Analysts project TSMC's second-quarter revenue could rise nearly 10% as urgent orders fuel momentum. Gross margins are expected to hold near 58.5%, with reduced risk of a full-year guidance cut.

Some warn that front-loaded demand could weaken second-half growth. With macroeconomic volatility and Trump's shifting policy stance, TSMC may still revise down its 2025 US-dollar revenue growth target of 25%.
