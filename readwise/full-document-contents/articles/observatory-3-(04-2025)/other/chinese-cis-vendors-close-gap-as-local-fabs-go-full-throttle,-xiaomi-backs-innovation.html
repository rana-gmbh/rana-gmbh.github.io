Following SK Hynix's exit from the CMOS image sensor (CIS) market, Chinese vendors are aggressively scaling shipments, gaining market share, and climbing the value chain by deepening ties with domestic smartphone brands. Armed with robust domestic foundry capacity across 40nm to 28/22nm nodes, these suppliers are now pushing into high-end CIS segments with ample supply and rising demand.

SmartSens pivots to local fabs, taps Nexchip for strategic upgrade

Since 2023, SmartSens Technology has moved large-scale CIS(2326.TW) production for surveillance applications from Samsung Foundry to domestic partner Nexchip Semiconductor.

In February 2025, SmartSens signed a long-term strategic agreement with Nexchip to co-develop stacked CIS technologies, targeting key technical bottlenecks with localized innovation.

Supply chain sources report that by 2025, CIS capacity at SMIC, Hua Hong Semiconductor, and Nexchip will reach full utilization, highlighting a shift to domestic production alongside an industry-wide push toward higher-end products.

Chinese challengers reshape global CIS rankings

The global CIS landscape shifted in 2024 as challengers like OmniVision Technologies, GalaxyCore Inc., and SmartSens gained ground despite Sony and Samsung maintaining leading positions.

While Sony and Samsung traditionally ranked first and second in CIS revenue, 2024 shipment data from Counterpoint shows Sony as the lone Japanese firm among the top three, joined by Chinese rivals GalaxyCore and OmniVision.

Sony retained its lead on the strength of high-end smartphone demand and process refinement. GalaxyCore climbed to second with a 34% YoY shipment jump, leveraging its cost advantage and Fab-lite strategy to ramp up 50MP sensor output.

OmniVision ranked third with 14% growth, driven by strong uptake of its 50MP OV50H sensor among Chinese smartphone makers.

SK Hynix's 2025 market exit is expected to free up shares for Chinese contenders.

GalaxyCore and OmniVision double down on high-end CIS strategies

Long absent in the high-end space, GalaxyCore is following the Sony–Samsung playbook by building a fab in Lingang, Shanghai, and partnering with foundries, marking its shift from Fab-less to Fab-lite.

It now operates a vertically integrated chain: chip design in Zhangjiang, process R&D and wafer production in Lingang, and advanced packaging and testing in Jiashan (Zhejiang).

GalaxyCore CEO Lixin Zhao said the company built its fab to overcome high-end CIS manufacturing barriers and compete with global imaging leaders.

OmniVision is advancing in the high-end space with a dual-track strategy of "technology differentiation + scenario coverage." Its OV50H sensor has broken foreign dominance in the 50MP class and is now a staple in Chinese flagship models.

High-end variants like the OV50K40 and OV50M40 are also helping OmniVision pull ahead in the premium CIS race.

Wallstreetcn.com reports that Xiaomi has supported OmniVision from 2019 to 2025, reducing early risk through technical validation while enabling rapid iteration via its flagship smartphones. The collaboration created a virtuous cycle of innovation, real-world testing, and mass production.

Counterpoint data shows global smartphone CIS shipments hit 4.4 billion units in 2024, up 2% year-over-year as demand rebounded. However, the average camera count per phone dipped from 3.8 to 3.7, signaling a shift toward streamlined yet capable configurations.

Canalys reports global smartphone shipments reached 1.22 billion in 2024, rising 7% YoY, with China accounting for 314 million units—an 8.7% increase.

Affordable, high-performance domestic 50MP CIS sensors have become standard in Chinese flagships, with over 80% featuring them as main cameras.
