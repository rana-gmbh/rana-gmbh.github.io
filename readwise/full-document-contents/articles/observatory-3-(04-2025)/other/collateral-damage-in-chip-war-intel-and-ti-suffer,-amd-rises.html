The US Department of Commerce has confirmed that lower-spec AI chips—such as Nvidia's H20 and AMD's MI308—now require export licenses for shipment to China. The move signals a tightening of export controls, previously focused only on high-end GPUs.

Industry sources indicate the restrictions extend beyond Nvidia and AMD, affecting other US semiconductor firms such as Intel and Texas Instruments (TI), along with their upstream and downstream domestic supply chains. These companies are now bracing for a potential downturn beginning May 2025, as China prepares retaliatory tariffs in response to escalating US trade measures.

Taiwanese chipmakers, component suppliers, and system integrators that closely collaborate with Intel could also feel the impact. Should the US and China fail to reach a resolution, Intel faces the risk of steep revenue losses in China across a range of products—from notebooks and servers to automotive platforms and industrial PCs.

Nvidia manages exposure while competitors struggle

In contrast, Nvidia appears to have anticipated the fallout. While the H20 chip can no longer be sold in China, supply chain sources say the product remains in demand across Southeast Asia and the Middle East, making inventory clearance relatively manageable. As a result, China's contribution to Nvidia's revenue is expected to fall from around 13% to the single digits—but not disappear entirely.

Other AI GPUs, such as the RTX 5090 and RTX 4090 are also subject to export restrictions. However, alternative models like the RTX 4090D remain available for gaming, workstation, and automotive markets. Historically, such products have continued to reach China via third-party channels, further softening the blow.

Nvidia has long been cautious in managing geopolitical risks, and the H20 ban was likely within its expectations. The company is betting that global AI demand will continue to grow, offsetting losses from Chinese restrictions and higher production costs associated with US-based manufacturing under "Made in America" initiatives.

Industry tensions escalate following policy shifts

Still, the broader chip industry remains on edge. Trump's aggressive trade posture has triggered concern across the supply chain, even among companies investing heavily in the US. After Trump's inauguration, TSMC announced a multi-year, multibillion-dollar investment in US chip manufacturing. However, it's unclear whether this move satisfies the Trump administration's national security and economic objectives.

Notably, Trump's policies have not spared Nvidia, despite its major US investment announcements. The company was formally notified on April 9 that its H20 chip would be subject to new export controls. Days later, Nvidia disclosed US$5.5 billion in inventory-related write-downs tied to the H20, which investors interpreted as a sign that the company had little leverage to counter the policy shift.

As tensions escalate, industry observers are asking: who truly bears the brunt of the US-China tech war?

Manufacturing origin becomes critical factor

China recently introduced import classification rules based on manufacturing origin. Under the new rules, chips produced at TSMC's Taiwan facilities are considered Taiwanese-origin, while chips made in the US are classified as American-origin. This distinction shields many US fabless firms—such as AMD, Nvidia, Qualcomm, and Broadcom—that rely on offshore foundries, while companies like Intel and TI, which fabricate domestically, face greater exposure.

For Intel, the implications are severe. CPUs designed and produced in the US will no longer be shipped to China, disrupting PC, server, and industrial computing markets. Once the current one-to-two-month inventory buffer held by Chinese customers is depleted, the supply of Intel CPUs in China could dry up, impacting motherboard and notebook production.

Unexpected beneficiaries emerge

Unexpectedly, AMD may benefit. As sales of Nvidia GPUs—typically paired with Intel CPUs—decline, PC and motherboard makers are reportedly shifting toward AMD platforms. Qualcomm and MediaTek, which are introducing Arm-based notebook chips, may also see new opportunities.

However, some industry veterans caution against assuming Intel will suffer irreparable damage. Intel has maintained a strong presence in China and operates major manufacturing facilities in Israel and Ireland, which together account for 60% of its output. The company is already reallocating orders from US fabs to these overseas sites, and has outsourced some chipset production to Samsung to maintain flexibility.

In the notebook market, while China's stance appears firm, actual implementation may leave room for negotiation. A full ban on Intel could cause major disruption to China's tech ecosystem, making a partial exemption more likely. For now, Intel holds roughly one quarter's worth of inventory in China, giving it time to adapt.

Many in the industry believe that the current outcome, particularly the impact on Intel and TI, was not fully anticipated by the Trump team. As the US and China continue to test each other's limits, a resolution may still emerge. Several supply chain stakeholders remain cautiously optimistic that ongoing tensions could ease in the coming months, allowing the global semiconductor industry a chance to stabilize.
