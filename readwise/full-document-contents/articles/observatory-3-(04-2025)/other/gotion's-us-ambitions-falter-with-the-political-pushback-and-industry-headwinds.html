Gotion High-Tech, one of China's leading lithium battery manufacturers, has suspended operations at its Michigan factory, marking the latest setback in its troubled attempt to establish a foothold in the American electric vehicle supply chain.

In what was once heralded as a US$2.36 billion bet on America's EV future, Gotion is now facing a sobering reality. Foreign media reported that Gotion High-Tech has temporarily withdrawn its permit application submitted to the Michigan Department of Environment, Great Lakes, and Energy (EGLE), indicating that the plant's power battery production equipment has been shut down. Gotion High-Tech stated that official announcements should be considered the definitive source for further updates.

A project mired in controversy

Gotion's Michigan project has been politically fraught from the start. Initially approved by the Green Charter Township board in late 2023, the factory was expected to begin operations in early 2026. But the deal quickly drew fierce opposition from residents concerned about environmental impacts and the company's Chinese ties. The backlash culminated in a dramatic political shake-up: five local officials, including the township supervisor, were recalled by voters.

A newly elected township board later withdrew support for the project in early 2024. In response, Gotion filed a breach of contract lawsuit, which prompted a legal counter from the township. The case remains pending.

Industry analysts say Gotion's experience is a cautionary tale for international firms trying to navigate the complex interplay of US local governance, public sentiment, and geopolitics.

Political crosswinds

Beyond local opposition, Gotion has faced criticism from both sides of the Pacific. In the US, the company's Chinese origins have fueled suspicion, with critics raising concerns about potential national security implications and foreign influence in critical infrastructure.

Back home, Beijing has reportedly been displeased with Gotion's decision to invest abroad, amid growing tensions over technology transfer and supply chain security. The company has even been labeled "dishonest" by some Chinese state-linked voices, adding to the strain.

Changing industry landscapes

The broader EV industry has also undergone a reality check. Automakers across Europe and North America have admitted they overestimated consumer demand for electric vehicles, prompting many to scale back production plans. Some companies have already written off EV-related investments as losses.

Meanwhile, the political landscape in the US is shifting. President Donald Trump has vowed to dismantle the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)—a key piece of legislation that incentivized clean energy and US-based manufacturing. Though Trump has expressed support for domestic EV production, he has also advocated for stricter "Made in America" standards, including the potential requirement that components be sourced 100% domestically.

Such conditions could significantly undercut the business case for Chinese battery firms trying to establish US operations, particularly if tariffs and trade barriers intensify.

High hopes, diminishing returns

Gotion's Michigan project was once seen as a strategic masterstroke. Backed by IRA subsidies, the company aimed to become a major supplier to automakers like Volkswagen and potentially Tesla. Its proximity to major EV players and its specialization in lithium iron phosphate (LFP) technology gave it a competitive edge in a market that—at least until recently—seemed ripe for disruption.

At the time, US-based LFP production was virtually non-existent, and Chinese companies held 99% of the global LFP market. Gotion's move was part of a broader Chinese strategy to expand internationally amid growing saturation and subsidy fatigue at home.

But the IRA also imposed strict requirements on the sourcing of raw materials and components, mandating increasing percentages be derived from US-friendly nations. Gotion's plan was to localize enough of its supply chain to meet these standards—but that now seems increasingly difficult, if not untenable.

A cautionary tale

Today, the site in Michigan still stands, but now as a symbol of high hopes dashed by the complex realities of modern geopolitics and industrial transformation. One supply chain executive stated that all they got was an utter mess.

As the global race for EV supremacy continues, Gotion's story may serve as a warning to others: building the future of clean energy is not just a matter of technology or capital—but of politics, patience, and timing.
