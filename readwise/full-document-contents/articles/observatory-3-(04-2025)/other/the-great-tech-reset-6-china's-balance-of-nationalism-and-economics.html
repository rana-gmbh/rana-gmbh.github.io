Over the past fifty years, China has driven its national progress through two key forces: economic growth and nationalism. Its rapid economic expansion has not only reshaped Chinese society but also played a crucial role in global economic development. Between 2009 and 2019, the world's total GDP grew from US$61 trillion to US$88 trillion, with China contributing a third of that US$27 trillion increase. This era was both China's "golden decade" and a time when the international community heavily relied on its economic engine.

However, the COVID-19 pandemic and the US-China trade war have significantly altered China's position on the global stage. Under increasing pressure from the US, can China sustain its long-held stance that "the Pacific is big enough for both China and the US"? With economic momentum fading, can nationalism alone drive the country forward? The world is watching closely, and more importantly, it remains to be seen whether government-driven economic policies can offset the departure of foreign businesses.

Shifting strategies

In recent years, multinational corporations wary of US scrutiny have adjusted their strategies, shifting toward a "Made in China for China" approach—producing goods domestically for the local market while diversifying their global supply chains beyond China.

In response to US efforts to restrict its technological advancement, China has launched the "Information Technology Application Innovation" initiative. This ambitious plan aims to ensure that China's domestic semiconductor needs are met entirely by Chinese production. The initiative not only excludes foreign companies but also seeks full self-sufficiency in chip manufacturing. It is an enormous, multi-billion-dollar endeavor—one of unprecedented scale that no other country has attempted.

Domestic market challenges

A recurring theme in China's industrial strategy is "domestic demand-driven growth." The electric vehicle (EV) industry is following a trajectory similar to that of the smartphone era, cultivating a "red supply chain" through domestic market expansion. However, the industry is also facing challenges, with an increasingly competitive market, unsold inventory, and prolonged payment cycles. Leading EV brands like BYD, XPeng, and NIO report payment delays of up to 180 days, while second-tier automakers face even longer cycles—far exceeding those of Toyota, BMW, or Volkswagen. Both the government and businesses are holding on, but for how much longer remains uncertain.

India's service sector rise

Meanwhile, India is emerging as a global service industry powerhouse. Zhang Lei, president of Hillhouse Capital, has described China as a manufacturing giant and India as a leader in services. If India successfully integrates AI into its service sector, how will the industry evolve in the coming decade?

Hillhouse Capital has already invested in India's service sector, targeting key companies to ensure a strong foothold. Additionally, India's financial industry is transforming, with more citizens gaining access to banking services and capital flowing into the system at unprecedented levels—a trend also seen in the Philippines and Indonesia. Indonesia, with its 40,000 islands, is exploring satellite communication solutions to bridge logistical gaps and enhance financial connectivity.

Looking ahead

As the digital revolution unfolds, will Indonesia become the next India—or an alternative version of it? The world watches with anticipation. The future of global technological and economic progress depends on the advancement of emerging markets like India and Indonesia, paving the way for a more diverse, resilient, and sustainable global economy in 2025 and beyond.
