As weaker players increasingly bow out of China's new energy vehicle (NEV) market, newcomers are now shifting their emphasis to smart platforms, the integration of battery, motor, and vehicle control systems, and supply chain resilience. In line with this trend, Xpeng, one of the three leading NEV startups in China alongside NIO and Li Auto, has recently launched its latest SEPA 3.0 platform and introduced a hybrid silicon carbide (SiC) module designed to balance efficiency and cost.

Market consolidation drives innovation

Although China's NEV market continues to grow, a clear pattern is now emerging in the industry as strong players grow ever stronger amid mounting pressures to deliver profit while technical thresholds rise. As one of the leaders in the game, Xpeng first introduced its 800V high-voltage superfast charging technology in 2022 and began using SiC power components in its G9 series. Building upon these earlier efforts, the company's latest SEPA 3.0 smart electric framework aims to further strengthen the integration of battery, motor, and vehicle control systems. According to Jie Gu, general manager of Xpeng's powertrain division, SEPA 3.0 is designed to support both pure electric and range-extended vehicles, to improve development efficiency while reducing costs.

Four key technologies lie at the heart of SEPA 3.0: powertrain systems, battery modules, product scheduling, and engines. Specifically, these include the company's latest 800V coaxial powertrain, second-generation cell-to-pack (CTP) battery pack, 800V hybrid SiC smart power unit, and AI engine. The combination of the new coaxial powertrain with an 800V framework and hybrid SiC modules allows for smaller components and more flexibility for placement, giving designers more room for vehicle exteriors and integrating other equipment.

Hybrid SiC delivers high performance at low cost

Xpeng's hybrid SiC power modules are another highlight of the SEPA 3.0 platform. SiC is widely viewed in the industry as a key material for improving EV efficiency and reducing charging times, yet even as the cost of SiC continues to fall, Gu believes that, based on its physical characteristics and production process, SiC prices will not drop to IGBT levels in the short term.

Xpeng's hybrid SiC solution aims to remedy this by retaining much of the high efficiency of SiC while significantly reducing costs. Gu notes that the hybrid architecture can achieve a system efficiency of 93%, only 0.2% below that of all-SiC systems, while saving over 60% in costs. Compared to conventional IGBT systems, which have an efficiency of roughly 89%, this represents a roughly 4% improvement, resulting in a 6% increase in overall vehicle range, as well as reduced battery strain and lower operating costs.

New battery technology balances charging performance and range

In terms of battery technology, the second-generation CTP battery pack offers a 5C charging rate, meaning that a battery module can be fully charged in as little as 12 minutes, a 40% improvement over 3C battery modules. In contrast with BYD, which recently unveiled a 10C supercharging solution, Gu says that Xpeng chose 5C to balance cost, energy density, and vehicle range: the company's internal evaluations showed that 10C charging would significantly compromise the batteries' energy density, leading to reduced vehicle range, and thus stability and overall effectiveness became the priorities.

AI engine offers realtime adjustments

In addition to hardware enhancements, Xpeng has also improved its power management logic. Compared to conventional ECUs, which are usually tuned according to fixed parameters and scenarios, Gu notes that the AI engine in Xpeng's SEPA 3.0 platform allows for real-time adjustments in response to external situations and changes in driving behavior, with GPU-accelerated computing to enhance the driving experience and improve energy efficiency.

To support the tight integration and real-time computing in its smart platform efforts, Xpeng has been actively refining its supply chain strategy. As the US-China trade war adds to the already growing risks in the global supply chain, Xpeng has adopted a dual-pronged strategy that focuses on both global and local approaches: in addition to its current suppliers, which include Rohm Semiconductor, Infineon, and STMicroelectronics, the company is also gradually incorporating components made in China. According to Gu, unless future trade wars or policies threaten to disrupt supply, Xpeng will switch between global and local supply chains based on practical cost-performance evaluations.
