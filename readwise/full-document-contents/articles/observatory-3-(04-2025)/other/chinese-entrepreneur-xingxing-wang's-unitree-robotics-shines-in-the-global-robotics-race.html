Among China's six Hangzhou start-ups, Unitree Robotics is one of the standout players. Founded in 2016, Unitree has been consistently profitable since 2020, achieving steady growth year after year. How has Xingxing Wang, a Chinese entrepreneur and founder of Unitree, managed to achieve such remarkable success?

Unitree's profitable strategy: Leveraging technology and cost efficiency

Recently, Nan Zhao, an early investor in Unitree, revealed in an interview with China Business Network (CBN) that the company has been profitable for the past five years. The company's market leader also confirmed that the company has been profitable year after year.

The general public did not expect Unitree to succeed in turning a profit, given the capital-intensive nature of the robotics industry. However, Unitree's self-developed technology and in-house manufacturing set it apart.

Wang, now recognized as a rising talent by even Nvidia's CEO Jensen Huang, has certainly made his mark. When Zhao met with Wang in April 2020, he recalls that Wang openly acknowledged the commercialization of the team was still in its early stages and lacked substantial proof of concept. Despite this, Wang's passion for technology was evident.

Zhao explained that Unitree's profitability lies in its unique approach to technology and cost. When Unitree began its commercialization efforts, Boston Dynamics was attempting to sell its robots for over US$70,000.

The latter struggled with the high manufacturing costs of its hardware, though it had secured several stable customers, proving there was market potential. In contrast, Unitree benefited from the systematic opportunities provided by China's industrial chain.

Zhao shared that in 2022, he visited Unitree's factory, located near the company's headquarters. The team had purchased relevant development molds, allowing for flexible supply chain production and the development of core components in-house.

Self-developed core components drive efficiency

A key factor in Unitree's success has been its ability to develop its core components in-house, shifting suppliers from component providers to raw material suppliers. This eliminated an additional layer of markup. Moreover, by handling the non-standardized component development process within its factory, Unitree could directly control production quality and costs.

This approach enhanced Unitree's ability to rapidly iterate on robot performance, particularly in areas like high-torque joint motors and joint force sensors—key components that enable advanced movements like backflips. If a robot's core components were damaged during training, working directly with in-house engineers allows for faster troubleshooting and repairs, minimizing downtime.

Unitree's cost-reduction strategy for robots

In 2020, Unitree launched the educational version of its quadruped robot, the A1, priced at around CNY100,000 (US$13,779.04). In 2021, the company introduced the Go1, a robot dog aimed at the consumer market, with a price tag of just CNY16,000. The 2023 Go2 model further reduced the cost to under CNY10,000.

This strategy of lowering costs for quadruped robots has since been applied to their bipedal humanoid robots. Interestingly, when Unitree was first founded, Wang had no intention of making humanoid robots.

Zhao revealed that it was not until the release of ChatGPT by OpenAI at the end of 2022 that Wang saw new opportunities in the field of intelligent robotic learning.

In February 2023, Unitree initiated its humanoid robot project internally. Just seven months later, the company unveiled its first humanoid robot, the H1.

Zhao believes that although Unitree was not the first to enter the bipedal humanoid robot space, its success in quadruped robots naturally paved the way for its expansion, reflecting the company's ability to adapt to new trends.
