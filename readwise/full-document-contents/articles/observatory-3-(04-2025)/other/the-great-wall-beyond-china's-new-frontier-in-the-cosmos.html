China's commercial space sector is reaching for the stars, but the journey has been rockier than anticipated.

The nation's ambitious plans to launch approximately 100 rockets in 2024 fell short, with only 68 attempts completed—two of which failed. This performance gap highlights the growing pains of an industry that's rapidly expanding yet still playing catch-up with international competitors.

"We believe that a lot of their so-called civilian space program is a military program. And I think, in effect, we are in a race," NASA Administrator Bill Nelson told the US Congress last April, underscoring the geopolitical tensions surrounding China's aerospace ambitions.


Credit: BryceTech

Indeed, the competition is intensifying. While SpaceX dominated global launches with 134 orbital missions in 2024, China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation (CASC) secured second place with 48 launches. Notably, of the 21 organizations worldwide conducting orbital launches, eight are Chinese entities—more than a third of the global total.

Military Dimensions Raise Alarm

US Space Command Commander General Stephen Whiting has raised significant concerns about China's rapid space expansion, particularly highlighting the military implications. "As of January 2024, the PRC's Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance (ISR) satellite fleet contained more than 359 systems, more than tripling its on-orbit collection presence since 2018," Whiting testified before the US Senate Armed Services Committee in 2024.

This dramatic increase in surveillance capabilities has serious implications for US military operations. "The PRC has also dramatically increased its ability to monitor, track, and target US and Allied forces, both terrestrially and on orbit," Whiting warned.

During a recent visit to allies, Whiting emphasized that China is developing counterspace weapons specifically designed to threaten US space capabilities while simultaneously using space technology to enhance their terrestrial military operations.

"Over the last six years they have tripled the number of intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance satellites on orbit, and they have used their space capabilities to improve the lethality, the precision, and the range of their terrestrial forces," said Whiting. "That obviously is a cause for concern, and something that we are watching very, very closely."


Credit: BryceTech

Of particular concern to US defense officials is China's novel approach to satellite deployment. "It's vital that given the threats we now see in novel orbits that are hard for us to get to, as well as the fact that the Chinese have been testing on-orbit refueling capability, that we need some kind of sustained space maneuver," Whiting noted, referencing China's technological advancements like the Shijian-25 satellite launched in January to test in-orbit refueling.

The general has been candid about the current strategic situation, stating: "I have testified before Congress that I believe we are in a window of vulnerability right now relative to China [because] they moved faster into the counter-space weapons than we have."

Trillion-Yuan Market Potential

Multiple industry research institutions project China's commercial aerospace market to reach at least CNY2.5 trillion(US$350 billion) by 2025. This explosive growth has attracted more than commercial aerospace companies across China, creating three major industrial clusters in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region, the Yangtze River Delta, and the Pearl River Delta. Beijing alone hosts over 300 companies spanning the entire industry chain.


A Long March-8 Y6 carrier rocket carrying 18 low Earth orbit satellites blasts off from the Hainan commercial spacecraft launch site in Wenchang, south China's Hainan Province, March 12, 2025.(AFP)

China is actively developing several major satellite megaconstellations to enhance its space-based capabilities and strengthen its position in the global satellite internet market. The Guowang Network is one of these initiatives, aiming to deploy nearly 13,000 satellites to provide global broadband internet coverage. The first batch of Guowang satellites was launched in December 2024, marking the beginning of this ambitious project. In February 2025, China successfully launched the Long March 8A rocket, which carried additional Guowang satellites into orbit.

Another significant project is the Thousand Sails constellation, also known as G60 Starlink, aims to rival SpaceX's Starlink with plans to deploy over 15,000 satellites. As of March 2025, 90 satellites are in orbit, following a recent launch of 18 satellites using the Long March 8 rocket. The project targets having 648 satellites by the end of 2025 for regional coverage, marking a significant step in China's efforts to establish a prominent presence in the global satellite internet market.

The Honghu-3 Constellation is another major project, though it has not yet launched any satellites. It aims to deploy 10,000 satellites, operating in 160 different orbital planes to provide extensive coverage. These megaconstellations are part of China's broader strategy to enhance its satellite communication, navigation, and monitoring capabilities.


A Long March-2C carrier rocket carrying two satellites blasts off from the Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center in northwest China, Feb. 27, 2025 (AFP)

The Reusability Challenge

A key battlefield in the commercial space race is reusable rocket technology. Companies like LandSpace, Deep Blue Aerospace, and iSpace are actively pursuing vertical take-off and landing capabilities similar to SpaceX's Falcon 9.

However, according to Taibo Intelligent Unit, China's commercial reusable rockets are still years behind. While development progress mirrors SpaceX's timeline, achieving vertical recovery may take until 2027, with mature recovery and reuse capabilities requiring approximately five additional years.

LandSpace's Zhuque-2 is scheduled for small-batch commercial launches this year, while the company develops its fully reusable Zhuque-3 liquid oxygen methane rocket, with a first flight planned for 2025.


Zhuque-3 reusable test rocket developed by LandSpace (Photo by Xinhua via AFP)

Funding Challenges and Government Involvement

The capital-intensive nature of the industry presents significant hurdles, a common challenge for commercial aerospace companies that have yet to establish a self-sustaining commercial cycle.

Until 2023, government industrial funds and guidance funds dominated investment in the sector, with limited private capital participation. While government-led funding continues to predominate in 2024, more private investors are entering the market.

One commercial aerospace executive noted that many companies simultaneously partner with multiple local governments, creating a "one company, many local government lead investors" model. This has led to scattered, unfocused investments that sometimes diverge from companies' development paths.

Infrastructure Bottlenecks

Despite growing investment, critical infrastructure remains insufficient. Commercial launch sites are in high demand but short supply, especially as major satellite constellations begin deployment.


Credit: AFP

With the successive launches of satellites from the Qianfan and GW constellations, both aiming for "10,000-satellite constellations," commercial rocket launch demand is expected to surge in the near future.

The limited number of commercial aerospace tracking and control stations also constrains operations, as high-frequency launches require resource-sharing with "national team" assets, reducing launch flexibility.

International Expansions and Military Implications

China's commercial space ambitions extend well beyond its borders. The country has active space partnerships with at least 22 African nations and countries including Saudi Arabia, UAE, Brazil, France, Belarus, Pakistan, Venezuela, and Egypt.

The Belt and Road Initiative serves as a platform for expanding China's space presence, with over 140 participating countries across Asia, Europe, Africa, and Latin America.


Credit: Green Finance & Development Center, Fanhai International School of Finance (FISF) at Fudan University

US Space Command Commander Stephen Whiting has expressed concern over China's rapid progress, noting that the country has tripled its number of spy satellites in orbit over the past six years.

In a significant technological milestone, China launched the Shijian-25 satellite in January to test in-orbit refueling—a capability that enhances China's space-based infrastructure and supports its broader geopolitical objectives. The development of this technology intensifies competition in the global space race.

Provincial Support Drives Growth

Marco Aliberti, associate manager at the European Space Policy Institute, attributes much of the industry's growth to provincial policies supporting the "New Space" sector.

"China's provincial policies need to be seen as a major contributing factor to the emergence and current flourishing of the Chinese commercial space industry," Aliberti told SpaceNews, noting that leading companies have secured substantial funding from provincial or city governments over the past decade.

This local support is driven by both strategic and economic rationales, enhancing provincial status while contributing to China's civil-military integration strategy and international power projection.


Credit: AFP

The Road Ahead

As the commercial space sector evolves, industry experts suggest that the "next decade" of development should focus on breakthroughs in key areas such as recoverable rockets, satellite internet, and high-precision navigation and positioning technology, while developing applications across communication, navigation, remote sensing, and scientific experimentation.

The ultimate goal extends beyond rocket launches and satellite networks to building a commercial aerospace industrial ecosystem covering the entire value chain—a prelude to the space economy era that China is determined to help shape. However, as General Whiting has warned, these developments carry significant implications for global security and military competition in space, creating what he describes as a "window of vulnerability" that demands urgent attention from US policymakers and defense officials.
