As US-China trade tensions escalate and global supply chains restructure, China's shift from exports to domestic sales is becoming a long-term strategy—not a short-term fix. Surging consumer stocks reflect growing investor confidence in the "China for China" model, where local production is tailored to serve local demand.

CCOOP leads retail rally as consumer sector soars

Chinese consumer A-shares surged, with Gansu Guofang Industry & Trade Co. and Zoy Home Furnishing Co. reaching their daily trading limits, according to Chinastarmarket.cn and 21jingji.com. Supply chain-focused stocks like Shandong Swan and Shandong Sino-Agri also gained expectations of a shift toward domestic consumption.

Leveraging its nationwide network, CCOOP has become a central player in China's "dual circulation" strategy. On April 14, 2025, it announced a "green channel" program to fast-track export-to-domestic transitions via its China Shun Ke Long supermarket chain and cross-border e-commerce platform, offering seven-day onboarding and dedicated retail displays.

CCOOP has also opened experience zones in upscale malls, using livestreams, samples, and interactive displays to boost brand visibility and engage local consumers.

The initiative is gaining momentum across retail. Major players—including Yonghui Superstores, JD.com, Meituan, Freshippo, China Resources, Dingdong, Be Friends, and Oriental Enterprise—have joined the export-to-domestic transition. Highlights include:

● Yonghui Superstores launched a 15-day fast-track listing program for exporters facing shipping delays, with added marketing and product development support.

● JD.com committed CNY200 billion (US$27.34 billion) to integrate export products into domestic retail channels.

● Oriental Enterprise received partnership requests from 22 suppliers—nine seeking in-house distribution, and 13 applying for third-party platform listings.

Execution is the real challenge: rethinking strategy beyond repackaging

Despite strong policy support, execution remains complex. Adapting export goods for local markets takes more than rebranding—it requires alignment with domestic consumer expectations, standards, and retail systems.

Zhongnong Fengmao Plant Protection Machinery Co. said that although paraquat and diquat remain exempt from US tariffs, it is shifting its focus to emerging markets in South America, Southeast Asia, and Africa amid rising geopolitical risks.

Anhui HuiLong and Guangdong Tianhe maintain long-standing ties with local supply cooperatives but have yet to launch new initiatives—highlighting the slow and complex nature of the transition.

Gansu Dunhuang Seed Group Co. clarified that while supply cooperatives are founding shareholders, its operations remain fully independent.

Ultimately, success in China's domestic shift depends not just on access, but on tailored pricing, branding, and distribution strategies that meet local consumer needs.

"China for China" in action: three supply chain shifts

DIGITIMES Vice President Eric Huang says the US is accelerating efforts to decouple from China—not only in semiconductors but across the broader electronics supply chain. From temporary tariff exemptions to renewed reshoring efforts, the direction is shifting toward localization.

Huang identifies three structural shifts now redefining global manufacturing:

Shifting out of China

Facing steep tariffs, manufacturers are moving operations to Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, India, and other lower-tariff regions. Most are using existing non-China facilities to mitigate disruption while avoiding the delay of setting up new plants.

Shifting into the US & Mexico

To retain USMCA tax advantages, firms are expanding production in North America—especially critical for tech and electronics exporters aiming to stay competitive in the US market.

Made in China, for China

The most pivotal shift is happening within China itself. Companies are prioritizing local production for domestic markets, cutting exposure to external risks and aligning with self-sufficiency goals. This includes sourcing local parts, using domestic supply chains, and tailoring products for Chinese consumers.

Huang notes that the "China for China" notion is becoming the backbone of China's industrial and consumer strategy.

Global markets redrawn: a dual-track world takes shape

Huang predicts the global market will split into three distinct ecosystems:

● US market: Centered on US-Mexico production, with a strong push to eliminate reliance on China.

● China market: Focused on domestic manufacturing and supply chains with minimal Western input.

● Rest of world: Encompasses Southeast Asia, the Middle East, Africa, and Europe, where strategies depend on local tariffs, geopolitical ties, and market stability.

In this new landscape, global supply chains will operate through three parallel systems—each shaped by its own regulatory, economic, and geopolitical realities.

China's answer to de-globalization

As globalization wanes and trade nationalism rises, Chinese firms are moving away from export reliance toward a model focused on efficiency, resilience, and local relevance.

The export-to-domestic shift and "China for China" strategy mark a fundamental realignment of China's manufacturing and consumption model—not just a temporary adjustment.

The resurgence of supply cooperatives, digital platform coordination, and policy support for domestic circulation all signal China's push toward long-term economic self-reliance.

In a bifurcated global landscape, companies that localize early, adapt quickly, and build resilient operations will be best positioned to lead.
