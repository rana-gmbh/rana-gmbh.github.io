China's Zuchongzhi 3.0 quantum computer surpasses Google's Willow in race for quantum supremacy

Some content could not be imported from the original document. View content ↗

As competition between the US and China escalates, quantum computing has emerged as a pivotal battleground in the quest for technological dominance. Scientists at the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC), led by Jian-wei Pan, Xiao-bo Zhu, and Cheng-zhi Peng, have introduced Zuchongzhi 3.0, a next-generation superconducting quantum computing prototype with 105 qubits—marking a major step in China's quantum research.

The achievement establishes a new global benchmark in superconducting quantum computing, reinforcing China's growing presence in the field.

Benchmark tests show Zuchongzhi 3.0 executes quantum random circuit sampling at speeds one quadrillion times (10¹⁵) faster than the world's most advanced supercomputers. Crucially, it outperforms Google's Willow, the tech giant's latest quantum processor unveiled in October 2024.

As quantum computing becomes a focal point in global tech competition, the US and China are ramping up efforts to secure a leading position.

China's quantum computing acceleration

In 2021, USTC researchers led by Pan developed Zuchongzhi 2.0, a 66-qubit superconducting quantum processor that performed quantum random circuit sampling 10 million times faster than the world's top supercomputers.

With Zuchongzhi 3.0, China is closing the gap with the US. The team's latest findings were published on March 3 in the peer-reviewed journal Physical Review Letters.

US tech giants double down on quantum R&D

US technology leaders Google, IBM, and Intel continue to invest heavily in quantum computing research, making steady advancements in processor performance and error correction.

China's Zuchongzhi 3.0 prototype integrates 105 readable qubits and 182 coupled qubits, positioning it among the world's most advanced quantum processors. In benchmark tests, it completed 83-qubit, 32-layer random circuit sampling, running calculations trillions to quadrillions of times faster (10¹²–10¹⁵) than the best supercomputers—again exceeding Google's latest quantum performance.

The three stages of quantum computing evolution

Experts break down quantum computing progress into three critical phases:

  1. Quantum Supremacy – proving quantum computers can outperform classical supercomputers in specific tasks.

  2. Quantum Simulation – scaling systems to hundreds of qubits to solve complex problems beyond classical computing capabilities.

  3. Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing – achieving high-precision qubit control and error correction for universal quantum computing applications.

Toward fault-tolerant quantum computing

Lead researcher Pan and his team are advancing in quantum error correction, quantum entanglement, quantum analog simulation, and quantum chemistry. The team has made early progress in surface code error correction with a code distance of 7—a key milestone toward scalable, fault-tolerant quantum computing.

As the US-China quantum rivalry escalates, China's rapid strides in the field could reshape the global technology landscape, accelerating the transition toward the next era of quantum computing.
