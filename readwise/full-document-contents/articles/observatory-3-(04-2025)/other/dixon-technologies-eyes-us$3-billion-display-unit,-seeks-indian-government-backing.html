Weekly news roundup: Dixon Technologies eyes US$3 billion display unit; Huawei Ascend 910C reportedly hits 40% yield

These are the most-read DIGITIMES Asia stories from February 24 – February 27.

Dixon Technologies, India's leading electronics manufacturing services (EMS) provider, is aiming to establish a US$3 billion display fabrication unit, contingent on government support through the India Semiconductor Mission 2.0 (ISM 2.0). The initiative seeks to reduce India's reliance on global display manufacturers like LG, Samsung, and BOE.

Additionally, Dixon is expanding its production capacity with a new INR10 billion (approx. US$120 million) manufacturing facility in Sriperumbudur, Tamil Nadu, which will produce laptops, servers, and SSDs. With 23 existing plants and over 22,000 employees, Dixon remains a key player in India's electronics manufacturing sector.

Huawei Ascend 910C reportedly hits 40% yield, turns profitable; aims for 60% industry standard

Huawei's Ascend 910C AI chip has reached a 40% manufacturing yield, making the Ascend production line profitable for the first time. The company aims to increase the yield to 60%, bringing it closer to industry standards. Huawei's advancements mark a significant step in China's push for semiconductor self-sufficiency, reducing its dependence on Western chipmakers like Nvidia and TSMC.

DRAM industry would have rebounded without China's output surge, says Nanya

The DRAM market is expected to remain at its lowest point in early 2025, with Chinese manufacturers' production capacity delaying recovery. Despite this, Nanya Technology plans to increase DRAM production, leveraging HBM and AI-powered devices while navigating market pressures from China and global trade tensions.

Trump administration pressures TSMC to partner with Intel in bid for stronger US ties

The Trump administration has urged TSMC to partner with Intel and accelerate its US investments, with reports suggesting TSMC may take a controlling stake in Intel's factories. However, experts warn this move could pose more risks than benefits. While TSMC continues its 2nm production plans for 2025, its expansion in the US remains under careful consideration, with Taiwanese authorities emphasizing regulatory oversight.

China's mature-node foundry price war eases as global clients shift orders

China's aggressive price war in mature-node wafer manufacturing is stabilizing, as global clients—especially from the US—diversify supply chains due to eroded trust in Chinese suppliers. While China's pricing war impact lingers, VIS sees signs of stabilization, with a gradual recovery in 2025 and long-term expansion through its Singapore joint venture.

SK Hynix reportedly hits 70% yield in HBM4 testing

SK Hynix has reached a major milestone in its sixth-generation high-bandwidth memory (HBM4) development, attaining a 70% yield rate in pre-production trials. SK Hynix's rapid progress in HBM4 yield optimization, strategic collaboration with TSMC, and potential alignment with Nvidia's AI accelerator timeline reinforce its dominance in high-performance memory solutions.

US tariffs on TSMC could force IC designers to shoulder higher production costs

US President Trump has announced a 25% tariff on semiconductors, automobiles, and pharmaceuticals starting in April 2025, with plans for gradual increases. This move could disrupt global supply chains, particularly affecting Taiwanese semiconductor giant TSMC and US fabless chip companies. While TSMC remains strategically resilient, US tech firms may face higher costs, and global market uncertainty is set to rise.
